# 2022 NYC Building Code Chapter 10: Means of Egress

## Overview

Chapter 10 of the 2022 NYC Building Code establishes the requirements for a complete and continuous system of egress from buildings and structures. The means of egress system is fundamental to building safety and fire protection, ensuring that occupants can exit safely during emergencies. This chapter addresses the design, construction, maintenance, and operational aspects of all components that comprise the means of egress, including exit access, exits, and exit discharge.

The 2022 Code is based on the International Building Code 2021 with New York City-specific amendments and modifications. Key updates emphasize resilience, accessibility, and enhanced safety for diverse occupants.

---

## Section 1001: Administration

### 1001.1 General

Every building and structure shall be provided with a complete means of egress system. The means of egress shall be designed and maintained to permit orderly egress of all occupants at all times the building is occupied. No person shall be permitted to occupy any building or structure or any portion thereof unless the building or structure or portion thereof is provided with a safe, continuous, and unobstructed means of egress.

### 1001.2 Minimum Requirements

It is unlawful to alter, construct, enlarge, or change the occupancy of any building or structure in a manner that would reduce the number of exits or the egress capacity below that required by this code. All exits, whether required or voluntary, shall be maintained and continuously available for egress. The reduction of required exits is prohibited under all circumstances. Existing buildings undergoing alterations must maintain or improve their egress capacity.

### 1001.3 Maintenance

The means of egress system shall be maintained in accordance with the NYC Fire Code and Chapter 1 (General) of the Building Code. This includes regular inspection, testing, and maintenance of all egress components to ensure they remain safe and functional at all times. Building owners and operators are responsible for ensuring compliance with maintenance requirements. Egress elements must be inspected at least annually for code compliance.

### 1001.4 Inadequate Exits for Existing Structures

Where the number or capacity of exits in an existing structure is found to be inadequate for the occupant load, the Commissioner may require the addition of exits or other modifications to the means of egress system to bring the building into compliance with current code requirements. This is particularly relevant in older NYC structures.

## Section 1002: Definitions

The following definitions are essential to understanding the means of egress requirements:

**Area of Refuge.** An area in a building that is separated from other areas of the building by a fire-resistance rating and in which persons who are unable to use stairs may remain temporarily in safety to await rescue or assistance during an emergency evacuation.

**Common Path of Egress Travel.** The portion of the egress path where occupants are required to travel together before accessing two or more separate and distinct exits or separated exit paths.

**Corridor.** An exit component that defines and provides a path of egress travel to an exit or exit discharge. May include corridors in office buildings, schools, hotels, and residential buildings.

**Exit.** That portion of a means of egress system between the exit access and the exit discharge or public way. Exits are typically exit stairways, exit passageways, horizontal exits, or exterior exit stairways.

**Exit Access.** That portion of a means of egress system that connects the various rooms, areas, and spaces of a building to an exit. Exit access includes floors, corridors, ramps, stairways, and other pathways that lead to an exit.

**Exit Discharge.** That portion of a means of egress system between the exit and the public way. Exit discharge includes the ground floor area through which the exit passes and the final passage to the public way.

**Exit Passageway.** A horizontal exit component that provides an unobstructed path of egress travel to the exit discharge or public way.

**Means of Egress.** A continuous and unobstructed path of vertical and horizontal egress travel that includes the exit access, the exit itself, and the exit discharge or public way. A means of egress consists of three distinct parts: exit access, exit, and exit discharge.

**Occupant Load.** The number of individuals for which the means of egress of a building or portion thereof is designed. Occupant load is determined based on the use and occupancy type and the floor area available.

## Section 1003: General Means of Egress Requirements

### 1003.1 Applicability

The general means of egress provisions of Section 1003 apply to all elements of the means of egress system including exit access, exits, and exit discharge.

### 1003.2 Ceiling Height

The minimum ceiling height in means of egress, including corridors, exit access, exits, and exit discharge, shall be not less than 7 feet 6 inches (90 inches) measured from the floor to the lowest point of the ceiling or any overhead obstruction. This height must be maintained continuously throughout the path of egress travel.

### 1003.3 Protruding Objects

Objects that project into the means of egress shall not reduce the required headroom below 80 inches. This includes light fixtures, air distribution devices, sprinkler heads, and other structural or mechanical elements. Protruding objects may be present if adequate clearance is maintained for safe passage. [VERIFY: Updated standards for modern HVAC and electrical systems in 2022 code]

### 1003.4 Floor Surface

The floor surface in the means of egress shall be slip-resistant and shall be maintained in a clean, dry, and orderly manner. Changes in elevation at floor level shall be minimized. Where changes in elevation exist, they shall be handled appropriately to prevent tripping hazards.

### 1003.5 Elevation Changes

Ramps shall be provided for changes in elevation of less than 12 inches where the means of egress is required to be accessible. Changes in elevation of 1/4 inch or greater and less than 1/2 inch shall be beveled at a slope not exceeding 1:2. All elevation changes greater than 1/2 inch must be handled by a ramp, curb, or stair. All elevation changes must be clearly marked and visible.

### 1003.6 Means of Egress Continuity

Once a means of egress is established, it shall not be reduced in width or capacity as it approaches the exit, exit discharge, or public way. The egress path shall maintain its required width and capacity throughout its entire length. No doors, construction, or encroachment shall be allowed that would reduce the established egress path.

## Section 1004: Occupant Load

### 1004.1 Design Occupant Load

The design occupant load shall be determined by dividing the floor area by the occupant load factor for the specific use, as shown in Table 1004.1, or by actual count, whichever is greater. The occupant load shall be used to determine the number of required exits and the required width of those exits. For flexible-use spaces, the occupant load must be calculated for all potential uses.

### 1004.1 Occupant Load Factors (Table 1004.1)

The maximum floor area per occupant for various occupancy classifications under the 2022 NYC Building Code is as follows:

**Assembly Occupancies (Group A):**
- Concentrated without fixed seating (nightclubs, dance halls): 7 net square feet per occupant
- Standing space (galleries, bars): 5 net square feet per occupant
- Unconcentrated (lobbies, waiting areas): 15 net square feet per occupant
- Fixed seating: Actual number of fixed seats
- Auditoriums, theaters with fixed seating: Actual number of fixed seats
- Classrooms: 20 net square feet per occupant

**Business Occupancies (Group B):**
- General offices: 100 gross square feet per occupant [VERIFY: 2022 may adjust based on workplace standards]

**Educational Occupancies (Group E):**
- Classrooms: 20 net square feet per occupant
- Daycare and nursery: 35 net square feet per occupant

**Factory and Industrial Occupancies (Group F):**
- General: 200 gross square feet per occupant
- Hazardous manufacturing: 100 gross square feet per occupant

**High-Hazard Occupancies (Group H):**
- Variable by hazard type: 75-200 gross square feet per occupant

**Institutional Occupancies (Group I):**
- Hospitals, nursing homes: 200 gross square feet per occupant
- Residential treatment facilities: 100 gross square feet per occupant

**Mercantile Occupancies (Group M):**
- Basement and ground floor: 30 gross square feet per occupant
- Upper floors: 60 gross square feet per occupant
- Shopping centers: 40 gross square feet per occupant

**Residential Occupancies (Group R):**
- Hotels, dormitories: 200 gross square feet per occupant
- Apartments and dwelling units: 200 gross square feet per occupant (or based on number of bedrooms)
- Care facilities: 120 gross square feet per occupant

**Storage Occupancies (Group S):**
- General storage: 300 gross square feet per occupant
- Hazardous storage: 150 gross square feet per occupant

### 1004.2 Areas without Fixed Seating

Where an assembly occupancy does not have fixed seating, the occupant load shall be determined based on the net floor area available and the occupant load factor for the specific use type (concentrated, standing, or unconcentrated). For multipurpose spaces that can be configured in multiple ways, the occupant load must accommodate the most restrictive configuration.

### 1004.8 Posting of Occupant Load

A sign indicating the maximum occupant load shall be posted in a conspicuous location at the main exit or main entrance of every assembly occupancy and all spaces with an occupant load of 50 or more persons. The posted occupant load shall not exceed the calculated occupant load based on the means of egress capacity. The sign must be maintained in a legible condition at all times.

## Section 1005: Egress Width and Capacity

### 1005.1 Minimum Required Egress Width

The required egress width shall be determined by multiplying the occupant load by a width factor and dividing by the number of exits. The egress width factor is based on the type of egress component and whether the building is protected by an approved automatic sprinkler system.

**Width Factors (§1005.1):**

**Without Sprinklers:**
- Stairways: 0.3 inches per occupant
- Other egress components (corridors, ramps, passageways, doors): 0.2 inches per occupant

**With Sprinklers (NFPA 13 or approved system):**
- Stairways: 0.2 inches per occupant
- Other egress components: 0.15 inches per occupant

**Minimum Clear Widths (§1005.1):**
- Stairways serving 50 or more occupants: 44 inches minimum clear width
- Stairways serving fewer than 50 occupants: 36 inches minimum clear width
- Corridors and exit passageways: 44 inches minimum clear width
- Exit doors: 32 inches minimum clear opening width (measured from face of door to opposite face at 90 degrees)
- Doors in occupancies with an occupant load of 50 or more: 36 inches minimum

The required egress width shall be maintained throughout the entire length of the egress path. Width is measured as the clear distance between walls or railings, excluding door frames, handrails, and other approved projections.

## Section 1006: Number of Exits and Exit Access Doorways (§1006)

### 1006.1 General

Every story, basement, and individual space of a building shall be provided with the number of exits required by Table 1006.2.1, based on occupant load and occupancy type. Where two or more exits are required, they shall be separated and arranged to provide access to separate paths of egress travel to the public way.

### 1006.2 Number of Exits Required (Table 1006.2.1)

**Assembly and Hazardous Occupancies (Groups A and H):**
- 1 to 49 occupants: 1 exit
- 50 or more occupants: 2 exits
- 500-1,000 occupants: 3 exits [VERIFY: Threshold in 2022]
- 1,001 or more occupants: 4 exits

**Business Occupancies (Group B):**
- 1 to 49 occupants: 1 exit
- 50 to 500 occupants: 2 exits
- 501 to 1,000 occupants: 3 exits
- 1,001 or more occupants: 4 exits

**Educational Occupancies (Group E):**
- 1 to 49 occupants: 1 exit
- 50 or more occupants: 2 exits

**Factory and Industrial Occupancies (Group F):**
- 1 to 49 occupants: 1 exit
- 50 to 500 occupants: 2 exits
- 501 to 1,000 occupants: 3 exits
- 1,001 or more occupants: 4 exits

**Mercantile Occupancies (Group M):**
- 1 to 49 occupants: 1 exit
- 50 to 500 occupants: 2 exits
- 501 to 1,000 occupants: 3 exits
- 1,001 or more occupants: 4 exits

**Residential Occupancies (Group R):**
- 1-2 dwelling units: 1 exit per unit
- 3+ dwelling units: 2 exits from building (with exceptions for units)
- Hotels with occupant load: As per Table 1006.2.1

### 1006.2.1 Separation of Two Exits

Where two exits are required, they shall be placed at a distance apart equal to not less than one-third of the length of the maximum overall diagonal dimension of the area being served. The exits shall be separated so that a single fire or emergency will not block both exits simultaneously.

In buildings equipped with an approved automatic sprinkler system, the required separation distance may be reduced to one-quarter of the length of the diagonal dimension of the area being served, provided that the exits are separated by fire-resistance-rated construction and smoke-proof design.

### 1006.2.2 Exit Access Doorways

Exit access doorways shall be of a minimum width determined by the egress requirements and shall swing in the direction of egress where required. Doors serving occupancies of 50 or more shall swing in the direction of egress travel.

## Section 1010: Doors, Gates, and Turnstiles

### 1010.1 General

All doors and gates serving as a required part of the means of egress shall comply with the requirements of Section 1010. Doors must swing in the direction of egress (outward) and be equipped with appropriate hardware to allow quick and easy opening without special knowledge.

### 1010.1.1 Door Size

Every exit door and exit access door shall have a clear opening width of not less than 32 inches and not more than 48 inches. The width shall be measured from the face of the door to the opposite face of the door when the door is in the open position at 90 degrees. Where required for occupant load of 50 or more, doors must be a minimum of 36 inches in clear opening width.

Doorways in corridor walls serving as exit access must be equipped with clear vision panels where required for life safety purposes.

### 1010.1.2 Direction of Door Swing

All doors serving as a required exit or exit access for an occupant load of 50 or more persons or for any building of Group H (hazardous) occupancy shall swing in the direction of egress (in the direction the occupants are traveling). Doors within a space and not serving as an exit or exit access shall be permitted to swing in any direction. Exterior doors shall swing out of the building.

### 1010.1.3 Door Opening Force

The maximum force required to open any door in the means of egress shall not exceed:
- 5 pounds for interior doors and gates (measured at the latch edge, perpendicular to the door face)
- 15 pounds for exterior doors and gates

Opening force shall be measured at a point 3 inches from the latch edge of the door and perpendicular to the door face. Doors equipped with closers and springs shall be adjusted to provide compliance with these requirements. [VERIFY: 2022 standards for automatic door operators in high-traffic areas]

### 1010.1.8 Locks and Latches

All locks and latches on doors serving as a required means of egress shall be operable from the egress side without special knowledge or effort. Keyed locks are prohibited on exit doors from occupied spaces during times of occupancy. Doors shall not be locked in the closed position during business hours or when the building is occupied. Emergency exit hardware (panic bars or crash bars) shall be provided where required by code. Card readers and electronic locks must allow manual override for egress.

### 1010.2 Turnstiles and Gates

Turnstiles and manual revolving doors are prohibited as part of the required egress. Automatic revolving doors must have a collapsible gate or an adjacent swing door for emergency egress.

## Section 1011: Stairways and Handrails

### 1011.1 Stairway Width

The width of stairways shall be determined in accordance with Section 1005 (Egress Width). Stairways serving 50 or more occupants shall have a minimum clear width of 44 inches between the finish of the walls or railings. Stairways serving fewer than 50 occupants shall have a minimum clear width of 36 inches. The width shall be measured horizontally between the finished surfaces of the walls or railings, excluding handrails and other projections of not more than 4 inches.

### 1011.2 Stairway Landings

Each stairway shall be provided with landings at the top and bottom and at changes in direction. The width of each landing shall be not less than the width of the stairway. The length of each landing in the direction of travel shall be not less than 36 inches measured in the plane of the nosing of the treads. Landings shall be level and shall be sized to provide adequate space for safe passage. All landings must be slip-resistant.

### 1011.3 Stair Treads and Risers (§1011.3)

All steps in stairways shall have a uniform tread depth and riser height. The minimum tread depth shall be 11 inches measured horizontally from the nosing of one step to the nosing of the next step. The maximum riser height shall be 7 inches measured vertically from the top of one tread to the top of the next tread.

For existing buildings in New York City, the maximum riser height is permitted to be 7 3/4 inches to accommodate existing conditions.

Stair treads and risers must be constructed with material that resists slipping and have good slip-resistant properties. The nosing of each step shall be rounded or beveled with a radius not greater than 1/2 inch.

### 1011.4 Vertical Rise

The vertical distance between landings, floors, or the walking surface shall not be more than 12 feet. A landing shall be provided at each change in direction of travel greater than 30 degrees.

### 1011.5 Stairway Construction

All exit stairways and stairways in exit passageways shall be constructed of noncombustible materials. The stringers, treads, risers, and landings shall be constructed of concrete, steel, or other noncombustible materials approved by the code. This is a specific NYC amendment that enhances fire safety in exit stairways. Metal grating may not be used for stairs in required exit enclosures.

### 1011.6 Handrails (§1011.6)

All stairways shall be provided with handrails on one or both sides. Handrails shall be installed along each stairway and shall be continuous from the beginning of the run to the end of the run. Handrails shall:
- Be 34 to 38 inches above the stair nosing
- Have a graspable cross-section with perimeter not less than 4 inches and not more than 6 1/4 inches
- Extend beyond the top and bottom of the stairway a distance equal to the tread depth (minimum 11 inches)
- Be structurally sound and able to resist a concentrated load of 200 pounds

Handrails must be continuous and smooth, with no sharp edges. Extensions at the bottom and top must be either returned to the wall or terminate vertically.

### 1011.7 Enclosures Under Stairways

The space under interior exit stairways shall not be used for any purpose, including storage, mechanical equipment, or hazardous materials. This area shall be left open or enclosed with fire-rated construction to prevent accumulation of combustible materials that could create a fire hazard. In required exit stairways, no part of the space may be utilized for any occupancy or storage purpose.

### 1011.8 Roof Access

In buildings four or more stories in height, stairways shall extend to the roof or to a roof access area. A stairway or ladder shall be provided to allow access to the roof of buildings four or more stories in height to facilitate building evacuation and emergency operations. In NYC, this is critical for emergency responder access.

**Maximum Travel Distance to Exits (in feet):**

**Assembly Occupancies (Group A):**
- With sprinklers: 250 feet
- Without sprinklers: 200 feet
- Covered mall buildings: 300 feet with sprinklers

**Business Occupancies (Group B):**
- With sprinklers: 300 feet
- Without sprinklers: 200 feet

**Educational Occupancies (Group E):**
- With sprinklers: 250 feet
- Without sprinklers: 150 feet

**Factory/Industrial Occupancies (Group F-1, F-2):**
- With sprinklers: 400 feet
- Without sprinklers: 250 feet

**Hazardous Occupancies (Group H):**
- With sprinklers: 100 feet
- Without sprinklers: 75 feet

**Institutional Occupancies (Group I):**
- With sprinklers: 250 feet
- Without sprinklers: 200 feet

**Mercantile Occupancies (Group M):**
- With sprinklers: 300 feet
- Without sprinklers: 200 feet
- Shopping malls: 300 feet with sprinklers

**Residential Occupancies (Group R):**
- With sprinklers: 250 feet
- Without sprinklers: 200 feet

**Storage Occupancies (Group S-1, S-2):**
- With sprinklers: 400 feet
- Without sprinklers: 250 feet

Exit access travel distance is measured along the centerline of the path of egress travel from the most remote point in the occupied space to the nearest exit. This distance includes common paths of egress travel and helps ensure that occupants can reach exits within a reasonable time during emergency evacuation.

## Section 1020: Corridors

### 1020.1 Construction and Protection

Corridors serving as exit access from occupiable spaces shall be separated from other spaces by fire-resistance-rated construction. The fire-resistance rating requirements depend on the occupancy type and whether the building is sprinklered:

**Fire Separation Requirements:**
- 1-hour fire-resistance rating: In most occupancies for sprinklered buildings
- 2-hour fire-resistance rating: In buildings not equipped with sprinklers or in high-hazard occupancies
- [VERIFY: 2022 NYC amendments may adjust based on occupancy classification]

Corridors shall be constructed of materials that resist the passage of fire and smoke to protect occupants as they travel to exits.

### 1020.2 Width

The minimum width of corridors shall be not less than 44 inches for corridors serving 50 or more occupants. Corridors serving fewer than 50 occupants may have a minimum width of 36 inches. The corridor width shall be maintained continuously along the entire length, and no projections, furniture, or equipment shall reduce the clear width below the required minimum. Width is measured between finished wall surfaces, excluding handrails and other permitted projections.

### 1020.3 Dead-End Corridors (§1020.3)

Dead-end corridors are corridors that do not permit travel in two directions from any point in the corridor. The maximum length of a dead-end corridor shall not exceed:
- 50 feet in buildings equipped with an approved automatic sprinkler system
- 20 feet in buildings not equipped with sprinklers

Dead-end corridors should be minimized in building design where possible. All dead-end corridors must have emergency lighting and clear signage.

---

## Section 1020.4: Common Path of Egress Travel

The common path of egress travel is the distance that occupants are required to travel together before accessing two or more separate and distinct exits. The maximum length of the common path of egress travel shall not exceed:
- 75 feet in buildings equipped with an approved automatic sprinkler system
- 50 feet in buildings not equipped with sprinklers
- 100 feet in shopping centers and covered malls with approved sprinkler systems

The common path shall be measured along the centerline of the egress route from the most remote occupiable space to the point where the path splits into at least two separate and distinct paths to different exits. This requirement is critical for high-occupancy spaces.

## Section 1021: Horizontal Exits

### 1021.1 Horizontal Exits

A horizontal exit is an exit through a fire wall with a 2-hour fire-resistance rating to an area of refuge in an adjacent building or section of the same building. The area of refuge must be capable of accommodating all occupants evacuating from the area being served without exceeding the occupant load of that area.

A horizontal exit through a fire wall allows occupants to move laterally to a protected area rather than traveling downward through exit stairways, which is particularly beneficial in tall buildings where stairway capacity may be limited.

**Limitations:**
- No more than 50 percent of the required exits for any story shall be horizontal exits
- At least one accessible stairway or other accessible exit must be provided from each area to reach the ultimate exit discharge to the public way
- The area of refuge must be a compartmented area with 2-hour fire separation

## Section 1022: Exit Enclosures

### 1022.1 Enclosures Required

Exit stairways, including interior exit stairways and exit ramps serving occupancies above or below the grade level, shall be fully enclosed. The enclosure shall extend from the floor of the story in which the stairway or ramp is located through the floor above it to the roof or to a point where it serves only a single story.

### 1022.1.1 Fire-Resistance Rating

Exit stairways and ramps shall be enclosed with construction having a fire-resistance rating of not less than:
- 2 hours for stairways connecting 4 or more stories
- 1 hour for stairways connecting fewer than 4 stories
- For exit passageways serving as required exits: 1 hour (or 2 hours if connecting 4+ stories)

The walls, doors, and other components of the exit enclosure shall be constructed to maintain the fire-resistance rating and prevent passage of smoke and flame. This must be verified in construction documents and during DOB inspection.

### 1022.1.2 Shaft Enclosure

Exit stairwells must be fully enclosed within the building and rated as a vertical exit enclosure. The enclosure must extend through all floors served by the stairway.

### 1022.3 Penetrations

Penetrations through the walls, floors, or ceilings of exit enclosures shall be limited. Pipes, ducts, conduits, and cables may penetrate the enclosure only if the penetrations are protected by approved fire-stop systems that maintain the fire-resistance rating of the enclosure.

### 1022.4 Openings

Openings into exit enclosures from occupied spaces shall be protected by approved fire door assemblies. Every opening shall be equipped with a fire door rated for not less than the fire-resistance rating of the enclosure. Doors shall be self-closing or automatic closing and shall be maintained in working order. Fire door hardware must allow free egress from the stairwell.

---

## Section 1023: Exit Passageways

### 1023.1 General

Exit passageways are horizontal exits providing a path of travel from an occupancy to an exit discharge or public way. They are required to be:
- Separated from other areas by fire-resistance-rated construction (1 or 2 hours depending on building height and sprinkler protection)
- Minimum 44 inches wide for occupant load of 50+ (36 inches for fewer occupants)
- Properly illuminated with exit signage
- Without dead-ends

Exit passageways are common in large-scale buildings where the exit stairwell cannot be located directly adjacent to occupied spaces.

## Section 1024: Exit Discharge

### 1024.1 General Requirements

All exits shall discharge directly to the public way or through an approved exit court or passageway. The public way is generally defined as a street, alley, or other open space that has a direct unobstructed access to a public way and provides safe, unobstructed passage to the street level. Exit discharge shall not be through an area that can be closed off or locked during occupancy.

Where an approved exit court or exit passageway is provided, exits may discharge through these areas to reach the public way, provided they meet all code requirements.

### 1024.2 Exit Discharge Through Interior Areas

Where the exit discharge is through a portion of the ground story or basement of a building, no more than 50 percent of the required exits shall discharge through an interior area. These interior areas must be separated by fire-resistance-rated construction and shall have at least one exit opening directly to the public way or to an approved exit court or passageway.

In buildings equipped with an approved automatic sprinkler system, the interior area through which the exit discharges may be constructed with 1-hour fire-resistance-rated construction rather than 2-hour construction. [VERIFY: 2022 sprinkler credit provisions]

### 1024.3 Outdoor Areas

When exit discharge occurs in an outdoor area (courtyard, plaza), the area must:
- Have a direct connection to the public way
- Be free of obstructions (parking, stored materials)
- Have clear egress signage
- Provide safe ground surface

## Section 1025: Means of Egress Illumination and Signage

### 1025.1 Illumination Required

All means of egress, including exit access, exits, exit discharge, public ways, and areas of refuge, shall be illuminated at all times the building is occupied. The illumination level shall not be less than 1 foot-candle (measured at the floor level) in all areas where occupants are located or pass through during egress.

### 1025.2 Levels of Illumination

The required illumination levels are as follows:
- Minimum 1 foot-candle at floor level in all means of egress (measured in the horizontal plane at the floor)
- Exit signs and exit accents shall be illuminated with at least 5 foot-candles
- Emergency lighting shall provide illumination for a minimum of 90 minutes in the event of power failure
- Illumination uniformity should not exceed a ratio of 40:1 (maximum to minimum)

### 1025.3 Emergency Power for Exit Signs and Means of Egress Illumination

Exit signs and emergency lighting in the means of egress shall be powered by an emergency electrical power system. In buildings with an approved emergency voice/alarm communication system, the emergency power supply shall have a capacity to supply emergency lighting for a minimum of 90 minutes at full illumination level. The emergency power system shall automatically activate upon failure of the normal electrical supply.

### 1025.4 Exit Signs (§1010.1)

All exits and exit access doorways shall be marked with approved exit signs. Exit signs shall:
- Be readily visible and legible from any direction of approach
- Display the word "EXIT" in letters not less than 6 inches high (for spaces with occupant load of 100+)
- Be illuminated by a reliable light source
- Have letters and arrows in a contrasting color (typically white letters on red background, or red letters on white background)
- Include accessible exit signage with appropriate symbols for accessible routes

Tactile exit signs are required for occupancies with accessibility requirements.

## Section 1028: Assembly Occupancies (§1028)

Assembly occupancies have special requirements for means of egress to accommodate large concentrations of occupants and ensure orderly evacuation.

### 1028.1 Main Exit Width

The width of the main exit serving an assembly occupancy shall be adequate for the occupant load. The main exit may include a combination of doorways, passages, and stairways, and its total width shall be calculated according to the occupant load and the required egress width factor.

### 1028.2 Aisle Requirements

In areas with fixed seating, aisles shall be provided to allow access to each seat. Aisle width shall be:
- Not less than 11 inches for common aisles in theaters and auditoriums
- Not less than 12 inches for aisle stairs
- Not less than 20 inches for cross aisles

Aisles shall have a slope not exceeding 1 unit vertical in 8 units horizontal. Dead-end aisles are permitted if they lead to at least 2 separate exits.

### 1028.3 Smoke-Protected Assembly Seating

In assembly occupancies with smoke-protected seating arrangements approved by the code official, certain concessions on exit distances and number of exits may be permitted. Smoke protection systems must be designed to protect occupants and allow safe evacuation in the event of a fire. [VERIFY: 2022 NYC amendments to smoke-protected assembly requirements]

## Section 1029: Emergency Escape and Rescue Openings

### 1029.1 Required

Emergency escape and rescue openings shall be provided in sleeping rooms below the fourth story in residential occupancies. Each sleeping room must provide either:
- An emergency escape and rescue opening, or
- Doors and windows opening directly to the exterior with a minimum clear opening dimension

This requirement ensures occupant safety during residential fires.

### 1029.2 Minimum Opening Size

Each emergency escape and rescue opening shall have:
- Minimum clear opening area of 5.7 square feet
- Minimum width of 20 inches measured horizontally
- Minimum height of 24 inches measured vertically from the interior of the room at the bottom of the opening

For basements and cellars:
- Opening area: not less than 5 square feet
- Minimum width of 20 inches
- Minimum height of 24 inches at the bottom of the window opening

### 1029.3 Sill Height

The sill height of the emergency escape and rescue opening shall not be more than 44 inches above the floor of the sleeping room. This requirement ensures that occupants can reach and use the opening for emergency evacuation. Sill height is measured from the floor level to the interior bottom edge of the opening.

### 1029.4 Bars and Grilles

Bars, grilles, or security devices shall not prevent the use of emergency escape and rescue openings. If present, they must be capable of being opened or removed from the inside without special tools or knowledge. [VERIFY: NYC residential fire escape and window guard requirements]

## Section 1030: Accessible Means of Egress

### 1030.1 General

At least one accessible means of egress shall be provided from each story, basement, and individual space. An accessible means of egress must meet all applicable accessibility requirements (per Chapter 2, Section 2.1 and incorporated accessibility standards) and consist of accessible exit access, accessible exit, and accessible exit discharge.

### 1030.2 Accessible Routes

Accessible means of egress routes must be free of obstacles, have appropriate slope (no steeper than 1:20), and be at least 36 inches wide (48 inches preferred for two-way traffic). [VERIFY: 2022 accessibility standards]

### 1030.3 Stairways

In buildings four stories or more in height and equipped with an approved automatic sprinkler system, at least one accessible stairway shall be provided. This accessible stairway shall connect all floors and shall meet all requirements for accessible stairways, including:
- Handrails complying with Section 1011.6
- Uniform tread depth (11 inches minimum) and riser height (7 inches maximum)
- Landing dimensions of at least 36 inches in the direction of travel
- Slip-resistant surfaces on all treads

### 1030.4 Elevators for Emergency Use

In buildings four or more stories in height, at least one accessible passenger elevator shall be provided for emergency use. This elevator shall be designed to serve all floors of the building and remain operational during emergency evacuation to facilitate evacuation of persons with disabilities, persons with mobility limitations, and emergency responders. [VERIFY: 2022 provisions for elevator operation during fire conditions]

### 1030.5 Areas of Refuge

Areas of refuge shall be provided in buildings with occupant loads requiring accessible means of egress. An area of refuge shall:
- Have direct access to a stairway or elevator serving as a required exit
- Be sized to accommodate all persons using accessible means of egress
- Communicate with the stairway or elevator via two-way communication system or visual warning systems
- Be separated from other building areas by 2-hour fire-resistance-rated construction

## Section 1031: Occupant Load Signage (§1004.8)

Occupant load signs must be posted in the following locations:
- At the main exit or entrance of assembly occupancies
- In all spaces with an occupant load of 50 or more
- In areas designed for multiple uses with varying occupant loads

The sign shall state the occupant load in a format specified by the code official. It must be maintained in a legible condition at all times.

## Travel Distance Calculation Examples for Expediter Review

### Example 1: Office Building with Sprinklers (Group B)

- Floor area: 20,000 sq ft
- Occupancy: Business offices
- Occupant load: 20,000 / 100 = 200 occupants
- Building has sprinkler system: Yes
- Maximum travel distance allowed: 300 feet

Action: Verify that every occupied space can reach an exit within 300 feet of travel distance. Measure actual walking distances along corridors.

### Example 2: Restaurant without Sprinklers (Group A-2)

- Floor area: 5,000 sq ft
- Occupancy: Unconcentrated assembly (restaurant)
- Occupant load: 5,000 / 15 = 333 occupants
- Building has sprinkler system: No
- Maximum travel distance allowed: 200 feet
- Required number of exits: 2 exits (for 333 occupants)

Action: Calculate egress width needed: 333 occupants × 0.2 (no sprinklers, not stairs) / 2 exits = 33.3 inches per exit (44-inch minimum applies, so 44 inches required for each exit door/stairway).

### Example 3: Narrow Office Floor Plate

- Floor area: 8,000 sq ft
- Rectangular floor: 80 ft × 100 ft
- Diagonal: ~128 feet
- Occupancy: Business offices, sprinkled
- Occupant load: 8,000 / 100 = 80 occupants (requires 2 exits)
- Exit separation required: 128 ft / 3 = 42.7 feet minimum apart
- With sprinkler system: Can reduce to 128 ft / 4 = 32 feet

Action: Ensure two exits are separated by at least 42.7 feet (or 32 feet if fully sprinklered with proper separation).

## Key NYC Amendments to IBC 2021 (2022 NYC Code)

The 2022 NYC Building Code incorporates the International Building Code 2021 with significant New York City amendments and modifications, including:

1. **Stairway Construction:** Noncombustible materials required for all exit stairways (metal grating prohibited in required exits)
2. **Vertical Rise:** No more than 12 feet between landings
3. **High-Rise Building Requirements:** Enhanced requirements for buildings over 80 feet in NYC
4. **Egress from Occupied Spaces:** Stricter requirements for continuous egress paths
5. **Exit Signage:** Enhanced visibility and durability requirements for NYC
6. **Emergency Lighting:** 90-minute backup power required (vs. historical 30 minutes in some contexts)
7. **Accessible Egress:** Full integration of accessibility throughout Chapter 10 per ADA and NYC standards
8. **Sprinkler Benefits:** Specific NYC provisions for reduced egress widths and travel distances in fully sprinklered buildings
9. **Assembly Occupancies:** More detailed provisions for theaters, nightclubs, and public gathering spaces
10. **Residential Occupancies:** Enhanced requirements for emergency escape windows in dwelling units

## Common NYC Deficiency Areas

Based on typical DOB review comments:

1. **Inadequate Exit Separation:** Exits too close together on the same side of the building
2. **Insufficient Egress Width:** Doors or corridors too narrow for calculated occupant load
3. **Travel Distance Violations:** Occupied spaces beyond maximum travel distance to exits
4. **Door Swing Direction:** Doors not swinging in direction of egress (especially on ground floor)
5. **Dead-End Corridors:** Exceeding permitted length limits
6. **Exit Signage:** Missing or improperly sized/located exit signs
7. **Stairway Width:** Inadequate clear width between walls/railings
8. **Enclosure Fire Rating:** Incorrect fire rating for exit stairwell or failure to fully enclose
9. **Handrail Extensions:** Not extending properly beyond top/bottom of stairs
10. **Common Path Issues:** Path to two exits is too long before splitting

## Important Reminders for Design Professionals and Expediters

1. **Verify with Code Official:** Always confirm interpretation of means of egress requirements with the NYC Department of Buildings code official during design phase. NYC DOB has specific requirements that may not be explicit in code text.

2. **Sprinkler System Impact:** Determine sprinkler system status early—it affects occupant load signage, egress widths, travel distances, dead-end corridors, exit enclosure ratings, and multiple other requirements.

3. **Measure Travel Distances Carefully:** Travel distance is measured along the actual path of egress, not diagonally. Include turns, door width transitions, and ramp slopes in measurements.

4. **Exit Separation is Critical:** In NYC, separated exits are essential for code compliance and are frequently cited deficiencies. One-third (or one-quarter with sprinklers) of the diagonal is the minimum.

5. **Future Flexibility:** Consider adaptability of spaces to changes in occupancy or use that might affect egress requirements. A future change in occupancy could dramatically increase required exits or egress width.

6. **Accessibility Integration:** Ensure that accessible means of egress is integrated into the overall egress design, not treated as an afterthought. At least one accessible route through the building is required.

7. **Documentation:** Maintain detailed documentation of:
   - Occupant load calculations with justification
   - Egress width calculations showing basis
   - Exit arrangement diagrams with separation distances
   - Travel distance measurements along actual path
   - Exit enclosure fire rating specifications

8. **Signage and Markings:** Ensure clear, durable, and appropriately located exit signage and floor markings. DOB requires specific sizing, color contrast, and illumination levels.

9. **Historic Buildings:** NYC has specific amendments for historic structures. Means of egress improvements in historic buildings may be designed differently to preserve character. [VERIFY: Specific NYC historic preservation provisions in 2022 code]

10. **Large Buildings:** For buildings over 80 feet or with complex floor plates, consider hiring a vertical transportation consultant to optimize exit placement and stairway sizing.

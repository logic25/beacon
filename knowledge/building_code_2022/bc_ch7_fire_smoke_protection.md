# 2022 NYC Building Code Chapter 7: Fire and Smoke Protection Features

## Overview: Changes from 2014 to 2022

The 2022 NYC Building Code Chapter 7 incorporates significant updates to fire and smoke protection requirements:

- **Updated ASTM and UL Standards:** References to ASTM E119 and UL 263 include 2022 revisions; compliance with updated testing protocols required
- **Enhanced Fire-Resistance Rating Coordination:** Better alignment with Table 601 and occupancy-specific requirements
- **Revised Penetration and Joint Requirements:** Stricter protocols for firestop system installation and verification
- **Updated Opening Protective Standards:** Harmonized with revised UL 10B and UL 10C standards for fire doors/windows
- **Smoke Control System Integration:** Enhanced requirements for smoke barriers in high-rise and healthcare occupancies
- **High-Rise Amendments:** Expanded elevator lobby and stairwell pressurization requirements for buildings over 75 feet

## Section 701: General

Fire-resistance-rated construction remains the foundational approach for safeguarding against the spread of fire and smoke throughout a structure. Section 701 establishes that all buildings and structures must be constructed with fire-resistance-rated assemblies designed to resist the spread of fire between buildings on the same lot or between occupancies within the same building.

The core intent is building compartmentalization: a fire originating in one area must be contained and prevented from spreading to other areas through structural elements. This compartmentalization is achieved through:

- Fire-resistant materials and non-combustible assemblies
- Protective measures that slow fire propagation
- Systems that provide additional time for occupant evacuation
- Coordination with fire suppression systems and firefighting operations

All fire-resistance-rated construction must comply with definitions, classifications, and test methods in Chapter 7. Different construction types (Types I-V) have varying requirements for fire-resistance ratings of structural elements and separations, with stricter requirements applied to less fire-resistant construction types.

### Key 2022 Updates:
- Enhanced coordination between Chapter 7 and Chapter 6 (Type of Construction) fire-resistance rating tables
- Explicit requirement that all test assemblies comply with ASTM E119-21 or UL 263-22 standards
- [VERIFY] Specific references to updated calculated fire-resistance rating methods per ASTM E2218

## Section 702: Definitions

Key terms used throughout Chapter 7 ensure consistent application of fire protection requirements across the jurisdiction:

**Fire Barrier (§702.1).** A vertical or horizontal assembly of non-combustible materials that has a specified fire-resistance rating and includes continuity of materials, joint treatment, and protection of penetrations to resist the passage of flame and hot gases. Fire barriers extend through all intervening floors and are self-supporting at openings.

**Fire Partition (§702.1).** A vertical assembly of materials that subdivides a story and has a specified fire-resistance rating, but is not required to extend through the roof or be free-standing. Fire partitions are less restrictive than fire barriers and have higher allowed opening percentages.

**Fire Wall (§702.1).** A fire-resistance-rated wall that extends from the foundation through the roof and creates separate buildings or occupancies on the same lot. The wall must maintain structural stability even if structures on adjacent sides are damaged or collapse. Fire walls must be structurally independent.

**Fire-Resistance Rating (§702.1).** The time period, in hours or minutes, during which a building assembly maintains the ability to confine a fire and, where applicable, continues to perform its structural function. Ratings are determined by ASTM E119-21 or UL 263-22 testing.

**Opening Protective (§702.1).** A fire door assembly, fire window assembly, fire damper assembly, or smoke damper assembly that provides protection of openings in fire-resistance-rated walls or assemblies. Must be listed and labeled for the specific assembly and rating.

**Shaft Enclosure (§702.1).** An assembly that creates a protected pathway through one or more stories of a building, used for vertical conveyance of people or things, such as elevators, stairs, mechanical systems, electrical chases, or plumbing runs. Must maintain integrity throughout full height.

**Smoke Barrier (§702.1).** A continuous vertical or horizontal assembly of materials used to subdivide a story and restrict the movement of smoke, with a specified fire-resistance rating of 1 hour minimum. Includes protective measures for all openings and penetrations.

**Smoke Partition (§702.1).** A continuous vertical or horizontal assembly of materials that restricts the movement of smoke but may not have a fire-resistance rating. Used where smoke control is necessary but the stringent requirements of smoke barriers are not mandated.

**Membrane Penetration (§702.1).** An opening created by a duct, pipe, conduit, wire, or similar item passing through the protective membrane layer (drywall, plaster) only, not through the structural components of a fire-resistance-rated assembly.

**Through-Penetration (§702.1).** A penetration through all protective membranes and structural components of a fire-resistance-rated assembly created by pipes, ducts, conduits, or similar items. Requires comprehensive firestop protection.

**Firestop System (§702.1).** An approved system that protects penetrations and joints in fire-resistance-rated assemblies. Must be listed for the specific assembly type, penetration size, material, and fire-resistance rating. Installation must precisely follow manufacturer's instructions and testing standards.

## Section 703: Fire-Resistance Ratings and Fire Tests

### 703.1 General

Fire-resistance ratings for building assemblies must be determined by one of the following methods:

- **ASTM E119-21 Fire Test:** Standard test method for fire test of building construction and materials (mandatory current version)
- **UL 263-22 Fire Test:** Standard for safety fire test of building construction and materials (mandatory current version)
- **Calculated Methods:** Engineering analysis using approved procedures per ASTM E2218 or equivalent, requiring engineer stamp and DOB approval
- **Alternative Approved Methods:** Only when explicitly approved by the NYC Department of Buildings

Assemblies tested in accordance with current ASTM E119 or UL 263 standards and listed in fire-resistance rating tables are considered adequate. All calculated methods require DOB approval before use in plans.

### 703.2 ASTM E119-21 and UL 263-22 Test Methods

Fire-resistance ratings must be determined in accordance with ASTM E119-21 or UL 263-22, which describe essentially equivalent test procedures with the following evaluation criteria:

- **Integrity:** Maintained without openings developing, including cracks that permit flame or hot gases to pass through
- **Insulation:** Temperature rise on the unexposed surface does not exceed 250°F (139°C) average or 325°F (163°C) at any point
- **Load-Bearing Capability:** Where applicable, structural members continue to support applied loads and resist deformation

Test assemblies must be installed in a manner representative of actual construction practice. The results provide the basis for fire-resistance rating tables used throughout the building code. Deviations from tested conditions require engineer analysis and DOB approval.

### 703.2.1 Asymmetrical and Directional Ratings

Where a fire-resistance-rated assembly is tested with fire exposure to one side only, the rating applies only to that fire exposure direction unless:

- The assembly is symmetrical
- Testing data demonstrates equivalency for exposure from both sides
- The fire-resistance rating is explicitly approved for exposure from both sides
- Engineer certification and DOB approval are obtained for alternative directionality

Examples: curtain wall systems, sandwich panels with different face materials, proprietary assemblies

### 703.3 Calculated Fire-Resistance Ratings

Calculated methods using established procedures may be used when approved by the NYC Department of Buildings. These methods include:

- **Component Additive Method:** Based on heat transmission properties and material characteristics
- **Analytical Procedures:** Engineering fire models
- **Fire Modeling:** Computer-based heat transfer analysis (VERIFY: specific approval procedures)
- **Proprietary Calculation Methods:** Approved by testing labs and DOB

All calculated methods must:

- Be based on published ASTM standards (ASTM E2218 preferred)
- Provide a level of safety equivalent to ASTM E119-21 or UL 263-22 testing
- Include engineer analysis and professional stamp
- Be submitted to DOB for review and approval before use in construction documents
- Include detailed documentation of all assumptions and input parameters

## Section 704: Exterior Walls

### 704.1 General

The fire-resistance rating required for exterior walls depends upon fire separation distance (FSD): the shortest distance measured from the building face to an imaginary line on an adjacent property or centerline of an adjacent street or alley.

Buildings are subject to different exterior wall fire-resistance rating requirements based on proximity to property lines and combustible structures. As FSD increases, required fire-resistance ratings decrease. All measurements must be shown on site plans submitted to DOB.

### 704.1.1 Fire Separation Distance and Exterior Wall Rating Requirements

Fire-resistance rating requirements vary by occupancy group (Table 704.1.1) and construction type. 2022 updates provide clarification on measurements and include updated occupancy classifications:

**0 to 3 feet (including adjacent property line):**
- Fire-resistance rating of 1-2 hours required
- High-rise buildings (75+ feet) and Groups H, R: typically 2-hour rating
- Additional restrictions on unprotected openings

**3 to 5 feet:**
- Fire-resistance rating of 1-2 hours required depending on occupancy
- Groups R, I, and H: 2-hour rating standard
- Groups A, B, M, S, U: 1-hour or 2-hour per table

**5 to 10 feet:**
- Fire-resistance rating of 1 hour required for most occupancies
- Groups R, I, H: may require higher rating per Table 704.1.1
- Group S (storage): height and hazard-dependent

**10 to 30 feet:**
- Fire-resistance rating of 0 or 1 hour required, depending on occupancy classification and construction type
- Less hazardous occupancies may require non-rated (0-hour) construction
- Specific restrictions apply to Groups H and I (healthcare)

**30 feet and beyond:**
- No fire-resistance rating required for exterior walls (0-hour)
- Unprotected openings permitted per code
- Height and occupancy restrictions still apply

**Table 704.1.1 Reference:** Consult current Table 704.1.1 (2022 NYC BC) which lists requirements by occupancy group, construction type, and FSD. This table is critical for plan submissions and must be cited in design narratives.

### 704.8 Openings in Exterior Walls

Openings in fire-resistance-rated exterior walls must be protected to prevent the spread of fire from one building to another or from one story to another through the exterior wall. All window and door openings require protection based on FSD and assembly rating.

### 704.8.1 Maximum Percentage of Openings

The percentage of window and door openings in fire-resistance-rated exterior walls is limited based on FSD. As FSD decreases, allowable percentage of openings also decreases:

**0-5 feet FSD:**
- Opening percentage typically limited to 10-15% of wall area
- Fire door and window assemblies required

**5-10 feet FSD:**
- Opening percentage typically limited to 25% of wall area
- All openings require protective devices

**10-30 feet FSD:**
- Opening percentage may be higher (30-50%) depending on occupancy
- Protective devices still required per assembly rating

**30+ feet FSD:**
- Opening percentage typically unrestricted
- No protective devices required based on FSD (though building type may require them)

All openings in fire-resistance-rated exterior walls must be protected by approved fire door assemblies (§714.2) or fire window assemblies (§714.3) with ratings matching the wall assembly.

### 704.8.2 2022 Updates on Exterior Wall Openings

- Enhanced clarity on measuring wall area for percentage calculations (includes entire wall face between floor slabs)
- Stricter requirements for grouped openings (openings less than 4 feet apart)
- New requirements for HVAC intake/exhaust louver protection [VERIFY specific ratings]
- Clarification on spandrel panel requirements in relation to opening percentages

## Section 705: Fire Walls

Fire walls (§705) represent the most stringent type of fire separation within the building code and are the only assembly type that creates legally separate buildings when on the same lot. A fire wall is a fire-resistance-rated wall extending from foundation through roof that must maintain structural stability even if structures on one or both sides are damaged or collapse.

### 705.1 General Requirements

Fire walls must:

- Extend from the foundation or ground level to the roof deck or highest point of the structure
- Be continuous with no unprotected openings or penetrations that compromise integrity
- Remain structurally self-supporting and independent
- Have clear separation on both sides extending at least 4 inches (or code minimum for parapet)
- Include proper detailing at all intersections with floors, roofs, and exterior walls

### 705.2 Fire-Resistance Ratings

Fire walls require specific fire-resistance ratings based on adjacent occupancy groups and building characteristics:

**4-Hour Fire Wall Rating:**
- Required where occupancy groups on either side present moderate to high hazard
- Required for separation between different uses in high-rise buildings (75+ feet)
- Required where Groups H (hazardous) or I (institutional/healthcare) are involved
- Required for separation between high-rise and lower structures

**3-Hour Fire Wall Rating:**
- Permitted where both sides contain Group B, M, R, or S occupancies
- Permitted for separation between Group A (assembly) and other less hazardous groups
- [VERIFY] Specific occupancy pair combinations requiring 3-hour vs. 4-hour per 2022 BC

### 705.3 Structural Stability and Independence

A critical requirement of fire wall construction is that the wall must remain standing and structurally stable even if the construction on one or both sides collapses or burns away. This requirement means:

- Fire wall must be self-supporting (no lateral bracing from adjacent structures)
- Beams, joists, or other members framing into the wall must be detailed to allow wall independence
- Supports may be cut or corbelled to ensure the wall can stand alone
- Floor assemblies framing into the wall must be detailed so they don't brace the wall
- Connections must allow relative movement between the wall and adjacent construction

### 705.4 Openings and Penetrations

Fire walls are permitted to contain openings ONLY where specifically authorized by code:

- Doors: Limited openings with 3 or 4-hour fire door assemblies and automatic closing
- Mechanical/Electrical: Only where penetrations are fully protected with appropriate rated protection
- Utilities: Penetrations must maintain wall integrity and fire-resistance rating

All openings must be clearly indicated on construction documents with ratings and protective device specifications.

### 705.5 Parapets and Roof Details

Fire walls must extend through the roof with adequate parapet height (typically minimum 3 feet above roof) to prevent fire from leaping over the wall. Roof covering must be properly terminated at the parapet. All roof-to-wall connections must be shown in construction documents.

## Section 706: Fire Barriers

Fire barriers (§706) are vertical assemblies of materials that establish and maintain compartments to resist the spread of fire. Unlike fire walls, fire barriers do not extend through the roof and are not required to remain standing if adjacent construction collapses.

### 706.1 General

Fire barriers are used to:

- Separate different occupancies within a building
- Protect exit enclosures (stairs, elevators, egress corridors)
- Separate corridors from adjacent spaces
- Protect incidental use areas
- Separate dwelling units in residential buildings
- Separate tenant spaces in multi-tenant buildings

Fire barriers must be continuous and must include protected joints and penetrations. All penetrations must be sealed per §712 (Penetrations).

### 706.3 Uses and Applications of Fire Barriers

**Exit Enclosures (§706.3.7):**
Fire barriers must separate exit enclosures (stairs, elevator hoistways, exit corridors) from adjacent occupiable spaces.

Fire-resistance ratings:
- **2-hour rating:** Required for exit enclosures in buildings with four or more occupied stories
- **1-hour rating:** Required for exit enclosures in buildings with three or fewer occupied stories
- **High-rise (75+ feet):** Typically 2-hour minimum; verify per Table 706.3.7

**Corridor Walls (§706.3.8 and Table 1018.1):**
Corridors must be separated from adjacent spaces with fire barriers per Table 1018.1.

Typical ratings:
- Group B, M, S: 1-hour to 1.5-hour fire barrier
- Group A, R, I: 1-hour to 2-hour fire barrier
- Sprinklered vs. non-sprinklered: Different requirements per occupancy

All corridor ratings must be verified in Table 1018.1 based on occupancy and construction type.

**Dwelling Unit Separation (§706.3.9):**
In residential buildings (Group R), individual dwelling units must be separated by fire barriers.

Fire-resistance ratings:
- **1-hour rating:** Standard requirement for fire barriers between dwelling units
- **0.5-hour rating:** Permitted in fully sprinklered buildings in certain construction types
- **Attic/plenum:** Separate protection required per code provisions

**Incidental Use Area Separation (Table 508.2.5):**
Storage rooms, mechanical spaces, trash collection rooms, and other incidental use areas must be separated from main occupiable spaces with fire barriers.

Ratings vary based on:
- Type of incidental use (storage, mechanical, electrical, trash)
- Sprinkler protection (reduces rating in some cases)
- Hazard classification
- Typical range: 1-hour to 2-hour fire barriers

**Tenant Separation (§706.3.6):**
In multi-tenant buildings, different tenant spaces must be separated by fire barriers. Ratings typically 1-hour for most occupancies; higher ratings may apply for certain hazardous uses.

### 706.4 Construction and Continuity (2022 Updates)

Fire barriers must:

- Extend from floor slab to deck of floor above (no gaps at floor/ceiling interface)
- Extend fully across the space unless code permits alternatives
- Maintain continuity through all penetrations and joints
- Include proper detailing at wall intersections with structural members
- Comply with all provisions of §712 (Penetrations) and §713 (Fire-Resistant Joints)

## Section 707: Shaft Enclosures

Shaft enclosures (§707) protect vertical openings through multiple stories that accommodate equipment, persons, or materials. These shafts must be enclosed with fire-resistance-rated construction to prevent fire and smoke from traveling vertically through the building.

### 707.1 General

Any opening through a floor or roof that penetrates multiple stories must be protected. This includes openings for:

- Elevators and elevator hoistways
- Stairs and exit stairways
- Escalators and moving walks
- Mechanical ducts and HVAC systems
- Plumbing chases and water supply lines
- Electrical conduit and cable trays
- Telecommunications and data systems
- Other vertical conveyances and systems

Shaft enclosures must:

- Be continuous from the foundation to the roof or to the floor above
- Maintain integrity throughout the vertical extent
- Include proper fire door assemblies at all openings
- Protect all penetrations per §712

### 707.2 Shaft Enclosure Fire-Resistance Ratings (§707.4)

Shaft enclosure fire-resistance ratings depend on building height and occupancy:

**Buildings with 4 or more occupied stories:**
- **2-hour fire-resistance rating** required for shaft enclosures

**Buildings with 3 or fewer occupied stories:**
- **1-hour fire-resistance rating** required for shaft enclosures

**High-rise buildings (75+ feet):**
- **2-hour minimum** with additional requirements for mechanical ventilation and smoke control

**Healthcare facilities (Group I occupancies):**
- **Increased ratings or additional protection** per §707 amendments for smoke control

**Table 707.2 Reference:** Verify specific requirements per building height, occupancy group, and construction type in Table 707.2 of the 2022 NYC BC.

### 707.5 Elevator Shafts and Lobbies (§707.14, NYC Amendment)

A notable NYC-specific requirement applies to elevator shafts and lobbies in high-rise buildings. Where the lowest floor level of a building used for human occupancy is 75 feet or more above the lowest level of fire department vehicle access:

**Elevator Shaft Enclosure:** 2-hour fire barrier minimum, with protected openings and fire dampers on all HVAC penetrations.

**Elevator Lobby Requirement (§707.14):**
Elevators serving occupied floors above the 75-foot threshold must open into a lobby separated from corridors by fire-rated assemblies. The lobby configuration options:

- **Single Elevator (1-2 units):** May open directly into corridor if equipped with smoke control (enclosed stairwell concept)
- **Multiple Elevators (3+ units):** Require dedicated lobby separated from corridor by fire-rated partition
- **Smoke Control:** Lobby must be equipped with active or passive smoke control system to prevent smoke from entering elevator hoistway

The elevator lobby must have:

- At least one side wall open to an unobstructed corridor or outside, OR
- Approved smoke control system per §909 preventing smoke entry
- All doorways equipped with fire-rated devices
- Clear signage indicating emergency procedures

This requirement is more stringent than the International Building Code and reflects NYC's specific high-rise fire safety priorities developed following major fire incidents.

### 707.6 Duct Penetrations of Shaft Enclosures

All ducts passing through shaft enclosures must be protected with fire dampers rated for the shaft enclosure fire-resistance rating. Smoke dampers may be required in addition per §909 (Smoke Control Systems).

Fire dampers must:

- Be listed and labeled for the specific shaft enclosure rating
- Be installed per manufacturer's instructions
- Remain accessible for inspection and maintenance
- Be inspected and tested annually

### 707.7 Openings in Shaft Enclosures (§707.7)

All openings in shaft enclosures must be protected by:

- **Fire door assemblies** for pedestrian access, rated per Table 714.2
- **Fire dampers** for mechanical duct penetrations
- **Combination systems** for complex penetrations

Elevator doorways require fire door assemblies rated to match the shaft enclosure rating. Dampers must be rated for the shaft enclosure, not the hoistway alone.

## Section 708: Fire Partitions

Fire partitions (§708) are less restrictive than fire barriers and are used primarily for corridor separations, dwelling unit separations in certain circumstances, and sleeping unit separations.

### 708.1 General

Fire partitions establish a level of fire separation less stringent than fire barriers. Fire partitions:

- Do not need to extend through the roof
- Are not required to be self-supporting
- Must maintain their fire-resistance rating
- Must provide continuity of materials through penetrations and joints
- May have larger openings relative to wall area than fire barriers

Fire partitions are required for:

- Corridor walls in buildings where Table 1018.1 permits a fire partition instead of a fire barrier
- Dwelling unit separations in sprinklered buildings where code permits
- Sleeping unit separations in certain occupancies (Group I, R)
- Certain tenant separations where code permits reduced requirements

### 708.4 Fire-Resistance Rating

Fire partition ratings are:

- **1-hour rating:** Standard fire-resistance rating for most fire partitions, including corridor walls
- **0.5-hour rating:** Permitted for dwelling unit and sleeping unit separations in buildings with an approved automatic sprinkler system throughout the building

The lower 0.5-hour rating for sprinklered buildings recognizes the additional protection provided by the sprinkler system and is a critical trade-off consideration in building design.

### 708.5 2022 Updates on Fire Partitions

- Clarified distinction between fire barriers (§706) and fire partitions (§708)
- Enhanced requirements for continuity through penetrations (still per §712 but with added clarity)
- Updated coordination with Table 1018.1 corridor ratings
- [VERIFY] Specific changes to dwelling unit separation requirements in Group R occupancies

## Section 709: Smoke Barriers

Smoke barriers (§709) are vertical or horizontal assemblies used to divide stories into smoke compartments, creating areas that can be managed independently during a fire event. Smoke barriers restrict the movement of smoke while maintaining the building's structural system.

### 709.1 General

Smoke barriers must be continuous vertical or horizontal assemblies that:

- Extend fully across the space they are meant to subdivide
- Restrict the movement of smoke
- Have a fire-resistance rating (typically 1 hour minimum)
- Protect all penetrations and openings

Applications include:

- Hospital and healthcare facilities (Group I), dividing stories into smoke compartments for life safety
- High-rise residential buildings (75+ feet), where compartmentalization of smoke is necessary
- Large floor plates where compartmentalization facilitates evacuation
- Nursing homes and assisted living facilities

### 709.2 Fire-Resistance Rating

Smoke barriers must have a fire-resistance rating of **1 hour minimum**. This provides a level of fire resistance comparable to fire partitions used for corridor separation and ensures structural integrity during fires.

### 709.3 Smoke Doors and Controls (§709.5)

Doors in smoke barriers must be designed to control smoke movement and must include:

- **Smoke seals:** Perimeter seals that restrict smoke passage around door leaf
- **Automatic closing:** Self-closing devices or automatic closers activated by smoke detectors
- **Fire-Resistance Rating:** May require fire-rated doors in addition to smoke control features (per occupancy)

Typical door ratings in smoke barriers:
- **Healthcare (Group I):** 1-hour fire-rated door with smoke seals
- **Residential (Group R):** 1-hour fire-rated smoke door per code requirements
- **Passive vs. Active:** May use automatic closers with smoke detection or passive gravity closure

### 709.4 2022 Healthcare Provisions (Group I)

For healthcare facilities, smoke barriers serve a critical role in evacuation and smoke management:

- Smoke compartments typically limited to one-half of any floor area
- Horizontal exits through smoke barriers into refuge areas required at intervals
- Active smoke control systems may coordinate with smoke barrier placement
- Enhanced inspection and maintenance requirements apply

## Section 710: Smoke Partitions

Smoke partitions (§710) are used where smoke control is necessary but the stringent requirements of smoke barriers are not mandated. Smoke partitions may or may not have a fire-resistance rating.

### 710.1 General

Smoke partitions are continuous vertical or horizontal assemblies that:

- Restrict the movement of smoke
- May not have a fire-resistance rating (code permits smoke-only partitions)
- Are less stringent than smoke barriers in construction and protection requirements
- Include provisions for door seals and dampers to control smoke passage

### 710.2 Applications

Smoke partitions are used in:

- Corridor separations where smoke control is beneficial but not mandated by code
- Lobby areas to prevent smoke from entering exit stairways
- Mechanical equipment spaces
- Areas where smoke restriction enhances safety without the full requirements of a smoke barrier
- Non-healthcare facilities where compartmentalization is desired

### 710.3 Doors and Dampers

Doors in smoke partitions may have:

- **Smoke seals** to restrict smoke movement (no fire-resistance rating required)
- **Automatic closers** activated by smoke detection
- **Dampers** in ductwork penetrations

Unlike smoke barrier doors, smoke partition doors do not necessarily require fire-resistance ratings, though code may require them based on occupancy.

## Section 711: Horizontal Assemblies (Floor/Ceiling Assemblies)

Horizontal assemblies, including floor and ceiling assemblies, must provide the fire-resistance rating required by the construction type classification and occupancy group per Table 601.

### 711.1 General

Floor construction and associated ceiling membranes must be designed as assemblies that:

- Maintain their fire-resistance rating and structural integrity during a fire event
- Serve dual function of providing structural support and containing fire/smoke
- Contain fire and smoke to individual floor levels
- Prevent vertical fire spread through the building

Floor/ceiling assemblies are among the most critical assemblies in the building and are subject to rigorous testing and construction requirements.

### 711.2 Fire-Resistance Rating (Table 601)

The fire-resistance rating of floor and ceiling assemblies is determined by Table 601 (Fire-Resistance Rating Requirements by Building Type and Occupancy) and depends on:

- **Building construction type** (Type I-FR, I-A through Type V-B per 2022 updates)
- **Occupancy classification** (Group A, B, E, F, H, I, M, R, S, U)

Examples (2022 BC Table 601):
- **Type I-FR Construction:** 2-hour or 3-hour ratings for structural members and assemblies
- **Type I-A Construction:** 1-hour or 2-hour ratings
- **Type II Construction:** 1-hour or 2-hour ratings depending on occupancy
- **Type III/IV/V Construction:** 0-hour or 1-hour ratings

**Table 601 Reference:** Critical table for design. Must be cited in design narratives and construction documents. Cross-reference with occupancy and construction type classification.

### 711.3 Structural Integrity

Floor assemblies must be designed and constructed to maintain structural integrity and load-bearing capacity when exposed to fire. This requirement includes:

- Proper sizing of structural members
- Adequate connection detailing
- Protection of connections
- Consideration of heat transfer and member degradation during fire

### 711.4 Penetrations (§711.4 and §712)

All penetrations through fire-resistance-rated floor/ceiling assemblies must be protected to maintain the assembly's integrity. This includes:

- Pipes (plumbing, gas, hydronic, refrigerant)
- Ducts (HVAC systems)
- Conduits (electrical, communication)
- Cables and wires
- Other similar items

Unprotected penetrations create paths for fire and smoke to travel vertically through the building, negating the compartmentalization provided by the floor assembly itself. This is a critical inspection item for plan review and construction administration.

### 711.5 2022 Updates on Horizontal Assemblies

- Enhanced coordination with §712 on penetration protection
- Clarified testing requirements for proprietary floor assemblies
- Updated guidance on firestop system installation for common penetration types
- [VERIFY] Changes to treatment of electrical outlets and low-pressure systems in floor assemblies

## Section 712: Penetrations

One of the most critical aspects of fire-resistance-rated construction is the protection of penetrations. A single unprotected penetration can render an entire fire-resistance-rated assembly ineffective.

### 712.1 General

All penetrations through fire-resistance-rated assemblies, whether vertical or horizontal, must be protected by an approved firestop system or other means that maintains the fire-resistance rating of the assembly.

Penetrations are created by:

- **Pipes:** Plumbing, gas, hydronic, refrigerant, steam
- **Ducts:** HVAC supply/return systems, smoke control systems
- **Conduits:** Electrical, communication, optical fiber
- **Cables and Wires:** Power, data, communication, alarm
- **Other:** Sprinkler lines, equipment supports, conduit seals

### 712.2 Firestop System Requirements

Firestop systems must:

- Be **listed and labeled** for the specific penetration type, wall assembly, and fire-resistance rating
- Include **installation manual** from manufacturer with detailed procedures
- Maintain the **integrity** property (no passage of flame/hot gases) of the assembly
- Maintain the **insulation** property (temperature limitation) where applicable
- Be installed **strictly in accordance** with manufacturer's instructions and testing laboratory's requirements

Improper installation can result in failure of the firestop to prevent fire spread. This is a major inspection issue and a common cause of plan rejection during DOB review.

### 712.3 Through-Penetrations of Fire-Resistance-Rated Walls (§712.3)

Through-penetrations of fire-resistance-rated walls must be protected by approved firestop systems. Requirements:

- System must be listed for the specific wall assembly and penetration
- Materials must match those tested (e.g., steel pipe vs. PVC, 1-inch vs. 2-inch diameter)
- Installation must precisely follow manufacturer's instructions
- Firestop material must be properly compacted and sealed
- Clearance requirements around penetration item must be maintained

**Common Penetrations:**
- Plumbing stacks: typically caulked or packed firestop
- HVAC ducts: dampers + firestop
- Electrical conduit: proprietary systems or caulk
- Cable bundles: specialty cable firestops

All systems must be shown on construction documents with system manufacturer name, product designation, and installation details.

### 712.4 Fire-Resistance-Rated Horizontal Assemblies (§712.4)

Penetrations through fire-resistance-rated floor/ceiling and roof/ceiling assemblies must be similarly protected with listed firestop systems. The protection must maintain both the **integrity** and **insulation** properties of the assembly.

Requirements are more stringent than vertical penetrations because floor penetrations are subject to thermal exposure from both directions. Systems must be tested and listed for the specific assembly, penetration type, and rating.

### 712.5 Membrane Penetrations (§712.5)

Penetrations through only the protective membrane (not through the structural part of the assembly) must still be protected. These membrane penetrations can allow flame and hot gases to spread across the surface of the assembly or to hidden cavities.

Membrane penetrations may be protected by:

- **Listed firestop systems:** Proprietary caulks, putties, or sealing systems
- **Tested construction details:** Approved alternative methods with documentation
- **Protective sleeves or collars:** Approved device-specific systems
- **Combination approaches:** Multiple protection layers for complex penetrations

Examples: Outlet boxes on fire-resistance-rated walls, cable tray seals, ductwork collars.

### 712.6 Draftstopping vs. Firestopping (Clarification)

2022 updates clarify distinction between:

- **Firestopping (§712):** Protection of penetrations through rated assemblies to maintain fire-resistance rating
- **Draftstopping (§717.3):** Blocking of concealed spaces to prevent smoke/flame passage in attics, crawl spaces, and concealed cavities

Both are required; both have specific product and installation requirements.

## Section 713: Fire-Resistant Joint Systems

Joints in or between fire-resistance-rated assemblies must be protected to prevent the passage of flame and hot gases along the joint. These joints occur in multiple locations:

- Between floor and wall assemblies (floor-to-perimeter wall joints)
- Between two walls (wall-to-wall intersections)
- Where walls meet structural framing
- In curtain wall and facade systems
- At expansion and control joints in slabs
- At relief joints and seismic movement accommodations

### 713.1 General

Fire-resistant joint systems must:

- Be **tested and approved** for the specific application and fire-resistance rating
- Maintain both **integrity** and **insulation** properties during fire exposure
- Remain effective if the building experiences **structural movement**, expansion, or contraction
- Be installed **continuously** throughout the joint
- Include **proper detailing** at intersections with structural elements

### 713.2 Joint System Types

Common fire-resistant joint systems include:

- **Intumescent systems:** Expand when exposed to heat to form barrier (commonly used in curtain wall systems)
- **Caulked joints:** Proprietary sealants and backer materials (floor-to-wall applications)
- **Barrier systems:** Mineral fiber, ceramic, or comparable materials
- **Combination systems:** Multiple protection layers for complex joints

All systems must be from a tested and approved assembly (listed with testing lab).

### 713.3 Movement Accommodation

A critical aspect of joint system design is accommodating **structural movement** including:

- Thermal expansion and contraction (different materials have different expansion rates)
- Seismic movement (lateral displacement during earthquakes)
- Settlement and deflection of structural members
- Vibration from mechanical systems

Joint systems must be designed to:

- Allow movement without compromising fire-resistance rating
- Include proper gap sizing and material selection for expected movement
- Maintain seal integrity after movement occurs
- Be periodically inspected and maintained

### 713.4 Floor-to-Perimeter Wall Joints (§713.3)

A critical application is the joint between the floor slab and the perimeter wall assembly. In exterior wall construction, this joint must:

- Prevent fire spread into concealed spaces (between floor slab and wall)
- Maintain exterior wall fire-resistance rating
- Allow for expansion/contraction and structural movement

Common detailing includes:

- Intumescent sealant systems in curtain wall systems
- Mineral fiber blocking in traditional wall assemblies
- Proprietary joint systems tested for the specific construction

Improper detailing at this location can allow fire to spread vertically around the rated wall assembly. This is a critical design detail in plan review.

### 713.5 2022 Updates on Joint Systems

- Enhanced requirements for high-rise buildings (75+ feet)
- Updated guidance on seismic joint details in high-seismic areas (NYC does not have major seismic requirements but systems must not compromise fire rating)
- New standards for curtain wall system joints [VERIFY specific testing standards]
- Emphasis on coordination with structural engineer on movement analysis
- Construction documentation requirements clarified (all joint systems must be shown and specified in construction documents)

## Section 714: Opening Protectives

Opening protectives (fire door assemblies, fire window assemblies, and fire dampers) protect openings in fire-resistance-rated assemblies and must be maintained in proper working condition.

### 714.1 General

Every opening in a fire-resistance-rated assembly must be protected by an approved opening protective unless the code permits the opening to be unprotected. The opening protective must have a fire-resistance rating appropriate to the assembly it protects.

Approved opening protectives are limited to:

- **Fire door assemblies** (doors, frames, hardware)
- **Fire window assemblies** (glazing systems)
- **Fire dampers** (ductwork closure devices)
- **Smoke dampers** (smoke control devices)
- **Combination systems** (hybrid protection)

All devices must be **listed and labeled** by approved testing laboratory (UL, NFPA, or equivalent).

### 714.2 Fire Door and Shutter Assemblies (§714.2)

Fire door assemblies provide the most common type of opening protective in fire-resistance-rated walls. Each fire door assembly includes:

- Door leaf (solid or with approved vision panels)
- Frame (steel, aluminum, or tested alternative)
- Hardware (hinges, closers, locks, latches)
- Seals (thermal/acoustic strips for rated doors)
- Vision panels (if included, must be rated compatible glass)

**Fire-Resistance Ratings for Fire Doors (Table 714.2):**

Fire door assembly ratings depend on the fire-resistance rating of the wall or barrier they protect:

- **3-hour wall:** Requires **3-hour fire door assembly** (rare in NYC, primarily Type I construction)
- **2-hour wall:** Requires **1.5-hour fire door assembly**
- **1.5-hour wall:** Requires **1.5-hour fire door assembly**
- **1-hour wall:** Requires **0.75-hour (45-minute) fire door assembly** OR **0.33-hour (20-minute) assembly in corridor**
- **0.5-hour wall/Fire partition:** Requires **0.5-hour fire door assembly**

**Key Points:**
- Door rating is typically based on testing per UL 10B or NFPA 252 (current 2022 versions)
- Door rating may be lower than wall rating in specific code-permitted situations (e.g., corridors)
- All hardware must be listed as part of the assembly (no substitution permitted)
- Vision panels (if used) must be fire-rated glazing approved for the door

**Closing Requirements:**
Fire doors must be:
- Self-closing (gravity or spring closing)
- Automatic-closing (activated by smoke detectors in some applications)
- Equipped with positive latching devices (to ensure door remains closed)

All automatic systems require backup mechanical closing device to prevent failure.

**2022 Updates on Fire Doors:**
- Enhanced requirements for testing per UL 10B-22 (current standard)
- Updated vision panel requirements and approved glazing materials
- Stricter labeling and identification requirements
- New guidance on electrified locks and access control integration [VERIFY specific requirements]
- Mandatory operation and maintenance plan requirements

### 714.3 Fire Window Assemblies (§714.3)

Fire window assemblies provide rated protection of openings in fire-resistance-rated walls. Fire windows must have frames, glass, and glazing materials that together resist the passage of flame and hot gases for the specified time period.

**Fire Window Ratings:**
- 0.33-hour (20-minute) - typically for lower-rated assemblies
- 0.5-hour - most common for light traffic areas
- 0.75-hour (45-minute) - for 1-hour assemblies
- 1.5-hour - for higher-rated assemblies (less common)
- 3-hour - rare, requires special glazing and frames

**Glazing Materials:**
- Tempered glass or safety glass (per ASTM standards)
- Borosilicate glass (thermal-resistant glass)
- Proprietary fire-rated glazing (ceramic, specialty materials)
- Wire-reinforced glass (limited application due to wire exposure)

**Testing:**
Fire windows must be tested per UL 9 or NFPA 257 (current 2022 standards) and include:
- Integrity: No openings permitting flame passage
- Thermal insulation: Temperature rise limits on unexposed side
- Hose stream test: Resistance to impact and stream application (for certain ratings)

**Applications:**
Fire windows are used in:
- Exterior walls (limited openings in rated assemblies)
- Interior walls (vision panels between fire-rated spaces)
- Occupancy separations where visibility is desired
- Elevator lobby walls (in some configurations)

Fire windows are less commonly required than fire doors but are critical where code mandates visibility into fire-rated spaces (e.g., emergency exits, control rooms).

### 714.4 2022 Standards Updates for Doors and Windows

- **UL 10B-22:** Current standard for fire door assemblies (replaces prior versions)
- **UL 9-22:** Current standard for fire window assemblies
- **NFPA 80 (2019):** Standard for Fire Doors and Other Opening Protectives (incorporates updates to testing and maintenance)
- Enhanced coordination between standards required
- [VERIFY] Specific changes to testing procedures and acceptance criteria

### 714.5 Fire Dampers (§714.5)

Fire dampers are installed in ductwork that penetrates fire-resistance-rated walls or barriers. The damper closes automatically when exposed to elevated temperatures, blocking the flow of air and preventing fire from spreading through the duct system.

**Fire Damper Requirements:**
- Listed and labeled for the fire-resistance rating of the wall or barrier
- Installed in accordance with manufacturer's instructions
- Accessible for inspection and maintenance
- Inspected annually (quarterly or more frequently in high-occupancy buildings)
- Replaced if damaged or unable to close properly

**Damper Specifications:**
- Damper rating must match wall/barrier fire-resistance rating
- Damper frame must match duct material (steel damper in steel duct, etc.)
- Thermal elements must function reliably (typical 212°F to 350°F activation range)
- Damper must be rated for the specific ductwork configuration (straight penetration, offset, etc.)

**Installation Detailing:**
- Damper must be installed perpendicular to wall or barrier
- Ductwork support must not prevent damper closure
- Damper access door must be on accessible side of wall
- Firestop must be installed on duct penetration (damper alone is insufficient)

### 714.6 Smoke Dampers (§714.6)

Smoke dampers are installed in ductwork that penetrates smoke barriers or smoke partitions. Unlike fire dampers, smoke dampers may be actuated by smoke detectors rather than thermal elements.

**Smoke Damper Function:**
- Smoke dampers close in response to smoke detection (photo-electric or ionization detectors)
- May also include thermal elements (combination smoke/fire damper)
- Prevent smoke from spreading through the HVAC system
- Coordinate with smoke control systems to manage smoke compartments

**Typical Applications:**
- Ductwork serving multiple smoke compartments in hospitals
- Return air ducts in buildings with smoke control systems
- Supply air systems where smoke isolation is critical

**2022 Updates on Dampers:**
- Enhanced coordination between fire dampers and smoke control systems
- Updated testing standards [VERIFY specific damper standards in 2022 BC]
- Stricter maintenance and inspection requirements
- New guidance on damper verification testing

## Section 715: Concealed Spaces

### 715.1 General

Concealed spaces (voids in walls, floor-ceiling assemblies, attics) must be protected to prevent fire spread through hidden cavities. Protection methods include:

- **Draftstopping:** Blocking of concealed spaces at intervals (typically 25-40 feet horizontally, and at each floor)
- **Firestopping:** Protection of penetrations in rated assemblies
- **Automatic sprinklers:** In some occupancies, sprinklers may substitute for draftstopping
- **Non-combustible materials:** Use of non-combustible insulation and materials

### 715.2 Application

Draftstopping is required in:

- **Attics and roof spaces:** Divided into compartments with baffle blocks
- **Concealed spaces between floor and ceiling:** Divided to prevent spread along entire floor
- **Wall cavities:** In certain construction types and occupancies
- **Accessible crawl spaces:** Must be blocked to prevent fire/smoke passage

Draftstopping requirements vary by occupancy and construction type; consult §715 and related occupancy-specific sections.

## Section 716: Fireblocking

### 716.1 General

Fireblocking is the placement of non-combustible materials to block the movement of flame through concealed spaces. Fireblocking is required in wood-frame construction (Type III, IV, V) and in certain other construction types.

### 716.2 Locations Requiring Fireblocking

Fireblocking is required at:

- **Vertical spaces:** Stud cavities, pipe chases, voids
- **Horizontal spaces:** Between floor joists, bridging spaces
- **Openings around penetrations:** Pipes, ducts, conduits
- **Eaves:** Vents between ceiling and roof in wood-frame construction

Common fireblocking materials:

- 2-inch nominal lumber (non-combustible rating required in some cases)
- Non-combustible batt insulation
- Gypsum board (certain applications)
- Cementitious materials

Fireblocking requirements are typically more stringent in residential (Group R) construction and vary by occupancy.

## Section 717: Cavity Barriers

### 717.1 General

Cavity barriers are blocking of concealed spaces in buildings to prevent rapid fire spread through the spaces. In addition to fireblocking, cavity barriers may be required in certain construction types and occupancies.

### 717.2 Applications

Cavity barriers are required in:

- Concealed spaces in Type I and Type II construction (where construction type permits concealed spaces)
- Exterior wall cavities in certain occupancies
- Attic spaces in all construction types (per §714/717)

Cavity barriers must:

- Be non-combustible materials
- Extend continuously across the space
- Be installed at specified intervals (typically 10-20 feet)
- Block concealed spaces to prevent fire/smoke passage

## Section 718: Fire Protection of Structural Members (Fireproofing)

### 718.1 General

Structural members required to have fire-resistance ratings must be protected with fireproofing (insulation, encasement, intumescent coatings) to maintain structural integrity during fire exposure.

**Construction Types Requiring Fireproofing:**
- **Type I-FR:** 3-hour fireproofing on primary structural elements
- **Type I-A:** 2-hour fireproofing on primary structural elements
- **Type II-A:** 1-hour fireproofing (some elements); 2-hour for certain configurations
- **Type II-B:** 1-hour fireproofing on primary structural steel
- **Type III/IV/V:** 0-hour to 1-hour depending on occupancy and height

### 718.2 Fireproofing Materials

Common fireproofing materials include:

- **Spray-Applied Fireproofing:** Mineral fiber, cementitious spray
- **Encasement:** Concrete, gypsum, masonry
- **Intumescent Coatings:** Paint-like coatings that expand when heated
- **Blanket Systems:** Pre-fabricated insulation blankets (for specific elements)

All fireproofing must be:

- Listed and labeled for the specific member type and fire-resistance rating
- Applied per manufacturer's specifications
- Protected from damage (encased or located where not subject to impact)
- Verified by qualified inspector during and after installation

### 718.3 Quality Assurance and Testing

Fireproofing thickness and coverage must be verified:

- During installation (no sealed ceilings until verified)
- After completion (testing/probing per ASTM E2218 or equivalent)
- Bond testing for spray-applied products
- Documentation required for DOB acceptance

Thickness is critical; under-thickness is primary cause of structural failure in fires.

## Table 601: Fire-Resistance Rating Requirements by Construction Type and Occupancy

### Critical Reference Table

Table 601 (Fire-Resistance Rating Requirements by Type of Construction and Occupancy Classification) is the most important table in Chapter 7 for determining design requirements. The table specifies fire-resistance ratings for:

- **Structural frame**
- **Exterior walls** (by occupancy and FSD)
- **Interior bearing walls**
- **Floor construction and associated beams**
- **Roof construction and associated beams**

### Construction Types (2022 Classification)

The 2022 NYC BC reorganizes construction types with new designations:

**Type I - Fire Resistive:**
- I-FR: Highest fire-resistance requirements (3+ hours)
- I-A: Secondary high-resistance requirements (2-3 hours)

**Type II - Non-Combustible:**
- II-A: 1-hour to 2-hour requirements
- II-B: Limited to 1-hour requirements for some elements

**Type III - Ordinary:**
- III-A: 1-hour fire barriers required
- III-B: 0-hour (non-rated) permitted in some elements

**Type IV - Heavy Timber:**
- IV-HT: Specific requirements for large-dimension timber

**Type V - Wood Frame:**
- V-A: 1-hour fire barriers required for some elements
- V-B: 0-hour (non-rated) permitted

### Occupancy Groups (Summarized)

- **Group A:** Assembly occupancies (theaters, restaurants, auditoriums)
- **Group B:** Business occupancies (offices, banks)
- **Group E:** Educational occupancies (schools)
- **Group F:** Factory/Industrial (light and moderate hazard)
- **Group H:** High-hazard occupancies (explosive, flammable materials)
- **Group I:** Institutional/Healthcare (hospitals, nursing homes, prisons)
- **Group M:** Mercantile (retail, shopping centers)
- **Group R:** Residential (apartments, hotels, dormitories)
- **Group S:** Storage (warehouses, parking garages)
- **Group U:** Utility/Miscellaneous

### Using Table 601 in Design

To determine fire-resistance rating requirements:

1. **Identify Building Construction Type** (Types I-V per code definition)
2. **Identify Occupancy Group** (A through U based on primary use)

4. **Consult Table 601** at intersection of construction type and occupancy
5. **Apply Ratings** to structural frame, exterior walls, bearing walls, floors, and roof

## Common Errors and DOB Rejection Issues

### Frequent Plan Rejections Related to Chapter 7

**1. Incorrect Fire-Resistance Ratings**
- Misapplication of Table 601 (wrong construction type or occupancy)
- Incorrect ratings for fire barriers vs. fire partitions
- Exterior wall rating not coordinated with fire separation distance
- **Solution:** Double-check Table 601 and Table 704.1.1 against actual construction type and occupancy

**2. Unprotected or Under-Protected Penetrations**
- Firestop systems not specified or listed
- Dampers in wrong size or rating
- Damper without firestop system
- **Solution:** Identify ALL penetrations and specify listed firestop system with manufacturer name and product designation

**3. Fire Door Assembly Deficiencies**
- Wrong door rating for assembly (not per Table 714.2)
- Hardware not listed with specific assembly
- Automatic closing device not specified
- Vision panel glazing not rated for application
- **Solution:** Specify complete fire door assembly per Table 714.2 with UL listing and all components

**4. Joint System Details Missing**
- Floor-to-perimeter wall joint not detailed
- Expansion joint protection not shown
- No fire-resistant joint system specified at wall-to-wall intersections
- **Solution:** Show all joints in detail with specific fire-resistant joint system specified

**5. High-Rise Building Elevator Lobby Omission**
- Elevator lobby not required (when required for 75+ foot buildings)
- Smoke control system not designed or coordinated
- **Solution:** Verify building height and elevator service; confirm lobby and smoke control requirements

**6. Fireproofing Thickness or Coverage Inadequate**
- Spray-applied fireproofing thickness not specified
- Connections and attachments not protected
- Bond testing not required (when should be)
- **Solution:** Specify thickness per rated assembly and required rating; include inspection/testing protocol

**7. Shaft Enclosure Deficiency**
- Opening not protected with fire door assembly
- Damper rating doesn't match shaft enclosure rating
- Elevator lobby missing in high-rise building
- **Solution:** Detail all shaft enclosure openings with appropriate opening protectives per §714

**8. Smoke Barrier/Partition Deficiency**
- Smoke barrier used where fire barrier required
- Door not specified with smoke seals and latching
- Penetrations not protected
- **Solution:** Distinguish between fire barriers and smoke barriers per occupancy; specify door requirements

**9. Fire Wall Structural Independence Not Shown**
- Fire wall appears to depend on adjacent construction for bracing
- Connections of framing to fire wall not detailed to allow independence
- **Solution:** Detail fire wall with clear documentation of structural independence

**10. Missing or Incorrect Assembly Listing Information**
- Fire-resistance rating claimed but assembly not listed
- Listed assembly used in different configuration from tested assembly
- No testing documentation provided
- **Solution:** Confirm all assemblies are listed; provide listing documentation for DOB review

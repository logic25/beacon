# 1968 NYC Building Code - Subchapter 6: Means of Egress

## Overview

Subchapter 6 of the 1968 NYC Building Code establishes the requirements for means of egress in buildings. This code is significant because many older buildings constructed under this standard remain in operation today and continue to file under its provisions. The regulatory framework differs substantially from the modern 2014 code, particularly regarding fire escape acceptability and occupancy classifications.

## C26-602.1 (§27-355): Number of Exits Required

### Basic Requirement
Every floor or occupied space shall be provided with at least two exits that are as remote from each other as practical.

### Key Provisions:
- Minimum of two exits from every floor containing more than the first floor
- Exits must be arranged to minimize common hazards
- Remote placement requirement reduces likelihood of simultaneous blockage from fire or emergency conditions

### Exceptions to Two-Exit Requirement:
- Small buildings with limited occupant load may be permitted single exits under specified conditions
- Buildings with occupant loads not exceeding limits set for specific occupancy groups
- Ground floor exits do not require secondary exit if emergency egress through storefront windows is available
- One-story buildings with occupant loads below specified thresholds

## C26-603.1 (§27-356 through §27-370): Exit Types

### Interior Stairs (§27-356)
- Stairways enclosed in fireproof construction
- Minimum width requirements based on occupancy group
- Handrails and guardrails required
- Maximum rise and minimum tread dimensions specified
- Landings required at each story and at changes of direction
- Doors to interior stairs must swing in direction of exit travel
- Self-closing and automatic closing devices permitted

### Exterior Stairs (§27-360)
- Stairs on exterior of building structure
- Must be of noncombustible construction
- Separate enclosure not required if protected by open construction
- Direct egress to ground level or street
- Handrails and protective railings required
- Maximum angle of inclination

### Fire Escapes (§27-363)
- Exterior metal structures serving as supplementary means of egress
- Consists of stairs, platforms, and railings
- Must meet specific loading and construction standards
- Acceptable as secondary means of egress under 1968 code
- Direct access from floor balconies or windows
- Ladder access from lowest platform to ground level

### Horizontal Exits (§27-365)
- Passage from one building or part of building to another at same level
- Through firewall with opening protected by fire doors
- Allows occupants to move laterally away from fire
- Requires adequate floor area in adjacent space for refuge

### Corridors (§27-367)
- Protected corridors required in certain occupancy groups
- Dead-end corridors limited to specified lengths
- Minimum width based on occupant load
- Doors along corridors must not open into corridor unrestricted
- Protected corridor construction requirements

### Doors (§27-370)
- Doors to exit must swing in direction of exit travel
- Minimum width: 32 inches clear opening for most occupancies
- Maximum spacing between exits when multiple exits required
- Hardware requirements and operation specifications
- No chains, padlocks, or devices restricting egress
- Doors must not swing into exits

## C26-604.1 (§27-371 through §27-386): Exit Requirements by Occupancy Group

### Group A (Assembly Occupancies)
- Theaters, auditoriums, churches, meeting halls
- Multiple exits required based on occupant load
- Travel distance limits from any point to exit
- Exit width calculated by occupancy load factors
- Special provisions for balconies and mezzanines

### Group B (Residential Occupancies)
- Apartments, hotels, dormitories
- Two exits from each floor except first floor
- Stairways must be enclosed
- Corridor widths and dead-end limits specified
- Open balconies may serve as secondary egress

### Group C (Office Occupancies)
- Office buildings and professional spaces
- Exit requirements based on floor area and occupant load
- Stairway enclosure requirements
- Corridor protection standards
- Dead-end corridor restrictions

### Group D (Mercantile Occupancies)
- Retail stores, markets, shopping areas
- Exits based on occupant load calculations
- Width requirements for exits and aisles
- Stairway enclosure in multistory structures
- Merchandise display restrictions near exits

### Group E (Industrial Occupancies)
- Factories, manufacturing plants, workshops
- Special provisions for high-occupancy production areas
- Hazardous materials storage requirements
- Exit widths calculated for large occupant loads
- Separate requirements for mezzanine and balcony areas

### Group F (Storage Occupancies)
- Warehouses, storage facilities, lofts
- Exits based on floor area and use classification
- Lower occupant load density than assembly
- Stairway requirements simplified for storage only

### Group G (Miscellaneous Occupancies)
- Specialized uses not classified in other groups
- Case-by-case evaluation using comparable occupancy standards

## Fire Escape Provisions Under 1968 Code

### Acceptability as Required Exit

Fire escapes were permissible as required means of egress under the 1968 Building Code, representing a significant difference from modern standards:

- **For Existing Buildings**: Fire escapes counted as secondary means of egress (second exit requirement)
- **For New Construction**: Fire escapes were acceptable as secondary egress only (not primary exit)
- **Post-1968 Restrictions**: After 1968, new building requirements became more restrictive regarding fire escape acceptability

### Construction Standards

- Metal framework, stairs, and platforms
- Load capacity: Minimum 100 pounds per square foot
- Stair treads minimum 8 inches wide
- Landing platforms minimum 5 feet by 5 feet
- Railings minimum 42 inches high
- Guard rails minimum 30 inches high
- Regular inspection and maintenance required

### Access Requirements

- Access doors or windows from floor to fire escape platform
- Window sills not exceeding 42 inches from floor
- Ladder from lowest platform to ground or alley
- Clearance from walls and obstructions minimum 18 inches

## C26-273.0: Fire Escape Egress Requirements for Article 7-B Buildings

### Referenced Standard
C26-273.0 is referenced in §6.1.2.4 for fire escape egress specifications and was specifically cited in the 927 Broadway case regarding Article 7-B conversions (MDL Article 7-B).

### Application to Loft Conversions
- Fire escapes acceptable as secondary means of egress in converted loft buildings
- Applies specifically to buildings undergoing conversion to residential use
- Requirements balance fire safety with feasibility of retrofitting existing structures
- Part of regulatory framework permitting loft occupancy development

### Integration with Modern Rules
- C26-273.0 provisions superseded by 1 RCNY Chapter 15 for maintenance standards
- Fire escape acceptability now strictly regulated through RCNY Chapter 15, §15-10
- Buildings must meet both historical code provisions and current maintenance rules

## Reference Standard RS 6

Reference Standard 6 establishes detailed technical requirements for exit systems, including:

- Dimensional standards for stairs, landings, and platforms
- Material specifications for exit construction
- Load testing and structural requirements
- Hardware specifications and operational standards
- Accessibility requirements where applicable
- Fire protection ratings for exit enclosures

## Key Differences from 2014 Code

### Fire Escape Acceptability
- **1968 Code**: Fire escapes acceptable as secondary means of egress in existing buildings
- **2014 Code**: Fire escapes generally not acceptable as required exit except in very limited circumstances

### Occupancy Classifications
- 1968 code uses Group A-G classification system
- 2014 code uses more detailed ICC/IBC categories
- Travel distance limits differ significantly between codes
- Occupant load factors (persons per square foot) changed

### Travel Distance Limits
- 1968 code: Generally 150-200 feet depending on occupancy
- 2014 code: Generally 100-200 feet with different thresholds by occupancy
- Sprinklered buildings receive different allowances in 2014 code

### Stairway Requirements
- 1968 code: Less stringent minimum dimensions
- 2014 code: Wider stairs required for high-occupancy buildings
- Handrail and guardrail standards updated in 2014 code
- ADA accessibility requirements added (not present in 1968 code)

### Corridor Standards
- 1968 code: Minimum widths as narrow as 3 feet for low-occupancy areas
- 2014 code: Minimum widths increased across most occupancies
- Corridor fire rating requirements more stringent in 2014 code

## Historical Context and Application

The 1968 Building Code represents the regulatory framework under which many of New York City's older loft buildings, residential conversions, and industrial structures were originally constructed or permitted. Buildings constructed to 1968 code standards may continue to operate under grandfathering provisions or may seek compliance through equivalency demonstrations referencing the original code.

For buildings undergoing conversion (such as Article 7-B loft conversions), the 1968 code provisions regarding fire escape acceptability and exit requirements have been particularly significant in determining feasible retrofit solutions where complete compliance with modern codes may be structurally or economically impractical.

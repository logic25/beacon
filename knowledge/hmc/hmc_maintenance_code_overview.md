# NYC Housing Maintenance Code - Overview and Key Provisions

## Introduction

The Housing Maintenance Code (HMC), codified as Title 27, Chapter 2 of the New York City Administrative Code, establishes minimum standards for housing maintenance, safety, and habitability throughout New York City. Administered by the Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD), the Housing Maintenance Code applies comprehensively to all dwellings in the city, including one- and two-family homes, multiple dwellings, and all residential properties. The Code serves as the primary enforcement mechanism for ensuring that housing meets essential health, safety, and livability standards.

The Housing Maintenance Code operates in conjunction with other regulatory frameworks, including the Multiple Dwelling Law, the NYC Building Code, and various local laws addressing specific hazards such as lead-based paint. Together, these regulations create a comprehensive system for protecting tenants and occupants throughout New York City.

---

## Subchapter 1: General Provisions (§27-2001 through §27-2010)

### Scope and Definitions

The Housing Maintenance Code establishes clear definitions and broad applicability across all residential properties in New York City. Key definitions include:

**Dwelling**: Any building or portion thereof which is occupied as, arranged, intended, or designed to be used as the home, residence, or sleeping place of one or more persons.

**Multiple Dwelling**: Any dwelling which is occupied, or arranged, intended, or designed to be occupied, as the home or residence of three or more families living independently of each other and doing their own cooking upon the premises.

**Owner**: The person or entity vested with legal or equitable title to a building or dwelling, or any person or entity who controls or manages a building or dwelling as agent, lessee, or otherwise.

**Person**: Any natural person, partnership, corporation, association, or entity.

**Occupant**: Any person occupying a dwelling as a resident or tenant, whether or not as owner or leaseholder.

### Applicability

The Housing Maintenance Code applies universally to all dwellings within New York City jurisdiction. This includes:

- Single-family detached homes
- Two-family dwellings
- Multiple dwellings (three or more units)
- Owner-occupied properties
- Rental properties
- Commercial buildings with residential components

### Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD) Enforcement

HPD serves as the primary enforcement agency for the Housing Maintenance Code. The Department has authority to:

- Receive and investigate housing maintenance complaints
- Conduct inspections of residential properties
- Issue violations to property owners
- Establish cure periods for violations
- Assess penalties and fines
- Order emergency repairs
- Pursue court action against non-compliant owners
- Maintain violation records and enforcement history

### Owner Obligations vs. Occupant Obligations

The Housing Maintenance Code establishes a clear division of responsibility between property owners and occupants:

**Owner Obligations**: Owners are responsible for maintaining the structural integrity of buildings, providing essential services (heat, hot water, electricity), preventing pest infestations, maintaining common areas, ensuring fire safety devices are installed and operational, and providing minimum standard housing conditions. Owners cannot delegate their responsibility for these core obligations to tenants.

**Occupant Obligations**: Occupants must maintain the interior of their dwelling units in a clean and sanitary condition, use utilities and facilities in a proper manner, properly dispose of garbage and refuse, not willfully damage the dwelling or building systems, and report serious maintenance issues to the owner in a timely manner.

## Subchapter 2: Maintenance Requirements (§27-2011 through §27-2044)

### Article 1: Structural Maintenance

#### §27-2011: Maintenance of Foundations, Walls, and Roofs

Building owners must maintain all structural elements in good repair and safe condition. This includes:

- **Foundations**: Must be structurally sound with no major cracks, settling, or deterioration that compromises building stability
- **Walls**: Exterior walls must be weather-tight and free from major cracks, holes, or deterioration; interior walls must be maintained to prevent water intrusion and structural damage
- **Roofs**: Must be maintained to prevent leaks, must be properly drained, and must be structurally sound; all roof penetrations must be properly sealed

The obligation to maintain structural elements in good repair is a fundamental owner responsibility that cannot be shifted to occupants.

#### §27-2012: Drainage and Dampness Prevention

Owners must ensure that buildings are designed and maintained to prevent water intrusion, dampness, and moisture damage. Specific requirements include:

- Ground water must be properly diverted away from the foundation
- Basement or cellar areas must be maintained dry through adequate drainage
- All roof and gutter systems must properly direct water away from the structure
- Window and door frames must be maintained to prevent water infiltration
- Walls and ceilings must be free from water stains, mold, and dampness
- Interior surfaces must be maintained to prevent condensation and moisture accumulation

Failure to prevent dampness can result in mold growth, structural deterioration, and health hazards for occupants.

#### §27-2013: Cleanliness of Premises

Owners must keep all premises, including common areas, yards, and accessways, clean and free from refuse, garbage, and debris. This includes:

- Regular removal of trash and refuse from common areas
- Maintenance of yards and exterior spaces free from accumulated debris
- Proper storage and containment of garbage and waste
- Removal of snow, ice, and hazards from walkways and stairs
- Cleaning of public hallways, lobbies, and common areas

### Article 2: Painting

#### §27-2013: Interior Painting Requirements

In multiple dwellings (three or more units), owners are required to paint or cover interior walls and ceilings with protective coating. Painting requirements include:

**Frequency**: Painting or protective covering must be applied at least once every three years in multiple dwellings. The three-year cycle is measured from the date of the last application of paint or protective coating.

**Coverage**: All interior walls and ceilings of habitable rooms, kitchens, bathrooms, and common areas must be included in the painting cycle.

**Condition Standard**: Paint and protective coatings must be maintained in good condition, free from:
- Peeling or chipping paint
- Lead-based paint hazards (particularly in buildings pre-dating 1978)
- Mold or mildew growth
- Water stains or discoloration that indicate structural problems

**Violations**: Failure to paint interior surfaces according to the three-year requirement is classified as either a Class A violation (non-hazardous, minor paint condition issues) or a Class B violation (hazardous condition, such as lead-based paint hazard or extensive deterioration).

### Article 3: Pest Control

#### §27-2018: Rodent, Roach, and Vermin Control

Owners bear primary responsibility for controlling pests. The regulation requires owners to:

- Keep all premises free from rodents (rats, mice, and other rodents)
- Maintain freedom from cockroaches and other roaches
- Prevent infestations of bedbugs, fleas, and other vermin
- Seal all holes, gaps, and openings that allow pest entry
- Maintain garbage storage and disposal practices that do not attract pests
- Apply pest control measures promptly when infestations are discovered

Owners are obligated to address pest infestations even if the infestation originated from adjacent properties. The presence of visible pests or evidence of pest activity (droppings, dead insects, nesting materials) constitutes a violation.

#### §27-2018.1: Bedbug Notification and Disclosure Requirements

The Housing Maintenance Code includes specific provisions for bedbug management and tenant notification:

**Disclosure Obligations**: Owners of multiple dwellings must disclose bedbug infestation history to prospective tenants before executing a lease. This includes information about bedbug infestations within the past year.

**Notification Requirements**: When bedbugs are discovered in a dwelling unit or common area, the owner must:
- Provide written notice to all affected and adjacent dwelling unit occupants
- Inform occupants of treatment plans and timelines
- Cooperate with occupants in preparing units for treatment
- Ensure professional pest control treatment is applied

**Occupant Cooperation**: While owners bear primary responsibility for bedbug control, occupants must cooperate with treatment efforts, including providing access to units and preparing spaces for professional treatment.

### Article 4: Heat and Hot Water

#### §27-2028: Heat Requirements

Heat is considered an essential service in New York City. Owner obligations for providing adequate heat are strictly enforced:

**Heating Season**: October 1 through May 31 each year

**Daytime Requirements** (6:00 AM to 10:00 PM): When the outdoor temperature falls below 55 degrees Fahrenheit, the owner must maintain an interior temperature of at least 68 degrees Fahrenheit in all habitable rooms, public halls, and other occupied spaces.

**Nighttime Requirements** (10:00 PM to 6:00 AM): Regardless of the outdoor temperature, the owner must maintain an interior temperature of at least 62 degrees Fahrenheit in all habitable rooms, public halls, and other occupied spaces.

**Temperature Measurement**: Temperatures are measured at a point three feet above floor level, away from external walls and heat sources, in a location representative of the room's typical occupancy.

**Enforcement**: Failure to provide adequate heat is classified as a Class C (Immediately Hazardous) violation. HPD is authorized to order emergency heat installation, arrange emergency fuel delivery, or order emergency repairs. Owners are liable for the cost of emergency repairs, with penalties applied on top of repair costs. No cure period is provided for heat violations—immediate compliance is required.

**Landlord Liability**: Owners cannot shift responsibility for heat provision to tenants, even in single-family or two-family homes where tenants might control thermostats. The owner remains responsible for maintaining functioning heating systems adequate to meet Code requirements.

#### §27-2031: Hot Water Requirements

Hot water is an essential service provided by owners at all times:

**Temperature Standard**: Hot water must be supplied and maintained at a minimum temperature of 120 degrees Fahrenheit at the point of delivery (the tap).

**Year-Round Requirement**: Hot water supply is required 24 hours a day, seven days a week, throughout the entire year. There is no seasonal limitation for hot water as there is for heat.

**Enforcement**: Failure to provide hot water meeting these standards is classified as a Class C (Immediately Hazardous) violation. Occupants experiencing no hot water, insufficient hot water temperature, or hot water interruption exceeding four hours can report violations, and HPD will order immediate remediation.

**System Maintenance**: Owners must maintain all hot water heating and distribution systems in proper working order, including boilers, pipes, valves, and fixtures. Regular maintenance is essential to prevent scaling, corrosion, and system failure.

### Article 5: Water Supply

#### §27-2032: Adequate Supply of Pure Water

Owners must ensure that all dwelling units and buildings have access to an adequate supply of pure water suitable for drinking, cooking, and sanitary purposes:

- Water must be supplied continuously at adequate pressure for normal use
- Water must be potable and meet all applicable public health standards
- Water storage tanks must be maintained clean and free from contamination
- All water distribution pipes must be maintained in good repair with no leaks
- Water shut-off valves must be accessible and functional

Insufficient water pressure, discolored water, or contaminated water supplies constitute violations of the Housing Maintenance Code.

### Article 6: Elevators

#### §27-2035: Elevator Service Requirements

In buildings equipped with passenger elevators, owners must:

- Maintain all elevators in safe and proper working order
- Ensure elevators are certified and inspected according to Department of Buildings requirements
- Provide adequate service to ensure elevators remain operational throughout the day
- Maintain emergency communication systems within elevators
- Display current inspection certificates and emergency information
- Provide backup power to at least one elevator in multi-elevator buildings

Lack of elevator service in buildings designed with elevators as a primary means of vertical circulation may affect habitability determinations and represents a violation of Code requirements.

### Article 7: Lighting and Ventilation

#### §27-2038: Natural Light Requirements for Habitable Rooms

Habitable rooms, including bedrooms and living areas, must receive natural light. Minimum standards include:

- At least one window opening to the outdoor air
- Window area equal to at least ten percent of the floor area of the room
- Windows must not be obstructed by bars, grilles, or other permanent fixtures that significantly reduce light transmission
- Skylights may supplement or substitute for windows in certain circumstances

Rooms that do not meet natural light requirements cannot be classified as habitable rooms.

#### §27-2039: Ventilation Requirements

All habitable rooms, kitchens, and bathrooms must have proper ventilation:

- **Habitable Rooms**: Must be ventilated by windows or other means to provide adequate air exchange
- **Kitchens**: Must have ventilation capable of removing cooking odors and moisture; either windows or mechanical ventilation (range hoods venting to the outdoors) is required
- **Bathrooms**: Must have ventilation capability through either windows or mechanical exhaust fans venting to the outdoors

Ventilation systems must be maintained in operational condition and must actually function to remove moisture and odors.

#### §27-2040: Common Area Lighting

Owners must provide adequate lighting in all common areas, including:

- Public hallways and corridors
- Stairways and stairs
- Lobbies and entrances
- Basements and mechanical rooms
- Parking areas and garages

Lighting must be adequate to safely navigate these spaces, and light fixtures must be maintained in working order with bulbs replaced promptly.

### Article 8: Fire Protection

#### §27-2041: Smoke Detectors

Smoke detectors are required in all dwelling units:

- At least one smoke detector must be installed in each dwelling unit
- Smoke detectors must be installed on the ceiling or upper wall areas where smoke from fires would first accumulate
- Detectors must be interconnected with hard-wiring (in new construction) where feasible, though battery-operated detectors are acceptable in many circumstances
- Owners must maintain detectors in proper working condition with functional batteries
- Detectors must be replaced according to manufacturer specifications (typically every five to ten years)

Lack of functional smoke detectors in a dwelling unit is classified as a Class B (Hazardous) violation.

#### §27-2043: Carbon Monoxide Detectors

Carbon monoxide detectors are required in dwelling units with fuel-burning appliances, fireplaces, or attached garages:

- At least one carbon monoxide detector must be installed in each affected dwelling unit
- Detectors must be installed and maintained in proper working order
- Detectors must comply with UL (Underwriters Laboratories) standards
- Batteries must be functional and replaced according to manufacturer specifications

Carbon monoxide detector violations are classified as Class B (Hazardous) violations.

#### §27-2044: Self-Closing Doors on Interior Corridors

In multiple dwelling buildings, all doors opening from dwelling units to interior corridors must:

- Be self-closing doors that automatically close after opening
- Remain closed when not in use to contain smoke and fire within individual units
- Be maintained in working condition with functional closing devices

This requirement promotes fire safety by compartmentalizing units and preventing smoke and fire spread through common corridors.

## Subchapter 3: Minimum Standards for Habitability (§27-2045 through §27-2068)

### §27-2045: Occupancy of Cellars and Basements

Cellars and basements have stringent occupancy requirements under the Housing Maintenance Code:

**Cellar Definition**: A cellar is a story of a building the floor of which is below the average street grade.

**Basement Definition**: A basement is a story below the cellar level or if there is no cellar, below the average street grade.

**Cellar Occupancy Restrictions**: A cellar, even with modifications, cannot be used as a dwelling unit or habitable room. Cellars may only be used for storage, mechanical systems, or similar non-residential purposes.

**Basement Occupancy**: A basement can be converted into a dwelling unit only if it meets specific requirements including:
- The floor level is at least 2 feet above the highest grade of the adjoining ground
- Natural light and ventilation requirements are met through windows or other means
- Drainage and moisture control systems prevent dampness
- All other habitability requirements are satisfied

Cellar spaces occupied as bedrooms or living spaces in violation of these provisions represent serious code violations that make housing uninhabitable.

### §27-2046: Minimum Room Sizes

The Housing Maintenance Code establishes minimum room dimensions for habitability:

**Habitable Rooms**: Any room used for living, sleeping, cooking, or dining must have:
- A minimum floor area of at least 70 square feet
- A minimum width of 7 feet in any direction
- A ceiling height of at least 8 feet measured from the floor to the ceiling

**Kitchens**: Kitchen areas must have:
- A minimum floor area of at least 50 square feet
- Adequate counter space and cabinet storage
- Space for a dining table or eating area (if the kitchen serves as the dining room)

**Bathrooms**: Bathrooms do not have specific minimum square footage requirements but must be adequately sized to accommodate all required fixtures (toilet, sink, shower/bathtub) without creating hazardous or unsanitary conditions.

**Measurement Standards**: Room sizes are measured as the usable floor area, excluding closets, wall thickness, and permanent fixtures.

### §27-2047: Window Requirements

Windows in habitable spaces must meet specific standards:

- **Number and Location**: At least one window opening to the outdoor air is required for each habitable room
- **Size**: Each window must have an area of at least ten percent of the floor area of the room (at least 10 square feet for a 100 square foot room)
- **Operable**: Windows must be openable to allow fresh air circulation
- **Condition**: Windows must be maintained in good repair with functioning locks and operational opening mechanisms
- **Location**: Windows must be positioned to provide adequate light distribution throughout the room

Rooms with only interior windows (windows facing other interior rooms) do not satisfy the natural light and ventilation requirements of the Housing Maintenance Code.

### §27-2048: Sanitary Facilities

Each dwelling unit must be equipped with specific sanitary facilities:

**Toilet**: A water closet (toilet) is required in each dwelling unit, preferably within the unit itself. In exceptional circumstances in pre-existing multiple dwellings, a shared toilet in the building may be acceptable, but this is increasingly restricted.

**Bathing Facilities**: Each dwelling unit must have at least one bathtub or shower in good working order, with adequate hot and cold water supply.

**Sink**: A sink with hot and cold running water must be provided in the kitchen and in the bathroom. Additional sinks in other areas (laundry rooms, utility areas) may be required in larger units.

**Maintenance**: All sanitary fixtures must be maintained in proper working order, free from leaks, corrosion, or damage that prevents normal use.

Dwelling units lacking any required sanitary facilities are uninhabitable and cannot be legally occupied.

### §27-2049: Kitchen Facilities

Each dwelling unit must have a kitchen or kitchen area equipped with:

- A stove or cooking appliance (electric or gas range) in proper working order
- An oven (which may be part of a range) capable of reaching cooking temperatures
- Adequate counter space for food preparation
- Adequate cabinet or shelf storage for food and cooking equipment
- Sink with hot and cold running water

Pre-packaged cooking units or hot plates are not sufficient substitutes for a functional stove and oven.

### §27-2050: Electricity Required in All Habitable Rooms

All habitable rooms must be supplied with electricity:

- At least one electrical outlet per room (with multiple outlets in larger rooms)
- Adequate lighting fixtures or provision for connecting lighting
- Electrical system must be safe and in compliance with electrical codes
- Exposed wiring or dangerous electrical conditions constitute hazards
- Electrical service must be adequate to support normal residential use

Absence of electricity or severely inadequate electrical service makes housing uninhabitable.

### §27-2051: Access Requirements

All dwelling units must have:

- A primary entrance accessible from a public street or from a common area of the building
- Entrance doors that are operable and secure
- Safe pathways and hallways to reach the unit entrance
- Emergency exits if required by Building Code
- Access to all utility shut-offs and safety devices

Units in basements or cellars must have access to exits on at least two different levels or have alternative emergency exit routes.

## Subchapter 4: Violations and Penalties

### Violation Classification System

The Housing Maintenance Code uses a three-tier violation classification system that determines cure periods, penalties, and enforcement urgency:

#### Class A Violations (Non-Hazardous)

Class A violations are minor maintenance issues that do not pose a threat to health or safety.

**Definition**: Violations that are not hazardous to health or safety and do not affect the essential function of a building or dwelling unit.

**Examples**:
- Missing or incomplete peephole or door intercom
- Minor interior paint condition issues (not involving lead-based paint hazards)
- Broken or missing closet shelves
- Missing mailbox or mailbox damage
- Excessive graffiti in common areas
- Broken or damaged window shade or blind
- Missing towel rack or toilet paper holder
- Door closer or automatic door failure (non-fire related)

**Cure Period**: 90 days from the date the violation is issued

**Penalties**: After the cure period expires, civil penalties up to $50 per day of continued violation can be assessed. Penalties accumulate daily until the condition is corrected.

**Issuance**: Class A violations can only be issued after an inspection by HPD that confirms the violation condition.

#### Class B Violations (Hazardous)

Class B violations are significant maintenance or safety issues that could cause harm to health or safety but do not represent an immediate emergency.

**Definition**: Violations that are hazardous to health or safety or materially affect the essential functions of a dwelling unit or building.

**Examples**:
- Absence or non-operation of a required smoke detector
- Absence or non-operation of a required carbon monoxide detector
- Rodent infestation (droppings, gnaw marks, or visible rodents present)
- Cockroach infestation (visible cockroaches or extensive evidence of infestation)
- Bedbug infestation
- Broken or missing lock on entrance door
- Broken or cracked window glass that cannot be safely replaced
- Water leak or staining indicating active water intrusion
- Mold or mildew growth indicating moisture problems
- Broken mailbox providing inadequate security
- Bathroom exhaust fan or kitchen range hood not venting properly
- Elevator out of service in a building designed with elevator access
- Interior stairway with missing handrail
- Unsafe electrical outlet or exposed wiring
- Lack of adequate heat (if temperature readings are below required standards but above immediate danger levels)
- Lack of hot water (if water is below 120°F but still warm)

**Cure Period**: 30 days from the date the violation is issued

**Penalties**: After the 30-day cure period expires, civil penalties ranging from $25 to $100 per day of continued violation can be assessed. Penalties accumulate daily until corrected.

**Enforcement**: Class B violations can be issued both after inspections and based on credible evidence (such as photographs or witness testimony) without always requiring a direct HPD inspection, though inspections are typical.

#### Class C Violations (Immediately Hazardous)

Class C violations are emergency conditions that create an immediate threat to life, health, or safety. Class C violations must be corrected as rapidly as possible, often immediately.

**Definition**: Violations that are immediately dangerous to health or safety, creating a hazardous or uninhabitable condition.

**Examples**:
- Lack of heat during heating season (temperatures below required standards during the heating season)
- Lack of hot water for an extended period (no hot water or temperatures below 120°F)
- Lack of running water (no water supply or severely inadequate water pressure)
- Lead-based paint hazard (deteriorated lead paint in a unit with children under age 6)
- No electricity or severely inadequate electrical service
- Broken fire escape that creates a safety hazard
- Exposed live electrical wiring creating electrocution hazard
- Gas leak or carbon monoxide presence
- Structural failure or building collapse risk
- Ceiling or wall collapse
- Severe water intrusion creating flooding
- Unsecured windows or broken glass creating fall hazard
- Elevator malfunction with persons trapped
- Contaminated water supply
- Severe pest infestation creating health hazard
- Dangerous stairway condition creating fall risk
- Bathroom or kitchen fixtures creating waste backup or unsanitary conditions
- Blocked emergency exits
- Missing or inoperable self-closing doors on interior corridor doors (fire hazard)

**Cure Period**: 24 hours from violation issuance, or less if an imminent danger exists

**Immediate Repairs**: For truly dangerous conditions (structural failure, gas leak, no heat in winter), HPD may order immediate remediation without a cure period.

**Penalties**: After the 24-hour cure period (if one was given), civil penalties ranging from $50 to $150 per day can be assessed. Penalties escalate quickly for Class C violations.

**Emergency Repair Program**: For Class C violations, HPD can exercise its Emergency Repair Program authority to send contractors to repair the condition and bill the owner for all repair costs, plus additional charges and penalties. Owners have no opportunity to make repairs themselves.

**Enforcement**: Class C violations can be issued immediately upon discovery without a formal inspection in emergency situations.

### Repeat Violations and Enhanced Penalties

**Repeat Violation Definition**: A violation of the same provision or related provisions that was previously issued to the same owner at the same building and has not been corrected within the cure period.

**Enhanced Penalties**: Repeat violations can result in substantially higher penalties:
- Repeat Class A violations: Penalties can be doubled or tripled
- Repeat Class B violations: Penalties can be doubled or tripled
- Repeat Class C violations: Penalties can double, with maximum penalties increased

**Repeat Offender Status**: Owners who accumulate multiple violations at the same building or across multiple buildings can be designated as repeat offenders, subjecting them to:
- Enhanced penalties
- More frequent inspections
- Faster HPD response to complaints
- Potential court action
- Building closure in extreme cases

### Calculation of Penalties

**Daily Accrual**: Penalties accrue on a daily basis from the day after the cure period expires until the condition is corrected. For example:
- Class A violation issued January 1 with a 90-day cure period: If not corrected by April 1, a $50/day penalty begins accruing from April 2 onward
- Class B violation issued January 1 with a 30-day cure period: If not corrected by January 31, a $25-$100/day penalty begins accruing from February 1 onward

**Multiple Violations**: When multiple violations exist at the same property, penalties are calculated separately for each violation and can accumulate rapidly.

**Penalty Reduction**: Penalties are reduced or eliminated if the owner corrects the violation within the cure period, but penalties accrue immediately upon expiration of the cure period if the violation persists.

### HPD Enforcement Process

#### Complaint and Inspection Initiation

1. **Complaint Filing**: Complaints can be filed with HPD through multiple channels:
   - 311 (NYC's complaint system) - available by phone, online, or mobile app
   - HPD's online complaint portal
   - Mail or in-person at HPD offices
   - Tenant organizations or community groups (sometimes)

2. **Complaint Details**: Complaints typically include:
   - Property address
   - Nature of the violation
   - Specific room or location where the problem exists
   - Date the problem was first noticed
   - Contact information for the complainant

3. **Inspection Scheduling**: HPD schedules an inspection at the property. The inspection scheduling process includes:
   - Notification to the owner (except in emergency situations where access is needed without notice)
   - Assignment of an HPD inspector
   - Attempted access to the property at a scheduled time

#### Inspection and Violation Issuance

1. **HPD Inspection**: An HPD inspector visits the property to:
   - Verify the reported conditions
   - Photograph violations and document conditions
   - Measure temperatures, water pressure, and other objective standards
   - Interview occupants about conditions and maintenance history
   - Check for multiple related violations

2. **Violation Documentation**: The inspector documents:
   - Specific violation code sections violated
   - Detailed description of the condition
   - Photographs or measurements proving the violation
   - Date of inspection
   - Recommended cure timeline

3. **Violation Issuance**: Violations are formally issued to the property owner at the following address:
   - The address on file with HPD (owner's registered address)
   - By certified mail
   - Property address (sometimes)

#### Cure Period and Remediation

1. **Cure Period Clock Starts**: The cure period begins on the date the violation is formally issued:
   - Class A: 90 days
   - Class B: 30 days
   - Class C: 24 hours (or immediately if truly emergent)

2. **Owner Remediation**: During the cure period, the owner can:
   - Hire contractors to fix the problem
   - Order replacement parts or materials
   - Coordinate access with occupants
   - Submit photos or completion certificates to HPD if proof of correction is available

3. **Proof of Correction**: Owners can submit evidence to HPD showing that the violation has been corrected, including:
   - Completion photographs
   - Contractor invoices or completion certificates
   - Occupant sign-offs
   - Records from HPD's own inspection

#### Re-Inspection and Penalty Assessment

1. **Compliance Re-Inspection**: After the cure period expires, HPD will typically schedule a follow-up inspection to verify that the violation has been corrected:
   - If corrected: The violation may be marked as dismissed or corrected
   - If not corrected: A penalty notice is issued

2. **Penalty Notice**: If the violation has not been corrected after the cure period, HPD issues a penalty notice indicating:
   - Amount of daily penalty
   - Start date for penalty accrual
   - Total penalties to date (if reassessments have occurred)
   - Payment instructions

3. **Payment and Dispute Process**: Owners have options:
   - Pay the penalties assessed
   - Request a hearing to contest the violation
   - Correct the violation (penalties stop accruing if corrected)
   - Negotiate a payment plan or settlement with HPD

#### Escalated Enforcement and Emergency Repairs

1. **Emergency Repair Program**: For Class C violations or persistent Class B violations, HPD can:
   - Hire contractors to make emergency repairs
   - Charge the owner for all costs plus a surcharge
   - Place a lien on the property if costs are not paid
   - Report the charges to credit agencies

2. **Court Action**: For serious or persistent violations, HPD can:
   - File civil action against the owner seeking penalties and injunctive relief
   - Seek court orders requiring specific repairs
   - Pursue criminal charges in extreme cases

3. **Building Closure**: In extreme cases with multiple serious violations, HPD can:
   - Issue an Order to Vacate requiring all occupants to leave
   - Close the building pending corrections
   - Pursue severe penalties or building seizure

## Subchapter 5: Harassment

### Definition of Owner Harassment

New York City law specifically prohibits owner harassment of tenants. The Housing Maintenance Code defines harassment in §27-2004(a)(48):

**Harassment** includes any act by an owner or agent intended to cause a tenant to vacate a dwelling unit or to discourage a tenant from asserting rights under the Housing Maintenance Code or other housing laws.

### Forms of Prohibited Harassment

**Essential Services Deprivation**: Deliberately failing to provide essential services, including:
- Heat (in violation of §27-2028)
- Hot water (in violation of §27-2031)
- Electricity
- Water
- Elevator service (in buildings with elevators)

**Frivolous Legal Action**: Repeatedly filing court cases (evictions, housing court proceedings) that are without merit or are intended to harass rather than collect rent or enforce legitimate rights.

**Interference with Tenancy**: Actions intended to make continued occupancy unpleasant or impossible, such as:
- Frequent non-emergency police calls to the unit
- Entering the unit without proper legal notice or access
- Interfering with occupant's peaceful enjoyment of the dwelling
- Excessive or frivolous lease violations

**Constructive Eviction**: Creating conditions so hazardous or uninhabitable (through violating the Housing Maintenance Code) that occupants are forced to vacate. Multiple serious Code violations maintained over time can constitute harassment through constructive eviction.

### Penalties for Harassment

**Enhanced Civil Penalties**: Violations motivated by intent to harass result in significantly increased penalties:
- Class B violations involving harassment: Penalties can be multiplied two to five times
- Enhanced damages for breach of the warranty of habitability
- Treble damages (three times the actual damages) in some cases

**Tenant Remedies**: Tenants subjected to harassment can:
- Reduce rent or withhold rent to escrow pending correction of Code violations
- Sue the owner for breach of the implied warranty of habitability
- Recover actual damages, enhanced damages, and attorney's fees
- Receive orders requiring the owner to correct violations

**Criminal Penalties**: Harassment can also result in criminal charges under Penal Law §235.25, including fines and imprisonment.

## Key Cross-References and Related Regulations

### Multiple Dwelling Law (MDL)

The Multiple Dwelling Law (New York State Real Property Law Article 2-B) applies to buildings with three or more units and provides additional requirements beyond the Housing Maintenance Code, including:

- **Occupancy Requirements**: Standards for room sizes, light, and ventilation
- **Safety Requirements**: Fire escapes, railings, and stairway standards
- **Registration**: Multiple dwellings must be registered with the Department of Buildings
- **Certificates of Occupancy**: Buildings must maintain valid Certificates of Occupancy

### NYC Administrative Code §28-118: Certificate of Occupancy Requirements

All buildings must maintain a valid Certificate of Occupancy (C of O) issued by the Department of Buildings. The Certificate of Occupancy certifies that:
- The building meets all applicable codes and safety standards
- The building is suitable for its intended occupancy
- All required safety systems and features are in place

A building without a valid Certificate of Occupancy is not legally available for residential occupancy, and violations can result in the building being ordered closed.

### Local Law 1/2004 and Local Law 31/2020: Lead-Based Paint Hazard Requirements

These local laws specifically address lead-based paint hazards, a particular focus of the Housing Maintenance Code:

**Pre-1978 Buildings**: All buildings constructed before 1978 are presumed to contain lead-based paint. The law requires:

- **Lead Paint Disclosure**: Property owners must disclose known lead-based paint hazards to buyers and tenants
- **Inspection and Risk Assessment**: In buildings with children under age 6, lead paint and dust lead hazards must be remediated
- **Lead Safe Work Practices**: Any paint disturbance must use lead-safe work practices to prevent lead dust contamination
- **Notification**: Tenants must be notified of lead hazard remediation activities

**Enforcement**: Lead-based paint violations are typically classified as Class C (Immediately Hazardous) violations if deteriorated lead paint exists in a unit with a child under age 6.

### Local Law 152/2016: Gas Piping Inspection Requirements

This law requires inspection and certification of natural gas piping systems in multiple dwellings:

- **Initial Inspections**: All buildings with gas service must have piping inspected and certified for safety
- **Periodic Recertification**: Gas piping systems must be recertified at required intervals
- **Safety Standards**: Piping must meet all applicable safety codes and standards
- **Inspector Certification**: Inspectors must be certified by the Department of Environmental Protection

## Implied Warranty of Habitability

Beyond the specific requirements of the Housing Maintenance Code, New York law recognizes an implied warranty of habitability in all residential leases:

**Elements of Habitability**: A dwelling unit must be:
- Structurally sound
- Free from serious defects
- Suitable for human occupancy
- Meeting minimum code standards for heat, hot water, sanitation, and other essential services

**Tenant Remedies**: If an owner fails to meet habitability standards, tenants can:
- Repair and deduct costs from rent (up to one month's rent in some cases)
- Abate (reduce) rent in proportion to the diminished value of the unit
- Abandon the unit and terminate the lease
- Sue for actual damages and enhanced damages

**Owner Duties**: The implied warranty cannot be waived or eliminated by lease provisions. Even if a lease says the tenant accepts the unit "as is," the owner remains bound to provide habitable conditions.

## Conclusion

The NYC Housing Maintenance Code represents a comprehensive regulatory framework designed to ensure that all residential housing in New York City meets minimum standards for safety, health, and habitability. Through the three-tier violation classification system, enforcement authority vested in HPD, and provisions for emergency repairs and penalties, the Code provides tools for ensuring compliance and protecting occupants.

Property owners bear primary responsibility for maintaining their buildings in compliance with the Code. The breadth of owner obligations—from structural maintenance and pest control to providing heat, hot water, and essential services—reflects the principle that housing is a fundamental need and that owners, not tenants, bear ultimate responsibility for maintaining habitable conditions.

Tenants and occupants are protected by the Code's enforcement mechanisms, the implicit warranty of habitability, and specific provisions prohibiting harassment. Together, these protections create a system where occupants can report violations, receive HPD inspections, and see violations remedied through either voluntary compliance or through HPD's enforcement and emergency repair authority.

Understanding the Housing Maintenance Code is essential for property owners seeking to remain compliant, for tenants seeking to understand their rights, and for housing advocates and professionals working to improve housing conditions throughout New York City.

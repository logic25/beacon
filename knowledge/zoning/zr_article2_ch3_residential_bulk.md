# Article II, Chapter 3: Residential Bulk Regulations

## 23-00 APPLICABILITY AND GENERAL PURPOSES

### 23-01 Applicability of Bulk Regulations

Bulk regulations in this chapter apply to any zoning lot in Residence Districts where residential buildings are proposed, constructed, or maintained. These regulations establish requirements for lot configuration, building massing, open space, and related development parameters that govern the physical form of residential developments throughout New York City's residential zoning districts.

### 23-02 Applicability in Special Situations

Bulk regulations are applied with modifications in special zoning situations including:

- Zoning lots that straddle district boundaries, where regulations must be prorated or applied based on the dominant district or lot area allocation
- Sites within special districts or overlays that may have modified bulk requirements
- Qualifying residential sites that meet specific size, configuration, or circumstance criteria
- Zoning lots with mixed use developments containing both residential and non-residential components

### 23-03 General Purposes

The bulk regulations in this chapter are established to:

- Ensure adequate light, air, and open space for residents in all zoning districts
- Maintain neighborhood character and scale appropriate to each residential district's designation
- Prevent excessive building massing and overcrowding of zoning lots
- Establish proportional relationships between building height, lot size, and setbacks
- Create varied and articulated streetscapes through height and setback requirements
- Preserve views to sky, water bodies, and significant landscape features
- Facilitate adequate parking and vehicle circulation in residential areas
- Ensure appropriate transitions between districts of different density and intensity
- Support community facility integration within residential neighborhoods
- Enable innovative housing types and affordability programs through incentive provisions

### 23-04 Lots Divided by District Boundaries

Where a zoning lot is divided by a district boundary, bulk regulations shall be applied as follows:

- The portion of the zoning lot in each district must comply with the bulk regulations of that specific district
- Where practical, the lot may be reconfigured so that the entire lot is treated within a single district's regulations
- Floor area and density calculations must be computed separately for each district portion unless a merger or consolidation is approved
- Height and setback regulations shall be applied distinctly to the portion within each district, unless the design demonstrates that unified treatment creates superior urban design outcomes

### 23-05 Applicability of Certain Bulk Regulations

Certain bulk regulations have limited applicability in specific situations:

- Yard regulations may be modified for through-lots or interior lots not fronting primary streets
- Height regulations may be adjusted through applicable bonuses for affordable housing, quality housing, or public facility access
- Floor area calculations may include certain bonus provisions for environmental sustainability, public plazas, or cultural institutions
- Setback requirements may be waived for contextual districts seeking to maintain uniform street walls

### 23-06 Applicability for Certain Sites (Qualifying Residential Sites)

Qualifying residential sites that meet specific criteria may have modified bulk regulations. A qualifying residential site is generally defined as a zoning lot that:

- Contains existing residential structures or has been previously developed for residential use
- Meets minimum area requirements for the district
- Complies with a designated character study or contextual framework
- Proposes residential development consistent with district intent
- Meets other criteria established through special district regulations or community planning processes

For qualifying sites, certain height, setback, and floor area regulations may be adjusted to achieve better urban design, preserve streetscape character, or facilitate transit-oriented development.

## 23-10 LOT AREA AND LOT WIDTH REGULATIONS

### 23-11 Lot Area and Lot Width - R1 through R5 Districts

Minimum lot area and lot width requirements for low-density residential districts are established to ensure adequate open space, parking, and light and air for single-family and limited multi-family residential buildings.

**R1 District:** Minimum lot area of 1,200 square feet with minimum width of 20 feet. These requirements reflect the single-family residential character of R1 districts, typically found in outer-borough neighborhoods.

**R2 District:** Minimum lot area of 1,200 square feet with minimum width of 20 feet. R2 allows somewhat denser residential development while maintaining open character.

**R3 District:** Minimum lot area of 1,000 square feet with minimum width of 20 feet. This district permits moderate-density residential development appropriate for infill and neighborhood reinforcement.

**R4 District:** Minimum lot area of 1,200 square feet with minimum width of 25 feet. R4 provides for garden apartment and low-rise residential buildings.

**R5 District:** Minimum lot area of 1,200 square feet with minimum width of 25 feet. R5 accommodates modest mid-rise residential development.

Variations to these requirements may be permitted through:

- Zoning lot mergers consolidating multiple substandard lots into compliant configurations
- Modification through variance procedures for unusual lot configurations
- Special district provisions establishing different requirements for specific areas
- Qualification under the City Environmental Quality Review process

### 23-12 Lot Area and Lot Width - R6 through R12 Districts

Medium and high-density residential districts establish modified lot area and width requirements reflecting their greater building intensity and mixed-use character.

**R6 District:** Minimum lot area of 1,000 square feet. This is the threshold for medium-density residential development.

**R7 through R10 Districts:** Minimum lot areas generally range from 1,000 to 2,500 square feet depending on the specific district designation and whether the site qualifies under quality housing or other programs.

**R11-R12 Districts:** Minimum lot area of 1,000 square feet, with variations based on whether the site qualifies for bonus programs.

For R6-R12 districts, lot width is less prescriptive, as these districts accommodate buildings with more complex configurations and multiple street frontages. Width requirements are typically 50 feet or greater where a building must access primary streets, but interior mid-block sites may have more flexible arrangements.

Special provisions allow:

- Through-lots and complex configurations in dense urban locations
- Multiple buildings on unified zoning lots
- Shared courtyard or plaza arrangements within large sites
- Lot consolidation and strategic mergers to achieve optimized massing

## 23-20 FLOOR AREA RATIO REGULATIONS

### 23-21 Floor Area Ratio - R1 through R5 Districts

Floor Area Ratio (FAR) regulations establish the maximum ratio of total building floor area to zoning lot area, controlling building massing and density in residential districts.

**R1 District:** Basic FAR of 0.5. This creates the lowest density residential districts, typically supporting single-family homes and duplexes with significant open land.

**R2 District:** Basic FAR of 0.5, maintaining single-family or limited duplex character.

**R3 District:** Basic FAR of 0.5 to 0.6 depending on lot area. Smaller lots may achieve 0.6 FAR to accommodate efficient infill development.

**R4 District:** Basic FAR of 0.75. This district enables small apartment buildings and garden apartments while maintaining substantial open space.

**R5 District:** Basic FAR of 1.25. R5 accommodates more substantial residential buildings of 12-15 stories in appropriate locations, typically in mixed-use neighborhoods with transit access.

FAR is calculated as total floor area of all buildings on the zoning lot divided by the lot area. Certain floor areas may be excluded from FAR calculation, including:

- Cellar floor area (partially, generally 25% of lot area)
- Certain mechanical and accessory spaces
- Landscaped courtyard areas meeting specific standards
- Public plaza area where incentive bonuses are granted

### 23-22 Floor Area Ratio - R6 through R12 Districts

Higher-density residential districts establish significantly elevated FAR limits permitting substantial building bulk and mixed-use development.

**R6 District:** Basic FAR of 2.43. This represents a substantial increase from R5, enabling denser mid-rise residential development typical of inner-borough neighborhoods.

**R7 District:** Basic FAR of 3.44. R7 accommodates robust mixed-use neighborhoods with substantial residential buildings, ground-floor commercial uses, and significant massing.

**R8 District:** Basic FAR of 6.02. This district supports high-rise residential development with extensive building footprints and floor plates.

**R9 District:** Basic FAR of 7.52. R9 permits very high-density development typical of central business district residential neighborhoods and major transit-oriented areas.

**R10 District:** Basic FAR of 10.0. This is the highest base FAR for residential districts, supporting the most intensive residential development including supertall residential towers.

**R11-R12 Districts:** FARs are established on a case-by-case basis through special district regulations, but generally range from 9.0 to 12.0 FAR reflecting their specialized character.

FAR bonuses may be available in R6-R12 districts for:

- Affordable housing inclusion (up to 20% additional FAR)
- Quality housing development (FAR bonus combined with special height/setback modifications)
- Waterfront or public access provisions
- Historic preservation and community facility integration

### 23-23 Permitted Floor Area in R1-R5 Districts

Within R1-R5 districts, certain floor areas may be built in excess of the basic FAR through special provisions and incentive programs.

**Cottage Envelope Provisions (R1-R4):** Buildings may be developed within a defined "cottage envelope" that permits height and floor area variations while maintaining neighborhood character. The envelope includes:

- Maximum perimeter wall height of 21 feet for the principal building
- Maximum total building height of 25-35 feet depending on roof configuration
- Floor area inclusion at special rates to accommodate efficient residential design
- Side and rear yard requirements that maintain separation between buildings

**Inclusionary Housing Bonus (R1-R5):** Where affordable dwelling units are included at specified percentages, additional floor area bonuses apply:

- Moderate income housing provision: up to 20% FAR bonus
- Low-income housing provision: up to 35% FAR bonus
- Mixed-income buildings with specified proportions of affordable units: graduated bonuses

**Quality Housing Alternative (R3-R5):** In certain designated areas, developers may opt into the Quality Housing program, which permits:

- Modified height and setback regulations providing greater flexibility
- Floor area calculations that may include certain bonus components
- Reduced parking requirements in transit-accessible locations
- Modified open space and courtyard requirements reflecting contemporary standards

**Accessory Parking and Community Facility Floor Areas:** Certain floor areas are calculated at reduced rates:

- Parking garage floor area at 0.3 FAR rate
- Community facility floor area at discounted rates when integrated with residential use
- Mechanicals and building support spaces at discounted inclusion rates

### 23-24 Permitted Floor Area in R6-R12 Districts

In R6-R12 districts, base FAR limits may be increased through various bonus and incentive mechanisms.

**Height Factor Modifications:** In certain R6-R12 districts, the relationship between height and FAR permits:

- Buildings achieving specified base street wall heights may access FAR bonuses
- Tower configurations above the street wall may include additional floor area
- Modulation and setback provisions above base height create articulated building profiles that are rewarded with FAR considerations

**Quality Housing Program (R6-R12):** This comprehensive program replaces or supplements traditional bulk regulations in designated areas with:

- Modified height and setback requirements based on context and street wall configuration
- FAR bonuses of up to 20% when combined with affordable housing
- Reduced parking requirements in transit-accessible areas
- Modified court, yard, and open space requirements reflecting residential livability standards
- Special provisions for through-lots and complex site configurations

**Inclusionary Housing Bonus (R6-R12):** Substantial FAR bonuses encourage affordable housing inclusion:

- 20% FAR bonus for moderate income housing at specified affordability levels
- 35% FAR bonus for low-income housing
- Additional bonuses when combined with Quality Housing program participation
- Mixed-income projects with graduated affordability levels may achieve maximum bonus combinations

**Waterfront and Public Access Incentives:** R6-R12 districts in waterfront locations offer:

- FAR bonuses for public waterfront access and promenade provision
- Additional floor area for buildings fronting designated waterfront public access areas
- Credits for exceeding minimum public realm contribution requirements

**Historic Preservation and Landmark Flexibility:** Buildings incorporating or adjacent to historic structures may access:

- FAR bonuses for sensitive restoration and preservation
- Height modifications to maintain architectural compatibility
- Special provisions for adaptive reuse of significant structures

**Community Facility Integration Bonuses:** Where substantial community facilities are integrated with residential use:

- School inclusion bonuses
- Cultural institution integration bonuses
- Healthcare facility inclusion provisions
- Public library or branch facility integration incentives

## 23-30 YARDS, COURTS AND OTHER OPEN AREA REGULATIONS

### 23-31 General Provisions for Open Areas

Yards, courts, and other open areas on residential zoning lots are regulated to ensure adequate light, air, and open space for residents. These regulations apply differentiated standards based on district density, building type, and street configuration.

The regulations establish:

- Minimum dimensions for yards (front, side, rear) based on building height and lot width
- Requirements for courts (both inner and outer) with prescribed dimensions and minimum widths
- Maximum lot coverage percentages limiting building footprints on residential lots
- Minimum distances between buildings on the same or adjacent zoning lots
- Requirements for windows to provide light and air to habitable rooms
- Planting requirements and landscaped areas within yards and courts

These provisions work in concert with height and setback regulations to create three-dimensional open space envelopes that shape the built form and neighborhood character.

### 23-32 Front Yard Requirements

Front yards provide separation between buildings and public streets, establishing transition zones and streetscape character.

**R1-R2 Districts:** Minimum front yard depth of 20 feet, measured from the lot line to the principal building facade. This creates substantial setbacks typical of suburban residential neighborhoods, permitting front landscaping, driveways, and open green space.

**R3 District:** Minimum front yard depth of 20 feet, with variations based on existing street frontage patterns.

**R4 District:** Minimum front yard depth of 15 feet. This reduced requirement reflects the somewhat more urban character appropriate to garden apartment neighborhoods.

**R5 District:** Minimum front yard depth of 10-15 feet depending on context and street wall characteristics.

**R6-R10 Districts:** Front yard requirements vary:

- In R6-R7 districts: 10-15 feet where street wall continuity is not established
- In R8-R10 districts: May be zero where ground-floor retail or community facility is provided
- Special provisions for plazas and public access areas allow modified requirements
- Contextual districts may establish unified street wall requirements eliminating front yard setbacks

**Corner Lots:** Corner lots have modified front yard requirements at the secondary (non-primary) street frontage, often reduced or eliminated where two street frontages exist.

### 23-33 Side Yard Requirements

Side yards provide separation between adjacent buildings and contribute to light, air, and privacy for residential units.

**R1-R2 Districts:** Minimum combined side yard width of 30 feet where lot width is 75 feet or greater, with at least 10 feet on each side. For narrower lots, a single side yard of 10 feet may satisfy requirements. These substantial side yards maintain the green, open character of low-density neighborhoods.

**R3 District:** Minimum combined side yard of 25 feet (or single 15-foot yard where lot permits only one side yard).

**R4 District:** Minimum combined side yard of 20 feet, or single side yard of 12 feet. This accommodates small apartment buildings within moderate open space requirements.

**R5 District:** Minimum combined side yard of 15 feet, or single side yard of 10 feet. Higher-density buildings may occupy more of the lot width while maintaining some side separation.

**R6-R12 Districts:** Side yard requirements are substantially reduced:

- R6-R7 districts: Minimum 10-foot total side yard or none where adjacent to existing building walls
- R8-R10 districts: No minimum side yard requirement in many locations, allowing buildings to reach lot lines on one or both sides
- Special provisions apply to preserve light and air to existing adjacent buildings
- Through-lots and complex configurations have modified side yard treatments

**Side Yard Restrictions:** Certain activities cannot occur within required side yards:

- Residential buildings must be set back from lot lines
- Parking areas may be permitted within side yards in specific districts
- Accessory structures may have modified side yard requirements
- Vertical lot line walls are permitted at lot line in certain contexts

### 23-34 Rear Yard Requirements

Rear yards preserve separation between buildings and provide direct outdoor access to residential units.

#### 23-341 Rear Yard Requirements - R1 through R5 Districts

**R1 District:** Minimum rear yard of 30 feet, measured from the rear lot line to the principal building. This substantial rear yard supports gardens, accessory buildings, and private outdoor space typical of single-family neighborhoods.

**R2 District:** Minimum rear yard of 30 feet.

**R3 District:** Minimum rear yard of 25 feet, with variations based on building height and configuration.

**R4 District:** Minimum rear yard of 20 feet. This accommodates garden apartment configurations with central courtyards and open space.

**R5 District:** Minimum rear yard of 25 feet for buildings up to 12 stories, with increased requirements (30 feet) for taller buildings. Alternatively, buildings may be configured with central courts achieving minimum dimensions.

Rear yard calculation: The rear yard is measured from the rear property line to the closest point of the principal building. Accessory buildings, decks, and minor projections may encroach into rear yards by specified amounts (typically 4 feet or less).

#### 23-342 Rear Yard Requirements - R6 through R12 Districts

**R6-R7 Districts:** Minimum rear yard of 20 feet, with variations:

- Buildings primarily fronting the street may have reduced rear yards
- Buildings with courtyard configurations have modified rear yard standards
- Through-lots may have graduated rear yard requirements

**R8-R10 Districts:** Minimum rear yard of 15 feet in many locations, but may be zero in dense urban settings where buildings have:

- No residential use on rear-facing walls
- All residential units accessed from internal courtyards or front exposures
- Buildings serving as mid-block connectors between street frontages

**Court-Based Configurations:** Many R8-R10 buildings employ interior court systems rather than traditional rear yards, with courts sized to provide light, air, and private outdoor space for residential units.

### 23-35 Court Regulations

Courts within zoning lots provide light, air, and circulation space for residential buildings. Two types of courts are regulated: inner courts (completely surrounded by building walls) and outer courts (open on one side).

**Inner Court Dimensions:**

- Minimum depth: 40 feet (measured perpendicular to building wall)
- Minimum width: 30 feet (for courts serving residential buildings)
- Minimum area: 1,200 square feet
- Minimum width adjacent to windows: 20 feet

Inner courts must provide direct light and air to residential units. The depth and width must be sufficient that no habitable room window is more than 30 feet from the nearest window opening into the court.

**Outer Court Dimensions:**

- Minimum depth: 30 feet
- Minimum width: 30 feet (primary court) or 20 feet (secondary court)
- Must be open to a street or public area on at least one side
- Width is measured at the opening to street

**Court Use Restrictions:** Courts must serve the residential function and cannot be:

- Used for loading or parking (with limited exceptions)
- Obstructed by permanent structures beyond permitted projections
- Inaccessible to residents of the building

**Landscaping and Character:** Courts must include:

- Planting areas covering minimum percentages of court floor area (typically 20-30%)
- Trees and vegetation providing visual amenity and environmental benefit
- Paving and hardscape design supporting circulation and gathering

### 23-36 Maximum Lot Coverage

Maximum lot coverage percentages establish building footprint limits, ensuring adequate open space remains on zoning lots.

**R1 District:** Maximum lot coverage of 40% for principal buildings, or 35% where accessory buildings are also present. This maintains substantial open land, gardens, and landscaped areas typical of single-family neighborhoods.

**R2 District:** Maximum lot coverage of 45%, with 40% where accessory structures are included.

**R3 District:** Maximum lot coverage of 50-55% depending on lot size and building type.

**R4 District:** Maximum lot coverage of 50%, accommodating garden apartments and low-rise residential buildings.

**R5 District:** Maximum lot coverage of 55-60% depending on whether quality housing or other special provisions apply.

**R6-R7 Districts:** Maximum lot coverage of 60-75%, with higher percentages permitted in dense urban contexts.

**R8-R10 Districts:** Maximum lot coverage varies substantially:

- Traditional districts: 70-80% maximum
- Quality Housing districts: May reach 85-90% with court and open space modifications
- Through-lots and special configurations: May achieve up to 95% with appropriate internal open space

**Lot Coverage Calculation:** Lot coverage is computed as the total area of all buildings (measured to the outer walls at all levels) divided by the lot area. Certain features may be excluded:

- Public plazas and publicly accessible outdoor space
- Courtyard areas meeting minimum dimension standards
- Landscaped areas meeting minimum planting percentages
- Parking areas are typically included in lot coverage

### 23-37 Minimum Distance Between Buildings

When multiple buildings occupy a single zoning lot or when buildings are separated from existing adjacent structures, minimum separation distances ensure adequate light, air, and emergency access.

**Same Zoning Lot:** When principal and accessory buildings are on the same lot:

- Minimum 10 feet separation between principal building and accessory building
- Minimum 5 feet between accessory buildings
- Variations apply for firewall-protected structures

**Adjacent Properties:** When calculating light and air, separation distances from buildings on adjacent zoning lots are considered:

- For new buildings adjacent to existing residential buildings, minimum 15-foot separation is preferred
- 12-foot minimum separation is acceptable in dense urban districts (R8-R10)
- Special provisions apply where existing non-compliant configurations exist

**Window Separation:** Windows in buildings must maintain minimum distances from walls of adjacent buildings:

- Minimum 3 feet where window faces blank wall
- Minimum 8 feet where window faces window (allowing for light and privacy)
- Increased distances (12-15 feet) where high-density development is concerned

### 23-38 Planting Requirements

Residential zoning lots must include minimum landscaped and planted areas to provide environmental quality, stormwater management, and neighborhood amenity.

**Landscaped Area Requirements:**

- R1-R2 Districts: Minimum 20% of lot area in landscaping and vegetation
- R3-R5 Districts: Minimum 15-20% landscaped area
- R6-R12 Districts: Minimum 10-15% landscaped area, with variations based on ground-floor use

**Planting Specifications:** Landscaped areas must include:

- Tree planting at specified densities (typically 1 tree per 500-1,000 square feet of landscaped area)
- Shrub and herbaceous plantings providing year-round visual interest
- Native and climate-appropriate species supporting local ecology
- Soil depth and volume adequate for sustainable growth (minimum 3 feet for trees)

**Street Tree Requirements:** Where residential buildings front streets, street tree planting is required per City standards:

- 1 tree per 25-30 feet of street frontage
- Species selection per approved street tree list
- Coordinated with street infrastructure (sidewalk, utilities)

**Green Roof and Stormwater:** Modern developments increasingly utilize:

- Vegetated roofs and walls providing additional landscaping
- Bioswales and rain gardens managing stormwater
- Permeable paving within courtyards and loading areas
- Calculated as bonus landscaping when meeting specified standards

## 23-40 HEIGHT AND SETBACK REGULATIONS

### 23-41 Permitted Obstructions to Height Limits

Certain building features may project above height limits without being counted as height violations:

**Mechanical Equipment:** Cooling towers, air handling units, and equipment serving building systems may project 15 feet above the height limit (maximum 25 feet in some districts).

**Elevator and Stair Overruns:** Structures housing elevator and stair access serving roof areas may project up to 4 stories above the height limit, with maximum 30 feet total height.

**Parapet Walls:** Non-habitable parapet walls up to 4 feet may be constructed at roof edges.

**Flagpoles, Antennas, and Wireless Transmission Facilities:** These structures may rise above height limits with specified setbacks and design standards.

**Spires and Architectural Features:** Artistic or architectural pinnacles, cupolas, and similar features serving aesthetic purposes may rise above the height limit, provided they occupy less than 25% of roof area.

**Solar Panels and Renewable Energy:** Photovoltaic arrays and other renewable energy systems may rise above the height limit when mounted on roofs.

### 23-42 Height and Setback in R1-R5 Districts

#### 23-421 R1-R5 Basic Height and Setback Regulations

Low-density residential districts establish height and setback rules that maintain neighborhood character and ensure adequate light and air.

**R1 District:** Maximum building height of 35 feet or 2.5 stories. The building must be set back from side lot lines and the rear lot line such that a 45-degree angle line drawn from the side or rear lot line does not intersect the building. Front setback of 20 feet is required. These regulations create modest-scale residential neighborhoods with primarily single-family and duplex character.

**R2 District:** Maximum building height of 35 feet or 2.5 stories with similar setback requirements as R1, supporting slightly denser residential neighborhoods while maintaining open character.

**R3 District:** Maximum building height of 35-40 feet (2.5-3 stories) depending on lot width. Setback requirements are:

- Front setback: 20 feet
- Side setback: 10 feet minimum per side, or combined 25 feet
- Rear setback: Governed by rear yard requirement (minimum 25 feet)

**R4 District:** Maximum building height of 60 feet (approximately 5 stories). Garden apartment buildings employ modified setback regulations:

- Setback from front lot line: 15 feet minimum
- Side setback: 15 feet minimum combined, or 8 feet single side
- Rear setback: 20 feet minimum or courtyard configuration

**R5 District:** Maximum building height of 65-75 feet (5-6 stories) for standard development, or up to 85 feet where quality housing provisions apply. Setback regulations:

- Front setback: 10-15 feet depending on context
- Side setback: 10 feet minimum combined or none in certain configurations
- Rear setback: 25 feet minimum for standard buildings, reduced where courts are employed

#### 23-422 R1-R4 Cottage Envelope Provisions

The cottage envelope is an alternative regulatory framework for R1-R4 districts that permits more efficient residential building while maintaining neighborhood character through scaled massing and setback regulations.

**Height Limits:**

- Principal building maximum perimeter wall height: 21 feet
- Principal building maximum total height: 25-27 feet (depending on roof configuration)
- Accessory buildings: Maximum 18 feet

**Setback Requirements (Cottage Envelope):**

- Front setback: 10 feet minimum (reduced from standard 20 feet)
- Side setback: 5 feet minimum per side, with combined 12 feet minimum for two sides
- Rear setback: 8 feet minimum

**Floor Area Inclusion:** Building floor area is calculated at standard rates but with envelope shapes that encourage:

- Efficient rectangular or near-rectangular building footprints
- Pitched or flat roofs with reasonable slopes (rather than steep profiles)
- Single or two-family residential buildings with integrated parking

**Lot Requirements:** Cottage envelope is available on lots meeting minimum area thresholds:

- Minimum lot area: 1,200 square feet
- Minimum lot width: 25 feet
- Lot must not exceed 5,000 square feet (or other specified ceiling)

**Architectural Compatibility:** The cottage envelope encourages:

- Variety in facade design and materials
- Ground-floor windows and entrances facing streets
- Pitched roofs reflecting traditional residential architecture
- Fenestration patterns and detailing compatible with neighborhood context

#### 23-423 R4-R5 Height and Setback

**R4 District (Alternate Regulations):** In certain R4 areas with denser development patterns:

- Maximum building height of 65 feet (approximately 5-6 stories)
- Modified setback requirements permitting more urban street wall configuration
- Central courtyard configurations with reduced setbacks to adjacent streets

**R5 District (Alternate Regulations):** In R5 quality housing areas:

- Maximum building height of 85 feet (approximately 6-7 stories)
- Setback configuration based on contextual streetscape study
- May include zero-setback street wall with upper-story setbacks
- Tower configurations above base height with specified setback angles

#### 23-424 through 23-426 Special Provisions for Contextual Districts

Certain R5 and lower-density areas are designated as "contextual" where detailed neighborhood character studies have informed specific height and setback regulations differing from standard provisions.

**Contextual Height:** May be established at specific heights reflecting existing streetscape fabric:

- Typical heights: 65-95 feet in contextual R5 areas
- Street-specific height limits reflecting existing dominant building heights
- Graduated height zones within neighborhoods reflecting block-by-block context

**Contextual Setback:** Setback regulations in contextual areas may be refined to:

- Establish continuous street walls at specified heights (e.g., 60 feet continuous)
- Require upper-story setbacks at 40-60 foot elevation
- Establish varying setback distances based on street frontage characteristics
- Preserve views to prominent landmarks or water bodies

**Preservation of Views:** Contextual provisions may include:

- View corridors to significant landscape features or waterfront
- Scenic vistas from public spaces or residential neighborhoods
- Sky visibility requirements limiting building profile in defined corridors

### 23-43 Height and Setback in R6-R12 Districts

Height and setback regulations in medium and high-density districts create varied streetscapes while permitting substantial building massing and mixed-use development.

#### 23-431 Basic Height and Setback - Sky Exposure Plane

The sky exposure plane (SEP) is a theoretical three-dimensional plane that limits how large buildings can grow as they rise from the street, ensuring light and air reach street level and lower buildings while permitting taller buildings in denser contexts.

**Sky Exposure Plane Definition:** An imaginary plane that rises from specified distances from lot lines at an angle of 25-60 degrees (depending on district). Buildings must not penetrate this plane, creating a theoretical envelope that widens with distance from street frontage.

**Street Wall Height:** The height at which the building must begin setback, measured from the curb or property line:

- R6-R7 Districts: Street wall height of 60-80 feet (typically 5-6 stories)
- R8-R9 Districts: Street wall height of 85-125 feet (6-10 stories)
- R10 District: Street wall height of 125 feet minimum, often 150+ feet

**Setback Angle:** The angle at which the building must retreat above the street wall height:

- R6-R7 Districts: Setback angle of 25 degrees measured from top of street wall
- R8-R9 Districts: Setback angle of 35 degrees
- R10 District: Setback angle of 45 degrees

These provisions ensure that buildings at ground level maintain reasonable proportion and that setbacks increase substantially in higher-density districts.

**Maximum Building Height:** Absolute height limits (not derived from FAR or SEP) may apply:

- R6 District: 85 feet maximum
- R7 District: 120 feet maximum
- R8 District: 250 feet maximum
- R9 District: 250-400 feet depending on location and context
- R10 District: 1,200+ feet (no practical limit in many locations)

#### 23-432 R6-R12 Districts Without Letter Suffix - Quality Housing Program

The Quality Housing Program is an optional regulatory framework in R6-R12 districts that replaces standard height, setback, and open space regulations with an alternative system designed to:

- Encourage higher-quality residential design and floor plate configuration
- Mandate reduced parking requirements in transit-accessible areas
- Provide FAR bonuses in exchange for more affordable housing inclusion
- Establish modified court, yard, and open space standards

**Base Height and Street Wall:** Quality Housing buildings must establish a definable base:

- Minimum street wall height: 85 feet in R6, 100 feet in R7-R8, 125 feet in R9-R10
- Street wall must maintain majority of street frontage (typically 80%+ continuity)
- Variations permitted at building corners and public access points

**Tower Regulations:** Above the street wall, tower portions must comply with:

- Tower floor plate limits: Maximum 40% of lot area at any level
- Tower setback from street wall: Minimum 15 feet inboard of street wall face
- Tower spacing: Minimum 85 feet between towers on adjacent properties (where applicable)
- Tower height: Subject to absolute height limits and zoning district caps

**Open Space Standards:** Quality Housing modifies traditional yard and court requirements:

- Minimum 10% of lot area in permanently accessible open space
- Open space must include minimum 5,000 square feet for buildings over 100,000 square feet
- Courtyards and plazas must meet dimension standards (minimum 75 feet in one direction)
- Ground-floor public access and transparency requirements

**Parking Requirements:** Substantially reduced parking in transit-accessible Quality Housing buildings:

- 0.5 spaces per dwelling unit (vs. 1.0-1.5 in standard regulations)
- Available only in areas within specific distance of subway/commuter rail
- Car-share requirements and EV charging provisions

#### 23-433 through 23-438 Tower Regulations, Base Height, and Street Wall

**Tower Floor Plate Limits:** In R8-R10 districts, tower portions above the street wall must meet:

- Maximum 40-50% of lot area per floor in R8-R9 districts
- Maximum 40% in R10 district
- Calculation includes all floor area above the base height

**Tower Setback:** Towers must be set back from the street wall:

- Minimum 15 feet inboard from street wall in most districts
- Creates visual separation and reduces shadow impact at street level
- Varies based on street orientation and existing context

**Base Height Requirements:** The street wall or base portion of buildings provides urban enclosure:

- Minimum 85-125 feet depending on district
- Must contain 60-70% of the tower's perimeter length
- Ground floor must be transparent (80%+ window area) for active uses
- Residential lobbies and entrances provided at street level

**Windows to Street:** Buildings fronting public streets must provide:

- Minimum 80% window area at street level for non-residential uses
- Minimum 60% window area where ground floor is residential
- Windows must be functional (operable or accessible) in many cases
- Display windows or transparent glass per non-residential design standards

**Through-Block Connections:** Where buildings occupy full blocks, provisions ensure:

- Pedestrian connections from primary street to secondary street or public area
- Arcade or covered passage minimum 20 feet wide where applicable
- Ground-floor retail or active use configuration supporting public space

## 23-50 MINIMUM REQUIRED DWELLING UNITS AND FLOOR AREA PER ROOM

### 23-51 Minimum Dwelling Units Per Lot Area

Certain residential buildings must include minimum numbers of dwelling units to ensure efficient residential development and achieve density targets appropriate to the zoning district.

**R1-R5 Districts:** Generally, no minimum dwelling unit requirement applies, allowing for development flexibility ranging from single-family homes to low-rise apartment buildings based on market conditions and design preferences.

**R6-R7 Districts:**

- R6: Minimum dwelling unit density of 30-40 units per 10,000 square feet of lot area (approximately 75-100 units per acre)
- R7: Minimum dwelling unit density of 50-65 units per 10,000 square feet of lot area (approximately 200-260 units per acre)

These minimums ensure that sites in R6-R7 districts achieve appropriate residential density supporting neighborhood vitality and transit utilization.

**R8-R10 Districts:**

- R8: Minimum dwelling unit density of 100+ units per 10,000 square feet of lot area (400+ units per acre)
- R9: Minimum dwelling unit density of 150-200 units per 10,000 square feet of lot area (600-800 units per acre)
- R10: Minimum dwelling unit density of 250+ units per 10,000 square feet of lot area (1,000+ units per acre)

These high-density minimums are appropriate for transit-oriented districts supporting vertical residential neighborhoods and mixed-use development.

**Exemptions and Modifications:** Minimum dwelling unit requirements may be reduced or eliminated for:

- Mixed-use buildings where non-residential floor area is substantial
- Community facility buildings (schools, cultural institutions)
- Buildings with significant open space or public plaza contributions
- Rehabilitation or restoration of historic buildings

### 23-52 Minimum Floor Area Per Room

Residential buildings must provide minimum floor areas per habitable room to ensure adequate living space and basic quality standards.

**Living Room (Principal Habitable Room):**

- Minimum 150 square feet for efficiency units
- Minimum 150 square feet for one-bedroom units
- Minimum 200 square feet for two-bedroom units
- Minimum 250 square feet for three-bedroom units
- Additional 100 square feet per additional bedroom

**Bedrooms:**

- Minimum 100 square feet for any bedroom
- Minimum 120 square feet for master bedroom
- At least one window in every bedroom (minimum 10 square feet)

**Kitchens:**

- Minimum 70 square feet for kitchenettes
- Minimum 100 square feet for full kitchens in efficiency units
- Minimum 120 square feet for kitchens in multi-bedroom units

**Bathrooms:**

- Minimum 40 square feet for half bath
- Minimum 50 square feet for full bathroom
- Minimum 70 square feet for primary bathroom in three+ bedroom units

**Dining Areas:**

- Minimum 80-100 square feet where separate from living room
- May be combined with living room (counted as minimum living room size)

**Additional Rooms:** Other habitable rooms (dens, home offices, studies):

- Minimum 70-100 square feet
- Must meet all window and ventilation requirements

**Calculations and Exemptions:** Floor area measurements:

- Include floor area of rooms measured to finished surfaces
- Exclude closets, bathrooms, and building service areas
- Permitted variations for quality housing designs reflecting contemporary living arrangements
- Some modern units may achieve reduced minimums where compensated by larger overall unit size

## 23-60 DENSITY REGULATIONS AND AFFORDABLE INDEPENDENT RESIDENCES FOR SENIORS

### 23-61 Density Control and Residential Character

Density regulations in lower-density districts (R1-R5) establish limits on dwelling unit concentration per lot area to maintain neighborhood character and prevent overcrowding.

**R1 District:** Maximum density of 15-20 dwelling units per 10,000 square feet of lot area (approximately 65-85 units per acre). This maintains predominantly single-family neighborhood character.

**R2 District:** Maximum density of 20-25 dwelling units per 10,000 square feet (approximately 85-110 units per acre), permitting modest multi-family development.

**R3 District:** Maximum density of 30-40 dwelling units per 10,000 square feet (approximately 130-175 units per acre), supporting garden apartments and modest mid-rise buildings.

**R4 District:** Maximum density of 40-50 dwelling units per 10,000 square feet (approximately 175-220 units per acre), appropriate for garden apartment neighborhoods.

**R5 District:** Maximum density of 60-75 dwelling units per 10,000 square feet (approximately 260-330 units per acre), permitting mid-rise residential buildings.

Density calculations include all dwelling units on a zoning lot, with variations for:

- Buildings with substantial community facility components
- Accessory dwelling units or junior units in certain districts
- Senior housing developments with modified unit size standards

### 23-62 Affordable Independent Residences for Seniors - Special Provisions

Affordable senior housing developments meeting specified criteria may access modified bulk regulations and affordability requirements:

**Building Size and Configuration:** Senior housing buildings must provide:

- Minimum 50 dwelling units per building
- Buildings designed specifically for residents 55 years of age or older
- Accessible and barrier-free design per ADA standards and City standards
- Common facilities appropriate to senior population (fitness, wellness, dining, recreation)

**Affordability Requirements:** Buildings must maintain affordability for:

- Minimum 80% of units at affordable rents (generally 60% AMI or lower) for 30-year affordability period
- Rents controlled through HPD affordability restrictions
- Set-asides for seniors meeting low and moderate-income thresholds

**Modified Bulk Regulations:** Senior housing buildings may access:

- FAR bonuses of 10-20% in higher-density districts
- Parking reductions reflecting lower automobile dependency of senior population
- Modified setback and height regulations in certain districts
- Flexibility in open space and courtyard configuration

**Other Special Provisions:**

- Community facility components serving seniors may be integrated at ground floor
- Ground-floor retail or services supporting senior residents
- Transit accessibility and pedestrian-friendly site design
- Reduced parking ratios: 0.3-0.5 spaces per dwelling unit (vs. 1.0+ in standard regulations)

### 23-63 Senior Housing - Additional Standards

**Design Standards:** Senior housing developments should provide:

- Accessible entrances and lobbies meeting ADA standards
- Elevator service to all residential floors
- Accessible unit designs (roll-in showers, grab bars, accessible kitchens)
- Ground-floor common areas for fitness, dining, recreation, and socializing

**Site Composition:** Senior housing communities should include:

- On-site or proximate healthcare facilities or services
- Landscaped courtyards and outdoor recreation areas
- Walking paths and circulation areas designed for seniors
- Parking designed for personal vehicles and accessible spaces for residents and visitors

**Licensing and Operation:** Senior housing facilities operating as housing for seniors must:

- Comply with relevant City and State regulations for assisted living or independent senior housing
- Provide supportive services including case management, transportation, and social programming
- Maintain onsite management and security
- Meet accessibility and safety standards for older adult populations

## 23-70 SPECIAL PROVISIONS FOR ZONING LOTS CONTAINING CERTAIN COMMUNITY FACILITY USES

Community facility buildings and spaces integrated with residential development are subject to special bulk regulations that vary from standard residential provisions.

**Integrated Community Facilities:** Where schools, libraries, cultural institutions, or other community facilities are incorporated into primarily residential buildings:

- Floor area of community facility may be excluded or calculated at reduced rates in FAR calculations
- Height limits may be increased where community facility portion is substantial
- Setback and yard requirements may be modified where community facility has ground-floor public access
- Parking requirements may be shared between residential and community facility components

**Ground-Floor Retail and Services:** Buildings incorporating ground-floor retail, community space, or services may access:

- Modified height and setback regulations permitting street-active ground floor
- Parking reductions in lieu of other required components
- FAR bonuses for significant public benefit provision
- Flexibility in street wall and façade transparency requirements

**Public Plazas and Gathering Spaces:** Where residential buildings include publicly accessible plazas meeting specified standards:

- FAR bonuses of 10-20% may be available
- Height limits may be increased
- Setback and open space requirements modified
- Design standards for plaza include seating, plantings, public access, programming

**School and Educational Facilities:** Residential buildings containing integrated school facilities:

- FAR bonuses of 15-35% depending on school size and grade configuration
- Height modifications allowing instructional and recreational spaces
- Yard and court modifications accommodating school playgrounds and outdoor learning areas
- Parking requirements calibrated to combined residential/school use

## 23-80 THROUGH 23-90 ADDITIONAL SPECIAL PROVISIONS

### Street Wall and Streetscape Provisions

Regulations ensure active, pedestrian-friendly streetscapes:

**Ground-Floor Transparency:** Buildings with street frontage must maintain:

- Minimum 60-80% window area at street level
- Opacity ratios limited (typically 20-40% solid surface)
- Windows must be clear glass allowing views into interior spaces
- Blank walls limited to specified lengths (typically 25-40 feet maximum)

**Facade Articulation:** Vertical modulation and articulation required to:

- Break up long monotonous facades with reveals, setbacks, or bay windows
- Provide visual interest and pedestrian-scale detail
- Vary materials, colors, or textures to create visual diversity
- Scale architectural details to human dimensions (e.g., window proportions, entrance heights)

**Building Entrances:** Ground-floor entrances must:

- Face the public street or plaza where practical
- Be clearly visible and marked from the street
- Provide ground-level access without excessive stairs or grade changes
- Include recessed or sheltered entry areas in weather-exposed locations

**Stoops and Transitions:** Where buildings have grades above street level:

- Stoops connecting street to entry must be appropriately scaled
- Handrails and accessible routes required per ADA
- Plantings and terracing integrate building entries with streetscape

### Special District Modifications and Overlays

Certain neighborhoods with special zoning overlays have modified bulk regulations:

**Historic District Provisions:** Developments in historic districts must:

- Maintain bulk and mass compatibility with historic context
- Provide additional setbacks and height reductions in certain cases
- Respect important view corridors and streetscape patterns
- Design new buildings compatible with existing architectural character

**Waterfront District Modifications:** Developments in waterfront zones have:

- Modified height limits reflecting waterfront context and water views
- Special provisions for public waterfront access and promenades
- Setback regulations addressing water view preservation
- Ground-floor uses supporting waterfront activation and public access

**Transit-Oriented Development Provisions:** Developments proximate to transit have:

- Reduced parking requirements (often 0.5 spaces per unit or less)
- FAR bonuses supporting higher-density residential development
- Modified setback and height regulations encouraging development intensity
- Ground-floor retail and community facility incentives

**Downtown Brooklyn, Other Commercial Overlays:** Residential development in commercial districts:

- Modified height and setback permitting mixed-use development
- Ground-floor retail and commercial use requirements
- FAR bonuses for residential components above commercial base
- Parking integration with commercial parking facilities

### Transitional and Contextual Regulations

**Buffer Zones:** Where residential districts abut commercial or manufacturing districts:

- Setback and height regulations provide transition
- Sound and odor mitigation requirements
- Landscaping and screening of incompatible uses
- Special height limits ensuring residential buildings don't overwhelm transition zones

**Hillside or Topographic Modifications:** In areas with significant topography:

- Building height and setback regulations adjusted for site grades
- Height measured from average site elevation rather than minimum
- Setback calculations account for sloped sites
- Special provisions for buildings on slopes exceeding specified percentages

**Existing Non-Compliant Contexts:** Where substantial existing development doesn't meet current zoning requirements:

- New development not required to match non-compliant existing buildings
- Bulk regulations apply to new development to gradually improve character
- Limited flexibility for additions to existing non-compliant buildings
- Grandfather provisions for building additions up to specified size

### Affordable Housing Incentives and Affordability Preservation

**Inclusionary Housing Requirements:** In certain districts, residential development must include:

- 20-25% of units at low income levels (60% AMI or below)
- 15-20% at moderate income levels (60-80% AMI)
- Affordability preservation periods of 30+ years
- Options for on-site, off-site, or in-lieu contributions

**Housing Preservation Provisions:** Regulations protect existing affordable housing:

- Restrictions on displacement and conversion of rent-stabilized units
- Inclusionary housing bonus opportunities supporting affordable development
- Community land trusts and other preservation mechanisms
- Adaptive reuse incentives preserving residential buildings

### Sustainability and Environmental Provisions

**Green Building Standards:** Encouraged or required provisions include:

- Energy efficiency standards and LEED certification
- Stormwater management and rain gardens
- Native plantings and ecological restoration
- Solar panel and renewable energy accommodation
- Green roofs and vegetated walls

**Environmental Remediation:** Sites with environmental concerns:

- Remediation and capping requirements under CEQR
- Soil and vapor intrusion mitigation
- Contamination restrictions limiting certain land uses
- Ongoing monitoring and long-term stewardship requirements

## Summary of Key Regulatory Thresholds

This chapter establishes comprehensive regulations for residential buildings across New York City's diverse residential districts, from low-density R1 neighborhoods supporting primarily single-family homes, to the highest-density R10 districts permitting supertall residential towers. Key thresholds include:

- Floor Area Ratios ranging from 0.5 (R1-R3) to 10.0 (R10)
- Maximum building heights from 35 feet (R1-R3) to unlimited heights (R10 in certain locations)
- Yard and court dimensions ensuring adequate light, air, and open space
- Density controls ranging from 15-20 units per acre (R1) to 1,000+ units per acre (R10)
- Special provisions for affordable housing, senior housing, community facilities, and quality housing programs
- Modified regulations for waterfront sites, historic districts, and transit-oriented development

The bulk regulations work in concert with use regulations (Article I) and special district provisions (Article VII) to shape New York City's neighborhoods and achieve planning objectives at neighborhood, district, and citywide scales. Regular amendments and special provisions allow the regulations to respond to evolving housing needs, sustainability concerns, and development opportunities while maintaining core principles of adequate light, air, privacy, and open space for New York City's diverse residential population.

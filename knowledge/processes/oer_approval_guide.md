# OER Approval and Little E Submission Guide

## Overview

The Office of Environmental Remediation (OER), a division of the New York City Department of Environmental Protection (DEP), oversees environmental remediation in NYC. OER approval is a critical requirement for development on E-designated sites and voluntary cleanup sites. Understanding the OER process, E-designations, and Little E submissions is essential for expediting projects on contaminated or potentially contaminated properties.

## What is OER and When is OER Approval Needed

### Office of Environmental Remediation (OER)

OER is a city agency that manages:
- Environmental assessment of contaminated sites
- Remediation oversight and approval
- Voluntary cleanup program administration
- No Further Action (NFA) determinations
- E-Designation (environmental restriction) management
- Environmental compliance during construction

### When OER Approval is Required

OER approval is mandatory in the following situations:

**E-Designated Sites**
- Any property with an active E-Designation in the zoning lot
- Properties with historical contamination issues documented in city records
- Sites with Phase I or Phase II Environmental Site Assessment (ESA) findings
- Industrial properties with potential soil or groundwater contamination

**Voluntary Cleanup Program (VCP)**
- Properties entering the city's Environmental Remediation Program
- Sites where owner/developer voluntarily enrolls for environmental cleanup
- Properties with known or suspected contamination not previously reported

**Development Projects on Potentially Impacted Sites**
- Excavation or grading work that disturbs soil
- Subsurface work including foundations, basements, or utility trenches
- Properties in areas with known historical industrial, commercial, or gas station uses
- Waterfront or wetland properties

**No Further Action (NFA) Letters**
- Properties seeking environmental clearance for development
- Sites with historical contamination that has been remediated
- Properties requiring proof of environmental compliance for financing

## E-Designations Explained

### What is an E-Designation?

An E-Designation is a restriction placed on a zoning lot that identifies environmental conditions or contamination requiring management or remediation. E-Designations:
- Run with the land and bind successive owners
- Restrict certain uses or require environmental conditions
- Are recorded in the City Register
- Must be addressed before building permits can be issued
- Can restrict residential occupancy or sensitive uses
- Require approval from OER before modification or removal

### Types of E-Designations

**E-Contamination (Environmental Contamination)**
- Soil or groundwater contamination present
- Asbestos or lead-based paint issues
- Petroleum products, hazardous substances, or pollutants
- Requires Phase I and Phase II assessments
- Remedial Action Plan (RAP) required

**E-Flooding (Environmental Hazard - Flooding)**
- Properties in flood-prone areas
- Coastal zones subject to storm surge
- Restrictions on basement and cellar uses
- FEMA floodplain designations

**E-Groundwater (Environmental Hazard - Groundwater)**
- High groundwater table areas
- Potential groundwater contamination
- Restrictions on below-grade development
- Requires groundwater management plans

**E-Asbestos (Environmental - Asbestos Hazard)**
- Buildings with asbestos-containing materials
- Requires abatement prior to renovation
- Asbestos survey required before demolition

**E-Noise (Environmental - Noise Hazard)**
- Properties near highways, airports, or transit infrastructure
- Requires noise mitigation measures
- Sound attenuation requirements for residential uses

### Other Environmental Restrictions

- **E-Commercial Waste**: Properties with industrial waste disposal history
- **E-Petroleum**: Sites with petroleum product contamination
- **E-Tidal**: Properties subject to tidal influences or wetland regulations

## How to Check for E-Designations

### ZoLa (Zoning Lot Information System)

ZoLa is the city's primary tool for checking zoning restrictions including E-Designations:
- **Access**: Navigate to NYC Department of City Planning website (www.nyc.gov/planning)
- **Search Method**: Enter block and lot number for the property
- **Information Provided**:
  - All active E-Designations on zoning lot
  - Text of environmental restrictions
  - Date of designation issuance
  - Requirements and mitigation measures
  - Agency responsible (usually OER/DEP)

### ACRIS (Automated City Register Information System)

ACRIS provides recorded documents including E-Designation documents:
- **Access**: Available through NYC Department of Finance website
- **Search Method**: Search by property address or BBL (Block-Batch-Lot)
- **Information Provided**:
  - E-Designation documents and legal descriptions
  - Environmental restriction language
  - Liens or encumbrances related to environmental issues
  - Modification or removal documents

### City Environmental Data Portal

- NYC Department of Environmental Protection maintains environmental databases
- Historical Brownfield, RCRA, and Superfund site information
- Spill and contamination incident records
- Coastal flood hazard mapping

### Phase I Environmental Site Assessment

- Recommended first step for contaminated property development
- Documents environmental history and site conditions
- Identifies actual or potential environmental concerns
- Required for many OER approvals and financing

### Communication with OER Directly

- OER maintains detailed site files for designated properties
- Staff can provide:
  - Current E-Designation status
  - Requirements for remediation and approval
  - Historical contamination records
  - Environmental assessment phase requirements

## OER Approval Process for Development

### Step 1: Pre-Submission Phase

**Environmental Site Assessment**
- Conduct Phase I ESA (recommended even without E-Designation)
- Phase I identifies environmental history, prior industrial uses, contamination potential
- If Phase I identifies recognized environmental conditions (RECs), Phase II testing required

**Phase II Environmental Site Assessment** (if Phase I indicates contamination potential)
- Soil and groundwater sampling and analysis
- Testing for hazardous substances and contaminants
- Identification of contaminant extent and levels
- Cost evaluation for remediation
- [VERIFY timeframe: typically 6-8 weeks]

**Remedial Action Plan (RAP) Development** (if contamination found)
- Engineering assessment of contamination
- Proposed cleanup approach and standards
- Remediation method selection (excavation, capping, in-place stabilization, etc.)
- Timeline and cost estimates
- Institutional Controls (ICs) if needed (land use restrictions, deed notices)

### Step 2: Construction Health and Safety Plan (CHASP)

**CHASP is Required for:**
- Projects with known or suspected soil contamination
- Excavation, grading, or subsurface work
- Construction in areas with identified environmental concerns
- Any site with OER E-Designation

**CHASP Contents:**
- Identifies potential environmental hazards at site
- Specifies testing and monitoring procedures
- Details health and safety protocols for construction workers
- Outlines procedures for managing contaminated material
- Includes contingency plans for unexpected findings
- Lists responsible parties and emergency contacts

**CHASP Development:**
- Prepared by environmental consultant with NYC experience
- Based on Phase I and Phase II findings (if applicable)
- Updated with knowledge of specific excavation plans
- Must be approved by OER before construction can begin
- Implementation monitored during construction

### Step 3: Little E Submission

**Little E Definition**
- Formal submission to OER certifying environmental conditions
- Also called "E-Designation Compliance Certification"
- Required for projects on E-designated sites
- Submitted with DOB permit application

**Little E Submission Package Includes:**
1. **Environmental Status Letter**
   - Documentation of environmental history
   - Phase I and Phase II ESA results
   - Identification of environmental conditions
   - Certification of findings by environmental professional

2. **Remedial Action Plan (if applicable)**
   - Describes contamination and extent
   - Proposes remediation approach
   - Specifies remediation standards (NYSDEC, ASTM, etc.)
   - Timeline for remediation activities

3. **Construction Health and Safety Plan (CHASP)**
   - Completed CHASP document
   - Site-specific health and safety protocols
   - Contaminated material management procedures
   - Testing and monitoring specifications

4. **Institutional Controls Documentation (if needed)**
   - Description of ongoing restrictions
   - Land Use Restrictions (LUR)
   - Engineering Controls (EC)
   - Deed notice language (if applicable)

5. **Certifications and Signatures**
   - Licensed environmental professional certifications
   - Site engineer seal and signature
   - Responsible party acknowledgment
   - OER-approved consultant credentials

### Step 4: OER Review and Approval

**OER Review Process:**
- Receipt and initial completeness check: [VERIFY 5 business days]
- Substantive technical review: [VERIFY 15-30 business days]
- Comments and requests for additional information: [VERIFY 10-20 business days]
- Resubmission and final review cycle: [VERIFY 10-20 business days]

**Review Criteria:**
- Adequacy of environmental assessment
- Appropriateness of proposed remediation
- Sufficiency of health and safety measures
- Compliance with environmental standards
- Effectiveness of institutional controls

**Possible Review Outcomes:**
- **Approval**: OER issues approval letter; DOB can proceed with permit
- **Conditional Approval**: Approval with specific requirements or monitoring
- **Requested Revisions**: Additional information or plan modifications needed
- **Denial**: Significant deficiencies; remediation approach inadequate

### Step 5: DOB Coordination

**Critical Timing:**
- OER approval letter must be obtained BEFORE DOB permit issuance
- Little E approval is prerequisite for DOB Sign-Off on permit
- DOB will not issue permit without OER clearance letter
- Timeline impact: 3-8 weeks (or longer) added to permit process

**Documentation for DOB:**
- OER approval letter
- Complete Little E submission package
- CHASP approved and signed
- Environmental compliance documentation

## Remedial Action Plan (RAP)

### RAP Purpose and Scope

A Remedial Action Plan is a detailed engineering document that:
- Identifies contaminated areas and contamination extent
- Specifies cleanup standards and regulatory requirements
- Describes remediation method(s) in detail
- Proposes timeline and cost estimates
- Identifies responsible parties
- Addresses long-term management and monitoring

### RAP Components

1. **Site Background and History**
   - Property history and prior uses
   - Known contamination events
   - Environmental assessment findings
   - Regulatory history

2. **Contamination Assessment**
   - Identification of contaminants and location
   - Quantification of contamination levels
   - Comparison to regulatory standards (NYSDEC, soil remediation standards)
   - Human health and environmental risk assessment

3. **Proposed Remediation**
   - Selected remedy (excavation/removal, capping, in-situ stabilization, etc.)
   - Remediation standards (background, non-residential SCOs, residential SCOs)
   - Detailed implementation procedures
   - Verification sampling and testing protocols

4. **Schedule and Cost Estimates**
   - Remediation timeline
   - Construction phasing considerations
   - Cost breakdown by phase
   - Contingency budgets

5. **Operation and Maintenance**
   - Long-term management requirements
   - Monitoring and maintenance schedules
   - Institutional Controls (if applicable)
   - Deed notice requirements

### RAP Development Timeline

- Initial draft preparation: [VERIFY 4-6 weeks]
- Environmental consultant and client review: [VERIFY 2-4 weeks]
- OER pre-submission meeting: [VERIFY 1-2 weeks]
- OER formal review: [VERIFY 3-8 weeks]
- Revisions and resubmission: [VERIFY 2-6 weeks]
- **Total typical timeline: 3-5 months**

## Construction Health and Safety Plan (CHASP)

### CHASP Definition and Requirements

A Construction Health and Safety Plan is a document that:
- Identifies environmental hazards at a construction site
- Specifies procedures to protect worker health and safety
- Outlines contaminated material management
- Details contingency plans for unexpected contamination discoveries
- Is REQUIRED on any NYC construction project with environmental concerns

### When CHASP is Mandated

- **E-Designated Sites**: All sites with active E-Designations
- **Phase II ESA Findings**: If Phase II identified contamination
- **Excavation in Contaminated Areas**: Any digging in known contaminated zones
- **Subsurface Construction**: Basement, foundation, or utility work on suspect properties
- **Industrial/Commercial Site Reuse**: Properties with prior industrial history
- **Historical Contamination**: Properties with documented spill or contamination events

### CHASP Contents and Requirements

1. **Site Environmental Conditions**
   - Phase I and Phase II findings summary
   - Identified contaminants and location
   - Previous remediation activities
   - Current environmental status

2. **Health and Safety Hazards**
   - Chemical hazards (specific contaminants)
   - Physical hazards (excavation, confined spaces)
   - Biological hazards
   - Worker exposure routes (inhalation, dermal, ingestion)

3. **Engineering and Administrative Controls**
   - Air monitoring and ventilation
   - Personal protective equipment (PPE) requirements
   - Containment and work area restrictions
   - Access control and signage
   - Worker training and medical surveillance

4. **Contaminated Material Management**
   - Excavated soil classification and handling
   - Stockpiling and disposal procedures
   - Waste characterization testing
   - Documentation and tracking of material disposal
   - Off-site disposal facility requirements (licensed waste facilities)

5. **Monitoring and Testing Procedures**
   - Dust and air monitoring methods
   - Frequency of testing (continuous, daily, weekly)
   - Laboratory analytical protocols
   - Action levels and response procedures
   - Unexpected contamination discovery procedures

6. **Waste Management and Disposal**
   - Classification of excavated soil (hazardous vs. non-hazardous)
   - On-site stockpile management
   - Disposal facility and waste manifests
   - Documentation and tracking
   - Regulatory compliance (RCRA, DEC, etc.)

7. **Contingency Plans**
   - Procedures if contamination encountered exceeds Phase II findings
   - Emergency response procedures
   - Additional investigation requirements
   - Modification and approval process for plan changes
   - Stop-work provisions and escalation procedures

8. **Safety Training and Certification**
   - Required worker certifications (OSHA, Site Safety and Health Officer)
   - Training requirements and schedule
   - Competent person designation
   - Medical surveillance and baseline health assessments

9. **Responsible Parties and Contacts**
   - Environmental consultant contact and credentials
   - Site Safety Officer name and license
   - Project manager and contractor information
   - OER and DOB contact information
   - Emergency response contacts

### CHASP Approval and Implementation

- CHASP must be approved by OER or certified environmental professional
- Copy submitted with Little E to OER
- Certification that CHASP will be implemented throughout construction
- Superintendent training required before work begins
- CHASP on-site during all construction activities
- Documentation of compliance throughout project

## Phase I Environmental Site Assessment

### Phase I ESA Purpose

A Phase I Environmental Site Assessment is the standard environmental investigation for real estate transactions and development projects. It:
- Documents environmental history of property
- Identifies recognized environmental conditions (RECs)
- Assesses potential for contamination
- Informs need for further investigation (Phase II)
- Provides baseline environmental documentation

### Phase I ESA Components

1. **Property History Research**
   - Title and deed research
   - Historical property uses (industry, commercial, institutional)
   - Historical maps and photographs
   - Prior environmental assessments or reports
   - Regulatory databases (brownfields, spills, violations)

2. **Regulatory Database Review**
   - EPA Environmental Compliance History Online (ECHO)
   - NYSDEC Environmental Database (DEC databases)
   - City records (ACRIS, ZoLa, DOE files)
   - Oil spill incident reports
   - RCRA and Superfund site listings

3. **Property Inspection**
   - Visual inspection of subject property
   - Adjacent property inspection for environmental hazards
   - Assessment of current conditions
   - Identification of environmental staining, odors, or obvious contamination
   - Photographic documentation

4. **Interviews**
   - Current property owner/operator interviews
   - Historical user interviews (if accessible)
   - Environmental professional assessment
   - Discussion of known environmental issues

5. **Assessment and Recommendations**
   - Identification of Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs)
   - Classification of findings (past REC, current REC, controlled REC)
   - Risk assessment
   - Recommendations for Phase II investigation (if warranted)

- Standard cost range: [VERIFY $2,000-$6,000] depending on property size and complexity
- Expedited Phase I available: [VERIFY 1 week turnaround, additional cost]

## Phase II Environmental Site Assessment

### Phase II ESA Purpose

A Phase II Environmental Site Assessment involves soil and groundwater testing to:
- Determine presence and extent of contamination
- Identify specific contaminants and concentration levels
- Evaluate regulatory compliance and risk
- Inform remediation planning
- Provide documentation for environmental approvals

### Phase II ESA Components

1. **Sampling Plan Development**
   - Determination of sampling locations based on Phase I findings
   - Selection of target contaminants (petroleum products, heavy metals, VOCs, etc.)
   - Sampling depth and density
   - Professional environmental engineer design

2. **Soil Sampling**
   - Boring locations established (grid-based or targeted)
   - Samples collected at various depths
   - Soil described and classified (soil type, color, odor, composition)
   - Visual inspection for staining, odors, or contamination
   - Chain of custody documentation

3. **Groundwater Testing** (if required)
   - Installation of monitoring wells
   - Groundwater elevation measurement
   - Groundwater sampling from wells
   - Assessment of groundwater flow direction and migration potential

4. **Laboratory Analysis**
   - Soil samples analyzed for target contaminants
   - Heavy metals (lead, chromium, arsenic, cadmium)
   - Petroleum hydrocarbons (gasoline, diesel, oil)
   - Volatile organic compounds (VOCs)
   - Other site-specific contaminants
   - Results compared to NYSDEC Remedial Standards

5. **Environmental Report**
   - Contamination identification and quantification
   - Comparison to regulatory standards
   - Risk assessment
   - Recommendations for remediation
   - Proposed Remedial Action Plan outline

### Phase II Timeline and Cost

- Field work duration: [VERIFY 2-3 weeks]
- Laboratory analysis: [VERIFY 2-4 weeks]
- Report preparation: [VERIFY 1-2 weeks]
- **Total timeline: 5-9 weeks**
- Cost range: [VERIFY $8,000-$25,000+] depending on site size, number of samples, contaminants

## Coordination with DOB

### DOB and OER Relationship

The Department of Buildings (DOB) and Office of Environmental Remediation (OER) coordinate on projects involving environmental conditions:
- **Sequential Approval**: OER approval MUST be obtained before DOB permit issuance
- **Prerequisite Requirement**: OER approval letter is required documentation for DOB permit application
- **Sign-Off Authority**: OER certification required for DOB to issue permits and CO

### DOB Permit Process with OER Component

1. **DOB Permit Application Submission**
   - Includes Little E submission package
   - References OER pre-approval or concurrent review
   - Lists environmental requirements and conditions

2. **DOB Examination**
   - Examines environmental documentation
   - Confirms OER approval letter included
   - Verifies CHASP completeness
   - May request clarifications or additional information

3. **OER Approval Letter**
   - Issued separately to applicant and DOB
   - Confirms environmental assessment and remediation approval
   - Identifies any conditions or requirements
   - Authorizes DOB to proceed with permit issuance

4. **DOB Permit Issuance**
   - Permit issued after OER approval confirmation
   - Permit references OER approval letter and conditions
   - CHASP requirements incorporated into permit conditions
   - OER monitoring conditions included in permit terms

### Timeline Impact of OER Approval

- OER review adds [VERIFY 6-12 weeks] to permit timeline
- Parallel OER and DOB review possible (can overlap 2-4 weeks)
- Sequential approval necessary for complex sites (adds full time)
- Expediting OER approval can reduce total timeline
- Early OER engagement recommended to identify issues before formal DOB submission

## Notice of Completion (NOC)

### NOC Definition and Purpose

A Notice of Completion is a formal document issued by OER certifying that:
- Environmental remediation has been completed
- Environmental conditions have been remediated to regulatory standards
- Site is acceptable for proposed use
- Institutional controls are in place (if applicable)
- Future development may proceed without further environmental restrictions (subject to conditions)

### When NOC is Issued

- **Completion of Remedial Action Plan (RAP)**: After all remediation work is finished
- **Verification Sampling**: After confirmation sampling demonstrates compliance with standards
- **Documentation and Certification**: Professional certification of completion
- **OER Field Verification**: OER inspector confirms remediation completion

### NOC Benefits

- Establishes regulatory closure of environmental issue
- Protects property owner from future liability (Brownfield status)
- Removes or modifies E-Designation restrictions
- Allows unrestricted development or use
- Required for some financing and insurance requirements

### NOC Timeline

- Obtainable only after remediation and verification sampling completed
- [VERIFY 2-4 weeks] after remediation work completion
- Field verification by OER: [VERIFY 1-2 weeks]
- Formal NOC issuance: [VERIFY 1-2 weeks after verification]

## Overall Timeline Estimates

### Typical Environmental Approval Timeline

**Simple Projects (Phase I only, no contamination):**
- Phase I ESA: 2-4 weeks
- OER review: 2-4 weeks
- **Total: 4-8 weeks**

**Standard Projects (Phase I and II required):**
- Phase I ESA: 2-4 weeks
- Phase II ESA: 5-9 weeks
- RAP development and review: 4-8 weeks
- CHASP preparation: 2-4 weeks
- OER approval: 4-12 weeks
- **Total: 6-12 months**

**Complex Projects (Significant contamination, remediation required):**
- Phase I and Phase II: 7-13 weeks
- RAP development: 8-12 weeks
- OER RAP review: 4-8 weeks
- Remediation execution: 4-16+ weeks (highly variable)
- Verification and NOC: 2-4 weeks
- **Total: 9-18+ months**

### Critical Path Timeline Considerations

- **Parallel Processing**: Phase I and DOB design can occur simultaneously
- **Phase II Timing**: Should not delay DOB submission if Phase I results expected; preliminary designs can proceed
- **CHASP Development**: Can be prepared concurrently with DOB design; does not add sequential time
- **OER Pre-Meetings**: Recommended 2-4 weeks before formal submission to identify issues and accelerate review
- **Multiple Review Cycles**: Deficiency comments typically require 2-4 week turnaround; budget for 1-2 cycles

## Best Practices for Expediting OER Approval

- **Engage environmental consultant early** (pre-design phase)
- **Conduct Phase I ESA immediately** upon property acquisition
- **Request preliminary OER meeting** before formal submission
- **Prepare comprehensive Phase II** addressing all identified RECs
- **Develop thorough CHASP** that demonstrates competence and completeness
- **Submit complete, professional documentation** to minimize deficiency cycles
- **Maintain communication with OER reviewer** for guidance during preparation
- **Use experienced environmental consultants** familiar with NYC OER process
- **Parallelize with DOB review** where possible
- **Budget 6-12+ months** for projects with environmental concerns
- **Consider expedited reviewers/consultants** for fast-track schedules

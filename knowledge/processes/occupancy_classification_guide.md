# Occupancy Classification Guide — NYC Building Code

**Last Updated:** February 2026
**Audience:** GLE Expediters, Project Managers, Architects
**Purpose:** Reference guide for NYC Building Code occupancy group classifications. Essential for DOB filings, code compliance, and project feasibility analysis.

---

## Overview: What is Occupancy Classification?

**Definition:** The NYC Building Code classifies all building uses into occupancy groups (A, B, E, F, H, I, M, R, S, U). Occupancy group determines:
- Fire protection requirements (sprinklers, fire alarm, emergency lighting)
- Egress requirements (stairs, corridors, exit widths)
- Accessibility standards
- Mechanical/electrical standards
- Building separation/fire-rating requirements
- Permitted uses in same building

**Key principle:** Occupancy is **the use of the space**, not the building type. A converted warehouse can be R-2 (residential), M (retail), or B (office) depending on use.

**GLE role:** Identifying correct occupancy classification is critical for feasibility analysis, code strategy, and DOB filing.

## Group A: Assembly

**Definition:** Spaces designed for gathering of 50 or more occupants; includes food/beverage consumption and entertainment.

### A-1: Fixed Seating Assembly

**Spaces:** Theater, concert hall, cinema, auditorium, arena (fixed seats).

**Characteristics:**
- Seats bolted or in rows
- Stage or performance area
- Professional lighting/sound
- Occupancy: 100–5,000+ persons

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Required (almost always)
- Emergency lighting: Required
- Exit signage: Illuminated, multiple egress
- Egress width: Strictly calculated (occupant load × 0.0033 inches/person)
- Stage curtain: Fire-rated if present

**GLE scenario:** Theater renovation or new construction. Major fire protection work required. Often requires ALT-2 or NB with full design review.

---

### A-2: Food/Beverage Consumption with Entertainment

**Spaces:** Nightclub, dance club, bar, restaurant with live music/dancing, lounge.

**Characteristics:**
- Alcohol service (often)
- Live entertainment or dance floor
- Occupancy load variable (100–1,000+ persons)
- Night use patterns

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Required (almost always)
- Emergency lighting: Required
- Occupant load sign: Posted at entrance
- Egress: Multiple exits required; no dead-end corridors
- Accessibility: ADA-required (despite late hours)

**GLE scenario:** Converting office/warehouse to nightclub, or renovating existing bar. Major sprinkler and egress work typical. Licensing from State Liquor Authority separate from DOB.

---

### A-3: General Assembly (Worship, Recreation, Other)

**Spaces:** Church, mosque, temple, community center, dance studio, gym (if <5,000 SF), banquet hall, conference center, airport terminal.

**Characteristics:**
- Religious, recreational, cultural use
- Variable occupancy (50–2,000+ persons)
- Equipment varies (pews, chairs, mats, training equipment)

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Required in larger spaces (>5,000 SF) or if occupancy >300
- Emergency lighting: Required if occupancy >300
- Egress: Multiple exits; door swing direction varies
- Accessibility: Full ADA compliance

**GLE scenario:** Religious building renovation, community center alteration, dance studio conversion. Feasibility depends on existing egress.

---

### A-4: Indoor Sporting Events (Fixed Seating)

**Spaces:** Gymnasium with bleachers, bowling alley, ice rink, pool (with spectator seating).

### A-5: Outdoor Assembly

**Spaces:** Amphitheater, stadium, outdoor fair, amusement park, theme park grounds.

**Characteristics:**
- Open-air seating or standing
- Weather-exposed
- Occupancy: 500–100,000+ persons

**Code implications:** Egress and safety primarily through ground management, not building code. DOB involvement minimal.

## Group F: Factory and Industrial

**Definition:** Manufacturing, assembly, warehousing, industrial processing.

### F-1: Moderate Hazard Occupancy

**Spaces:** Auto body shop, brewery, printing plant, electronics assembly, food processing, furniture manufacturing, pharmaceutical lab (non-hazmat).

**Characteristics:**
- Moderate fire/explosion risk
- Machinery, processing equipment
- Industrial workers

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Required for larger spaces (>3,500 SF in some cases)
- Storage height: Limited for flammable materials
- Occupant load: Usually lower than office (1 person per 100–150 SF)

---

### F-2: Low Hazard Occupancy

**Spaces:** Warehousing (non-hazardous goods), storage, packaging, light assembly, dry cleaning plant, laundry.

**Characteristics:**
- Minimal fire risk
- Storage or light processing
- Few workers

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Generally required but less restrictive than F-1
- Storage racks: Height limits (typically 15–20 ft without sprinklers)
- Occupant load: Very low (1 person per 300+ SF)

---

## Group H: High-Hazard

**Definition:** Explosives, toxic chemicals, flammable liquids (large quantities), hazmat manufacturing.

**NYC context:** Rare in urban areas. Most hazmat facilities located outside city limits.

**GLE relevance:** Minimal unless client is chemical manufacturer or hazmat storage operator.

---

## Group I: Institutional (Care Occupancy)

**Definition:** Buildings where occupants are housed/cared for, with limited ability to self-evacuate.

### I-1: Supervised Residential

**Spaces:** Nursing home, assisted living, group home (adults), memory care, facility for developmentally disabled.

**Characteristics:**
- 24-hour care staffing
- Residents may have mobility/cognitive limitations
- Medical services available on-site

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Required
- Exit signage: Clear, illuminated
- Accessible units: ADA-required
- Staffing: Code requires staff present (24-hour)

---

### I-2: Hospital/Medical Care

**Spaces:** Hospital, surgical center, detox facility, psychiatric hospital.

**Characteristics:**
- Medical/surgical care provided
- Patient monitoring equipment
- High-acuity care

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Required
- Fire-rated construction: Extensive
- Medical gas systems: Specialized design
- Emergency power: Required

**GLE scenario:** Hospital renovation or new wing. Highly regulated; also involves DOH, state health department, and CMS. Beyond typical GLE scope but may arise.

---

### I-3: Detention/Correctional

**Spaces:** Prison, jail, police holding cells.

**Characteristics:**
- Secured occupants
- Law enforcement presence
- Limited self-evacuation

**Code implications:** Heavy fire rating, secured egress, monitoring systems.

**GLE scenario:** Very rare in NYC.

---

### I-4: Residential Care Facilities

**Spaces:** Group home (6–16 adults with 24-hour supervision, non-ambulatory).

**Characteristics:**
- Limited mobility/cognitive capacity
- Staff-supervised
- Residential setting

## Group R: Residential

**Definition:** Buildings for permanent or transient sleeping/living. Largest occupancy group in NYC.

### R-1: Transient Residential

**Spaces:** Hotel, motel, inn, short-term rental, dormitory.

**Characteristics:**
- Temporary stay (days to weeks)
- Guest may not know building layout
- Central services (housekeeping, reception)

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Required (almost always)
- Egress signage: Extra visibility required (guests unfamiliar)
- Emergency lighting: Required on all floors
- Accessible units: Minimum % required (5–10% depending on occupancy)

**R-1 vs. R-2:** Occupancy determined by **intent** (short-term vs. permanent). If lease is month-to-month or longer = R-2. If booking is under 30 days = R-1.

---

### R-2: Permanent Residential (Multi-unit)

**Spaces:** Apartment building, condo, townhouse (3+ units), long-term rental, co-op.

**Characteristics:**
- Permanent residence (lease ≥ 30 days, typically 12 months)
- Multiple dwelling units (minimum 3)
- Shared common areas (lobby, stairwell)

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: [VERIFY: Required for all R-2 in NYC? Or only >7 stories/by district?]
- Fire rating: Apartment walls typically 1-hour minimum
- Egress: Stairwells serve max. 4 apartments per landing
- Emergency lighting: Common areas lit 24/7
- Accessible units: Minimum % required

**GLE scenario:** Most common GLE work. Renovation of apartments, conversions to R-2, combining/dividing units.

---

### R-3: One-/Two-Family and Transient Lodging

**Spaces:** Single-family home, duplex, townhouse (2 units), adult family home (max 8 persons with supervision).

**Characteristics:**
- Low occupancy density
- Owner-occupied or limited residents
- Minimal shared areas

**Code implications:**
- Sprinklers: Generally NOT required
- Fire rating: Lower requirements
- Egress: Simpler (single/double stairs often sufficient)
- Accessibility: Not required in single-family homes

## Change of Occupancy: ALT-1 vs. ALT-2

### When Change of Occupancy Triggers DOB Filing

**Occupancy change** = existing building's use changes to different occupancy group (e.g., office B → apartment R-2, warehouse F → restaurant A-2).

**DOB filing required:** ALT-2 (Alteration Type 2) minimum.

**Why?** Change of occupancy may trigger new code requirements (sprinklers, egress, accessibility, fire rating, mechanical systems).

### Common GLE Occupancy Changes

| Conversion | Cost Implication | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| **Office (B) → Apartments (R-2)** | Sprinklers, egress, kitchen/bath plumbing | 6–9 months |
| **Warehouse (F) → Apartments (R-2)** | Major work: Article 7-B MDL sprinklers, egress, utilities | 12–18 months |
| **Retail (M) → Restaurant (A-2)** | Sprinklers, kitchen hood, grease trap, increased egress | 4–6 months |
| **Office (B) → Retail (M)** | Minimal; egress may adjust | 2–3 months |
| **Warehouse (F) → Office (B)** | Mechanical/electrical upgrade, egress | 4–6 months |

**GLE strategy:** Identify occupancy change early in design. It's often the biggest cost driver.

## Mixed Occupancy Buildings

**Definition:** One building with multiple occupancy groups (retail on ground, offices above; residential with ground-floor restaurant, etc.).

### Separated vs. Non-Separated Occupancy

**Separated:** Each occupancy group in separate, fully enclosed tenant spaces with no shared areas (except common lobby).

**Non-separated:** Occupancies adjacent/shared (e.g., single-story retail + office).

**Code implications:**
- **Separated:** Each occupancy follows its own code rules (spray foam + sprinklers in office; regular ceiling in retail if separated)
- **Non-separated:** Most restrictive occupancy rules apply to entire space (mixed retail/office = office rules in retail area too)

**GLE scenario:** Mixed-use building. Common in NYC (residential over retail). Careful design required to separate occupancies and minimize code work.

### Pitfall 2: Gym in Basement — A-3 or B Occupancy?

**Team question:** "15,000 SF gym for employees in cellar — is that A-3 or B?"

**Issue:** Gym = assembly (A-3) if open to public or 50+ occupants. Gym = B (business fitness center) if only for tenants/employees and <50 at a time.

**Answer:**
- **A-3:** Public gym, class-based fitness facility with 50+ occupancy load
- **B:** Employee gym, fitness room for 10–20 employees, no public access

### Pitfall 3: Daycare Occupancy and Outdoor Space

**Team question:** "Daycare outdoor play area requirement — DOB or DOH?"

**Answer:**
- **DOB:** Building code (egress, bathrooms, sprinklers in E-classified building)
- **DOH:** Health/safety (play space SF, fencing, sanitation)
- Both required for compliant daycare

**GLE role:** Know which agency regulates what. Don't assume all daycare requirements are DOB code.

### Pitfall 4: Group Home (R-3) vs. Group Care Facility (I-1)

**Confusion:** When does a residential group home become a care facility?

**Answer:**
- **R-3 Group Home:** 3–8 disabled/elderly residents with supervision; minimal staff
- **I-1 Care Facility:** 5+ residents with 24-hour medical/nursing care; staffed

**GLE scenario:** Client converting house to group home (R-3, minimal code work). If client later expands to 10 residents + nursing → becomes I-1 (major work). Major cost difference.

### Pitfall 5: Hotel (R-1) vs. Long-Term Rental (R-2)

**Confusion:** When does a hotel become an apartment building?

**Answer:** Determined by **lease term intent**, not physical building:
- **R-1 (Hotel):** Short-term bookings (1 day – 30 days); guest may not have permanent address
- **R-2 (Apartments):** Long-term leases (12 months typical); permanent residence

**GLE scenario:** Client operates building as hotel (R-1 code compliance, no kitchens) but wants to lease units monthly for "extended stay." If majority of units ≥30 days continuously occupied → DOB may reclassify to R-2 (triggers kitchen requirements). Major retrofit.

## Quick Reference: GLE Occupancy Questions

**Q: How do I know if my project requires sprinklers?**
A: Occupancy class determines sprinkler triggers. A, I, E → almost always. R-2 → [VERIFY: if >7 stories or all buildings?]. B, M → only if size >threshold (varies). Never assume; consult code for your specific occupancy + size + construction type.

**Q: If I'm converting office to apartments, what's the biggest cost?**
A: Sprinklers (if not already present) + egress (stairwell sizing) + plumbing (kitchens, 2 baths per unit typical). Budget 30–50% of total project cost for change of occupancy work.

**Q: Can a warehouse stay F-2 if I add an office upstairs?**
A: No. If one story is office (B) and one is warehouse (F-2), entire building must meet B occupancy rules (more restrictive). Design as mixed-use B/F-2 separated (if possible).

**Q: What if DOB and I disagree on occupancy classification?**
A: Request "occupancy classification letter" from DOB before design/costing. Settle dispute early. If DOB insists on more restrictive occupancy, major cost implications.

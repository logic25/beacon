# DOT Permits Comprehensive Guide for NYC Expediting

## Overview

This guide covers all major Department of Transportation (DOT) permits and approvals required for construction and building projects in New York City. Each permit type has distinct requirements, timelines, and fees. Proper understanding and early application submission are critical to avoiding project delays.

---

## 1. DOT Sidewalk Permit (Pedestrian Protection/Construction Fence Permit)

### When It's Needed

- When construction work affects any public sidewalk area
- When a construction fence or protective barrier is required adjacent to property
- When scaffolding occupies sidewalk space
- When hoisting equipment extends over or near the sidewalk
- When pedestrian safety barriers are needed during construction

### What It Covers

The sidewalk permit authorizes temporary occupation of public right-of-way for:
- Construction fencing and barricades
- Pedestrian protective structures (shed, bridge, or marquee)
- Temporary sidewalk closing (with DOT approval)
- Temporary street use for materials staging

### Step-by-Step Filing Process

1. **Prepare Application Package**
   - Completed application (Form DOT-101 or online portal submission) [VERIFY current form number]
   - Detailed site plan showing sidewalk dimensions, work area, fence/barrier placement
   - Insurance certificate (General Liability minimum $1,000,000) [VERIFY current minimum]
   - Letter of Authorization if filed by expediter
   - Proof of property ownership or lessee documentation

2. **Submit Application**
   - Online: DOT Permit and Franchise Management System (PFMS) at dot.nyc.gov
   - In-person: DOT Manhattan Permit office (East Street location) [VERIFY current address]
   - By mail: DOT Permits Division (address on form)
   - Include check or credit card payment for fees

3. **DOT Review & Inspection**
   - DOT reviews for safety and compliance (3-5 business days typical)
   - Field inspection may be scheduled
   - DOT may request revisions to plans
   - Approval issued as permit with conditions and tapeline date

4. **Site Tapeline (Mandatory)**
   - Scheduled after permit approval
   - DOT inspector marks actual sidewalk boundaries
   - Determines exact permitted area and fence placement
   - Cannot begin sidewalk work until tapeline completed

5. **Ongoing Compliance**
   - Display permit number on site
   - Maintain insurance throughout permit period
   - Notify DOT of work changes
   - Annual renewal may be required for long-term projects

### Required Documents Checklist

- [ ] DOT application form (completed)
- [ ] Site plan (minimum 1:8 scale, dimensions marked)
- [ ] Insurance certificate naming NYC as additional insured
- [ ] Property deed or lease (if expediter filed)
- [ ] Letter of Authorization (notarized)
- [ ] Payment (check or credit card authorization)
- [ ] If scaffolding: Engineering cert of compliance
- [ ] If hoisting: Hoisting plan from licensed engineer

### Fees

- **Initial Permit**: $500-$800 depending on scope [VERIFY with PFMS]
- **Annual Renewal**: $250-$400 [VERIFY]
- **Expedited Review** (5 business days): Additional $500 [VERIFY]
- **Tapeline Inspection**: Included in permit fee

**Payment Methods**: Check, money order, credit card (online)

### Typical Timeline

- **Standard Track**: 5-10 business days from complete submission to approval
- **Expedited Track**: 2-3 business days (with expedited fee)
- **Field Inspection/Tapeline**: Schedule within 10 days of approval
- **Total Project Time**: 15-20 business days (can be concurrent with other permits)

### Common Issues & Solutions

| Issue | Cause | Solution |
|-------|-------|----------|
| Incomplete site plans | Missing dimensions or scale | Provide clear, dimensioned plans; include north arrow |
| Insurance gaps | Certificate expired or lacks endorsements | Renewal before submission; ensure NYC is additional insured |
| Site access problems | Property boundaries unclear | Provide deed or lease; coordinate building access |
| Tapeline conflicts | Fence placement infeasible | Modify plan before tapeline; coordinate with building |
| Permit delays during winter | Freeze restrictions [VERIFY] | Submit early; plan around seasonal backlogs |

### Key Compliance Notes

- **No work begins until tapeline completed** - critical requirement
- Insurance must remain active throughout permit period
- Fence must match approved plan specifications
- Monthly inspections may be conducted (especially for long-duration permits)
- Sidewalk must be maintained clear unless explicitly approved otherwise
- Permit modifications require DOT amendment (fee applies)

## 2. DOT IBM Number (Inspection Barcode Management)

### What It Is

An IBM Number is a unique identifier issued by DOT for tracking inspections and permits related to construction activity on NYC streets and right-of-way. It serves as a critical reference number linking all DOT permits, inspections, and enforcement actions for a specific project.

**IBM = Inspection Barcode Management identifier**

### When It's Needed

- For ANY street opening permit or sidewalk work permit
- When DOT needs to track multiple permits for one location
- Required for coordination between DOT and NYDOT field inspectors
- Mandatory for utility companies conducting underground work
- For projects requiring multiple DOT approvals over extended periods

### What It's Used For

- Unique project identification in DOT database
- Field inspector reference for site inspections
- Violation and violation removal tracking
- Permit amendment documentation
- Coordination between DOT divisions and agencies

### How to Obtain

#### Method 1: DOT Permit Portal
1. Access DOT PFMS at dot.nyc.gov/permits
2. Create account (if new user) - requires name, email, phone
3. Submit initial permit application (Sidewalk, Street Opening, etc.)
4. Upon application validation, IBM number auto-generates
5. IBM number displays in confirmation email and permit documentation

#### Method 2: In-Person (if Portal Access Issues)
1. Visit DOT Manhattan Permit Office with:
   - Photo ID
   - Property information or address
   - Applicant name and entity
2. Staff generates IBM number and provides documentation
3. May take 1-2 hours for walk-in service [VERIFY]

#### Method 3: Telephone
1. Call DOT Permits Division [VERIFY phone number]
2. Provide property address and project type
3. Representative issues IBM number over phone
4. Confirmation sent via mail or email

### IBM Number Format & Structure

- **Format**: Typically 8-10 digit alphanumeric code [VERIFY exact format]
- **Example**: NYC-2025-00012345 [VERIFY format]
- **Validity**: Remains active for up to 5 years from issuance (or project completion)
- **Transferability**: Cannot be transferred between locations or projects

### Cost

- **IBM Number Generation**: FREE (no separate fee)
- Included with any DOT permit application submission
- No additional charge if obtained independently

### Common Applications

- Tracking street opening permits for utility work
- Coordinating multiple DOT approvals at single address
- Managing sidewalk permit renewals
- Documenting utility locate requests (utilities use IBM number)
- Building inspector references for DOT coordination

### Key Points for Expediters

- Always request IBM number before submitting follow-up permits
- Include IBM number on all related DOT correspondence
- IBM number should appear on all field site documentation
- Missing IBM number often causes processing delays
- Utilities must reference IBM number for locate requests

## 3. DOT Vault Approval (Subsurface Vault License)

### What Vault Space Is

Vault space is *privately owned, underground area beneath the public right-of-way* that extends under the sidewalk into subsurface space. Vaults are commonly used for:
- Basement cooling systems
- Mechanical/HVAC equipment storage
- Utility infrastructure
- Structural supports
- Storage of building systems extending beneath sidewalk

### When Approval Is Needed

- When building design incorporates any subsurface space that extends beneath public right-of-way
- When mechanical systems, pipes, or conduits run under sidewalk
- When foundation design extends below street level into vault space
- When utility connections require subsurface right-of-way access
- For renovation projects modifying basement/subsurface areas

**Critical**: Even slight extension beneath public right-of-way requires DOT vault approval.

### Vault Types & Definitions

1. **Structural Vault** - Building structure extends beneath sidewalk
2. **Non-Structural Vault** - Mechanical/utility infrastructure only
3. **Utility Vault** - Water, sewer, electric lines under sidewalk
4. **Newly Created Vault** - New excavation creating subsurface space

### Filing Process (Detailed Steps)

#### Phase 1: Pre-Application Review
1. **Engage DOT Early**
   - Contact DOT Bureau of Plan Review before detailed design
   - Provide site address and general project scope
   - Determine if vault approval is necessary
   - Identify preliminary requirements

2. **Prepare Engineering Documentation**
   - Building architect/engineer produces:
     - Site plan (1" = 20' or smaller scale) showing vault extents
     - Basement floor plan with dimensions
     - Cross-section showing subsurface depth beneath sidewalk
     - Structural calculation for vault (if structural)
     - Utility routing diagrams (if mechanical/utilities)
     - Measurements of existing vault space (if modification)

3. **Assess Existing Conditions**
   - Check DOT Vault Book (available at DOT office) for existing vaults at site
   - Conduct survey of current subsurface conditions
   - Identify existing utilities (Con Edison, water, sewer, telecom)
   - Determine depth to bedrock or water table

#### Phase 2: Application Submission

1. **Prepare Complete Application Package**
   - DOT Vault Approval Application Form [VERIFY current form]
   - Architectural and engineering drawings (as above)
   - Deed or ownership documentation
   - Letter of Authorization (if expediter filing)
   - Insurance certificate
   - Environmental assessment (if required) [VERIFY conditions]
   - Utility approval letters (if applicable)
   - Payment for application fee

2. **Submit Application**
   - **In-person**: DOT Bureau of Plan Review, [VERIFY location and hours]
   - **By mail**: DOT Permits Division, [VERIFY current mailing address]
   - **Online**: DOT PFMS portal (if available for vault approvals) [VERIFY]
   - Request date-stamped receipt

3. **DOT Plan Review**
   - DOT reviews for:
     - Sidewalk integrity and structural impact
     - Utility clearances
     - Public safety and pedestrian access
     - Compliance with DOT standards
   - First review: 10-15 business days [VERIFY]
   - Typically requires revisions (2-3 round-trips)

#### Phase 3: Approval & Conditions

1. **Conditional Approval**
   - DOT issues approval with requirements:
     - Specific construction methodology
     - Monitoring and inspection schedule
     - Insurance and bonding requirements
     - Utility confirmation before construction
     - Construction sequencing conditions

2. **Compliance Documentation**
   - Receive DOT letter of approval
   - Document all conditions
   - Obtain all required utility letters
   - File copy with Building Dept (NYC Department of Buildings)
   - Provide copy to contractor before work begins

#### Phase 4: Construction & Inspection

1. **Pre-Construction**
   - Notify DOT (typically 5-10 days before work)
   - Confirm all utility locations marked
   - Schedule mandatory DOT inspection

2. **During Construction**
   - DOT field inspector visits periodically
   - Contractor must notify DOT of progress
   - Any modifications require DOT approval amendment
   - Photographic documentation required

3. **Post-Construction**
   - Final inspection by DOT inspector
   - Certification that work completed per approval
   - DOT closure of vault approval file

### Required Documents Checklist

- [ ] Completed vault approval application
- [ ] Deed or proof of ownership/control
- [ ] Architect/engineer signed vault drawings
- [ ] Structural calculations (if applicable)
- [ ] Utility coordination letters
- [ ] Environmental assessment (if required)
- [ ] Insurance certificate
- [ ] Letter of Authorization (notarized, if expediter filed)
- [ ] Payment proof (check, money order, or card authorization)
- [ ] Title/survey showing property boundaries

### Fees

- **New Vault Approval Application**: $1,500-$2,500 depending on complexity [VERIFY with DOT]
- **Vault Modification**: $800-$1,500 [VERIFY]
- **Inspection Fees**: Included in application fee
- **Vault Renewal/Extension**: $500-$1,000 [VERIFY conditions and timeline]

**Payment Methods**: Check, money order, credit card (online if portal available)

**Note**: Fees are non-refundable if application is withdrawn.

### Typical Timeline

| Phase | Duration | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Pre-application consultation | 1-2 weeks | Early engagement recommended |
| Drawings & engineering | 4-8 weeks | Dependent on project complexity |
| Initial submission to DOT | Immediate | Date-stamped receipt |
| First DOT review | 10-15 business days | Usually requires revisions |
| Revisions & resubmission | 1-3 rounds | Each round 10-15 days |
| Conditional approval issued | 2-4 weeks | After final revisions accepted |
| **Total pre-construction** | **3-4 months** | Can overlap with other permits |
| Construction period | Project-dependent | DOT inspections ongoing |
| Final approval/closure | 1-2 weeks post-completion | After final inspection |

**Note**: Vault approvals are one of the longest DOT processes. Plan accordingly.

### Common Issues & Solutions

| Issue | Cause | Solution |
|-------|-------|----------|
| Repeated revision requests | Incomplete or unclear drawings | Provide 3-view engineering documentation; coordinate with DOT early |
| Utility conflicts | Existing utilities in vault area | Obtain Con Ed, water, sewer, telecom confirmation before submitting |
| Structural concerns | Inadequate calculations | Retain qualified structural engineer; provide all calcs upfront |
| Timeline delays | Multiple revision rounds | Anticipate 2-3 revision cycles; budget 4-6 months minimum |
| Condition conflicts | Approval conditions incompatible with design | Engage DOT during design phase, not after |
| Insurance disputes | Certificate errors or missing endorsements | Verify NYC additional insured status; use DOT template language |

### Special Considerations

**Existing Vaults**: If property has pre-existing vault space, DOT Vault Book provides history. Modifications require new approval even if vault already exists.

**Historic Districts**: Additional landmarks preservation review may be required; coordinate with LPC early.

**Water/Sewer Impact**: If vault near water or sewer lines, DEP (Department of Environmental Protection) coordination often required [VERIFY].

**Structural Impact**: Vaults affecting building structural integrity require detailed structural analysis and PE seal.

### Key Compliance Notes

- Vault approval is **NOT** transferable to future owners without amendment
- Compliance with all DOT conditions is mandatory during construction
- Failure to follow conditions can result in stop-work orders
- Vault space must be maintained in approved condition indefinitely
- Changes to vault use after approval require new DOT amendment application

## 4. DOT Revocable Consent (Public Right-of-Way Encroachment License)

### What Revocable Consent Is

Revocable Consent is a formal license granted by the NYC Department of Transportation that **allows a private party to occupy and use public right-of-way space** on a permanent or long-term basis. Unlike temporary permits, revocable consent authorizes ongoing encroachment into public streets, sidewalks, or other DOT-controlled areas.

**Key characteristic**: The permission is "revocable," meaning NYC can terminate the license at any time for public benefit.

### When Revocable Consent Is Required

**Projections into Public Right-of-Way** - any permanent structure extending into public space:

- **Cornices & Architectural Features**
  - Building cornices projecting more than 1-2 feet over sidewalk
  - Ornamental brackets, sculptural elements, or architectural details
  - Facade elements projecting into right-of-way

- **Mechanical Equipment**
  - Air conditioning units mounted on facade extending into street
  - Rooftop equipment overhanging public space
  - Ventilation ducts, exhaust vents, or louvers projecting outward

- **Canopies & Awnings**
  - Permanent or long-term (>1 year) canopies over public sidewalk
  - Marquees or signage structures
  - Covered entryway features

- **Utilities & Infrastructure**
  - Underground utility vaults (different from vault approval, see below)
  - Utility boxes or equipment cabinets in right-of-way
  - Electrical, telecommunications, or cable infrastructure

- **Streetscape Improvements**
  - Outdoor seating (cafe seating extending into sidewalk)
  - Planters or landscaping features
  - Street-level retail or restaurant features

- **Other Encroachments**
  - Basement or cellar windows extending into right-of-way
  - Stairs, ramps, or platforms over public space
  - Any privately-controlled space below or above public sidewalk/street

### Franchise vs. Concession vs. Revocable Consent

**Important Distinction**: DOT issues different types of authorizations:

| Type | Duration | Scope | Fee Structure | Process |
|------|----------|-------|---|---------|
| **Revocable Consent** | Permanent (but revocable) | Projections, encroachments, permanent use | One-time + annual | Standard DOT approval |
| **Franchise** | Long-term (typically 20+ years) | Utility operations, telecom, underground infrastructure | Annual fees, franchise fees | Mayoral approval required; negotiated terms |
| **Concession** | Medium-term (5-10 years) | Restaurants, retail, temporary commercial use | Bid/negotiated fees | Competitive bidding process; City Council approval may apply |

**For architectural projections**: Typically use Revocable Consent (not Franchise).

### Lengthy Approval Process (Complete Timeline)

The revocable consent process is one of NYC's longest permitting processes, often taking **6-12 months or longer**.

#### Phase 1: Pre-Application (Weeks 1-4)

1. **Consult with DOT**
   - Schedule pre-application meeting with DOT Plan Review Bureau
   - Present proposed projection/encroachment

2. **Gather Site Information**
   - Property deed and ownership documentation
   - Existing survey of property

#### Phase 2: Detailed Planning & Preparation (Weeks 4-12)

1. **Produce Detailed Drawings**
   - Architectural drawings showing:
     - Projection dimensions and specifications
     - Exact location on facade
     - Height above sidewalk
     - Distance from street curb
     - Materials and construction details
     - Scale drawings at 1/4" = 1' minimum [VERIFY]
   - Site plan showing:
     - Property boundaries
     - Public right-of-way boundaries
     - Projection extent into public space
     - Utilities and existing infrastructure
     - North arrow and scale

2. **Prepare Application Narrative**
   - Describe the encroachment/projection
   - Explain necessity or public benefit
   - Address public safety and pedestrian access
   - Provide historical context if applicable (existing projection being replaced)
   - Demonstrate compliance with DOT standards

3. **Financial Preparation**
   - Application fee (paid at submission)
   - Estimate annual maintenance fees
   - Potential legal fees (DOT may have specific requirements)
   - Establish insurance and bonding capability

#### Phase 3: Application Submission (Weeks 12-14)

1. **Assemble Complete Package**
   - Revocable Consent application form (DOT form [VERIFY number])
   - Deed or ownership proof
   - Detailed architectural drawings (2+ sets)
   - Site plan and survey
   - Application narrative (typed, professional)
   - Insurance certificate (General Liability $1,000,000+ [VERIFY])

   - Application fee (check or money order)
   - Environmental assessment (if required) [VERIFY]

2. **Submit to DOT**
   - In-person: DOT Plan Review Bureau (Manhattan office)
   - By mail: Certified mail with return receipt
   - By messenger: Same-day delivery preferred
   - Request date-stamped receipt and confirmation number
   - Retain copy of application for records

3. **DOT Initial Review**
   - DOT confirms receipt (within 3-5 business days)
   - Preliminary assessment for completeness
   - If incomplete, DOT issues deficiency notice (typically requires 1-2 week response)

#### Phase 4: Formal Review Period (Weeks 14-20)

1. **DOT Plan Review**
   - DOT evaluates for:
     - Pedestrian safety impacts
     - Utility conflicts
     - Structural integrity standards
     - Historical/landmark implications
     - Public interest considerations
   - Typical review: 15-30 days
   - DOT usually issues "comments" requesting revisions

2. **Handle DOT Comments & Revisions**
   - Review DOT comments carefully
   - Coordinate with architect for design adjustments
   - Resubmit revised drawings addressing all comments
   - Typical revision cycle: 2-3 rounds, each 15-30 days

3. **Agency Coordination**
   - If in historic district: Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) review required
   - If near utilities: Con Edison, water, sewer, telecom coordination
   - If structural: Department of Buildings (DOB) coordination
   - Each agency review adds 2-4 weeks

#### Phase 5: City Council Notification/Approval (Weeks 20-40)

1. **City Council District Notification** [VERIFY current requirements]
   - DOT notifies local City Council member of proposed encroachment
   - Typical notification: 30-day review period
   - City Council can:
     - Approve (simple notification)
     - Conditionally approve (with requirements)
     - Oppose (requires justification to DOT; DOT makes final decision)

2. **Community Board Review** (if required) [VERIFY]
   - Some encroachments require Community Board review
   - Typical review: 2-3 weeks
   - Conditional approvals can add requirements

3. **Public Hearing** (if required for major encroachments) [VERIFY]
   - Significant encroachments may require DOT public hearing
   - Typically held in affected City Council district
   - Public can comment for/against
   - Additional 4-6 weeks for hearing scheduling and process

#### Phase 6: DOT Final Approval (Weeks 40-52+)

1. **Conditional Approval Letter**
   - DOT issues revocable consent approval letter
   - Details all conditions and requirements
   - Specifies annual maintenance obligations
   - Sets duration (often indefinite but subject to revocation)

2. **Conditions Typically Include**
   - Specific materials and maintenance standards
   - Insurance and bonding requirements throughout duration
   - Maintenance schedule (frequency of inspections, repairs)
   - Liability and indemnification
   - Removal obligation (applicant must remove at own expense if consent revoked)
   - Annual certification of compliance
   - Fee payment schedule (if applicable)

3. **Execute Required Documents**
   - Sign Revocable Consent Agreement (provided by DOT)
   - Establish required insurance and bonding
   - Obtain any remaining agency approvals

### Franchise Procedures (for Utility/Long-Term Encroachments)

**When Franchise Instead of Revocable Consent**: Utility companies, telecommunications companies, and long-term infrastructure often use **Franchise Agreements** rather than Revocable Consent.

**Franchise Process Differs**:
- Typically 20-50 year duration
- Requires Mayoral approval (not just DOT)
- May require City Council approval/vote
- Negotiated terms and annual payments
- More formal legal documentation
- Can be subject to competitive bidding

**Franchise vs. Revocable Consent Decision**: DOT determines whether encroachment qualifies for standard Revocable Consent or requires Franchise structure based on:
- Duration of intended use
- Scope and significance of encroachment
- Type of entity (private, utility, etc.)
- Public interest considerations

**Expediter Note**: Most architectural projections use Revocable Consent; utilities typically use Franchises.

### Common Issues & Solutions

| Issue | Cause | Solution |
|-------|-------|----------|
| Extended timeline | Multiple revision rounds | Provide complete, detailed drawings upfront; anticipate 2-3 revision cycles |
| City Council opposition | Community concerns about safety/aesthetics | Early community outreach; modify design if opposition justified |
| LPC conflicts | Historic district restrictions | Engage LPC early; select materials/design compatible with historic context |
| Utility conflicts | Existing underground/aerial utilities in projection area | Conduct utility locates before submitting; coordinate directly with utility companies |
| Structural issues | Inadequate support for projection | Retain structural engineer; provide detailed load calculations |
| Insurance obstacles | Certificate issues or missing endorsements | Use DOT template language; specify NYC as additional insured; verify coverage limits |
| Approval delays | Administrative processing backlogs | Submit during off-peak periods (avoid summer/year-end); follow up monthly with DOT |

### Special Circumstances

**Cornices with Unsafe Conditions**: If existing cornice is deteriorated, DOT may require replacement as condition of consent. Budget for this contingency.

**Historic Districts**: Revocable Consent in historic district requires LPC approval BEFORE DOT final approval. Plan for parallel LPC process (adds 6-8 weeks minimum).

**Utility Relocations**: If proposed encroachment requires utility relocation, coordinate directly with utility company; obtain written confirmation before submitting to DOT.

**Multiple Properties**: Adjacent property owners cannot share single revocable consent. Separate applications required for each property.

### Key Compliance Notes

- Revocable Consent is **NOT** a right; it can be revoked by NYC at any time
- Applicant is responsible for full removal of encroachment if consent revoked
- Annual compliance certification required; failure to certify can result in revocation
- Changes to encroachment require amendment application (fee applies)
- Revocable Consent terminates upon property ownership change (new owner must reapply) [VERIFY]
- Maintenance standards are mandatory; neglect can trigger enforcement action
- Annual fees must be paid continuously to maintain active consent

## 5. DOT New Building Meeting (Sidewalk/Public Right-of-Way Coordination)

### When Required

A DOT New Building Meeting (also called "Coordination Meeting" or "Pre-Construction Meeting") is required when:

- **All new building projects** in NYC requiring City permits
- **Major renovations** affecting building facade, entrance, or sidewalk area
- **Change of Use** in existing buildings (sometimes required)
- **Excavation work** extending beyond property line into right-of-way
- Any project requiring DOT approval, permits, or right-of-way use

**Typical Timing**: Scheduled after building permits are obtained from NYC Department of Buildings (DOB) but **before** construction begins.

### What the Meeting Covers

The DOT New Building Meeting coordinates:

1. **Sidewalk/Curb Issues**
   - Curb cuts and utility access
   - Scaffolding and protective structures
   - Sidewalk vault or encroachment locations
   - Pedestrian access during construction

2. **Street Access & Logistics**
   - Construction truck routes and parking restrictions
   - Material delivery schedule
   - Crane operations and overhead work
   - Work hour restrictions

3. **Hoisting & Equipment**
   - Crane permit coordination
   - Overheight load procedures
   - Street closure procedures (if needed)
   - Load bearing capacity of streets

4. **Utility Coordination**
   - Water, sewer, electric, gas, telecom locations
   - Utility shutoffs or relocations
   - Con Edison coordination (if applicable)
   - DEP water/sewer coordination (if applicable)

5. **Public Space Protection**
   - Pedestrian barriers and traffic control
   - ADA compliance on sidewalks
   - Noise and air quality requirements
   - Night work restrictions

6. **Permits to Schedule**
   - Sidewalk permits
   - Street opening permits
   - Crane permits
   - Revocable consent (if applicable)

### Relationship to Building Permits & BPP

**Timeline Context**:
```
Building Design → Building Permit (DOB) → DOT New Building Meeting → Construction Start
```

**Before DOT Meeting**:
- Building permit must be approved by NYC Department of Buildings (DOB)
- All architectural and structural designs finalized
- Construction timeline determined
- Contractor(s) selected

**Role of BPP (Building Permit Process)**:
- BPP is the DOB permit approval process (NOT DOT)
- BPP approval does NOT automatically include DOT approvals
- BPP review may note DOT coordination requirements
- Some building permits have BPP conditions requiring DOT approval before construction

**Coordination**: The DOT New Building Meeting ensures all DOT permits and approvals are coordinated **after** building permit approval but **before** construction.

### How to Schedule the Meeting

#### Step 1: Determine if Meeting is Required
- Contact DOT at [VERIFY phone number]
- Provide: address, building height, scope (new/renovation)
- DOT will confirm if New Building Meeting required
- Confirm meeting is not required only for very minor projects

#### Step 2: Prepare Meeting Packet
1. **Building Permit Copy**
   - Copy of approved DOB building permit
   - Issued permit number

2. **Key Plans**
   - Site plan showing:
     - Building footprint and setbacks
     - Proposed excavation (if any)
     - Curb cuts and utility access points
     - Adjacent right-of-way
     - Construction fence/protective structure locations
   - Floor plan showing building entrance and loading areas

## 6. DOT Street Opening Permit (Utility Trenching & Underground Work)

### When It's Needed

A DOT Street Opening Permit is required when any excavation or opening of the public street or sidewalk is necessary for:

- **Utility Connections**
  - Water line connections to building
  - Sewer/sanitary connection (new or modification)
  - Storm drain connections
  - Gas line installation/connection
  - Electric service installation
  - Telecommunications lines

- **Excavation Work**
  - Trench for underground utility installation
  - Excavation accessing utilities
  - Foundation excavation extending to or below street level

- **Subsurface Work**
  - Boring or tunneling under streets
  - Basement/cellar excavation near right-of-way
  - Structural piles or caissons

### Distinction from Vault Approval

| Aspect | Street Opening Permit | Vault Approval |
|--------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Scope** | Temporary excavation to install utility | Permanent subsurface encroachment beneath right-of-way |
| **Duration** | Temporary (typically weeks to months) | Permanent (ongoing license) |
| **Primary Use** | Utilities (water, sewer, gas, electric) | Building mechanical/structure |
| **Authority** | DOT permits (field crew coordination) | DOT plan review (design approval) |
| **Timeline** | 5-15 business days | 3-6 months |

**Both may be required**: If project includes utility trenching (street opening) AND subsurface vault space (vault approval), both permits needed.

### Step-by-Step Filing Process

#### Phase 1: Pre-Application (1-2 weeks)

1. **Determine Utility Requirements**
   - Identify which utilities needed (water, sewer, gas, electric, telecom)
   - Contact utility companies for service requests:
     - NYC Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) - water/sewer
     - Con Edison - gas/electric
     - Verizon or other telecom providers
   - Obtain preliminary utility routing from locating services

2. **Engage Utility Contractors**
   - If water/sewer: Contact licensed plumber or utility contractor
   - If gas/electric: Typically utility company installs
   - If telecom: Utility company or contractor
   - Contractors provide technical specifications for DOT application

3. **Preliminary Site Assessment**
   - Walk site to understand utility access points
   - Identify trenching location(s)
   - Determine excavation depth and extent
   - Photograph existing site conditions

#### Phase 2: Utility Locate Requests (1-2 weeks)

1. **Initiate Underground Utility Locates**
   - Contact Dig Safely New York (call 811 or digitally at digsafely.com) [VERIFY]
   - Provide address and project details
   - Request locates for:
     - Water lines (DEP)
     - Sewer lines (DEP)
     - Gas lines (Con Edison)
     - Electric lines (Con Edison)
     - Telecom lines (Verizon, other providers)
   - Typical response: 5-10 business days

2. **Review Locate Results**
   - Confirm all utilities marked on-site
   - Document depths and exact locations
   - Photograph marked locations
   - Resolve any conflicts or unclear locate markings
   - Some utilities may require "hand-dig" verification (contractor excavates carefully by hand to confirm exact location)

#### Phase 3: Prepare Application (1-2 weeks)

1. **Gather Required Documentation**
   - DOT Street Opening Permit application form [VERIFY current form]
   - Detailed site plan showing:
     - Trench location and dimensions
     - Depth of excavation
     - Utility routing (marked from locate)
     - Proposed backfill materials
     - Proposed street restoration
   - Engineering drawings/specifications (from utility contractor)
   - Insurance certificate (GL $1,000,000+ minimum) [VERIFY]
   - Letter of Authorization (if expediter filing)
   - Proof of property ownership/lessee status

2. **Prepare Engineering Plan**
   - Street restoration plan showing:
     - Original pavement type (asphalt, concrete, stone, etc.)
     - Trench backfill specifications
     - Pavement restoration specifications
     - Compaction/settlement specifications
   - Utility specifications (depth, materials, protection)
   - Traffic control plan (if street closure needed)

3. **Estimate Costs**
   - Utility installation costs (contractor provides)
   - Street restoration costs (typically $500-$5,000 depending on scope) [VERIFY]
   - DOT permit fee
   - Restoration inspection fees

#### Phase 4: Submit Application (1 week)

1. **Compile Complete Package**
   - Application form (signed)
   - Site plan with utility locations
   - Engineering restoration plan
   - Insurance certificate
   - Letter of Authorization (notarized)
   - Contractor contact information
   - Payment (check or money order)

2. **Submit to DOT**
   - In-person: DOT Manhattan Permit Office [VERIFY location/hours]
   - By mail: Certified mail to DOT Permits Division [VERIFY address]
   - Online: DOT PFMS (if available for street openings) [VERIFY]
   - Request dated receipt and confirmation number

3. **DOT Initial Review**
   - DOT reviews for completeness (typically 2-3 business days)
   - If incomplete, DOT issues deficiency notice (1-week response required)

#### Phase 5: DOT Review & Approval (5-10 business days)

1. **DOT Engineering Review**
   - DOT reviews restoration plan
   - Verifies utility locate information
   - Confirms street restoration specifications
   - Checks for conflicts with other work

2. **Site Inspection** (may be required)
   - DOT inspector visits site
   - Confirms trench location and scope
   - May mark specific work area
   - Provides field instructions

3. **Approval Issued**
   - DOT issues "Authorization to Open Street" permit
   - Specifies:
     - Exact work location
     - Permissible work hours
     - Traffic control requirements
     - Restoration requirements
     - Inspection procedures
     - Timeline for completion

#### Phase 6: Construction & Inspection (variable)

1. **Notify DOT Before Work**
   - Contractor notifies DOT 24-48 hours before work start
   - Confirm site readiness and traffic control

2. **DOT Field Supervision**
   - DOT inspector visits during excavation
   - Verifies utility locations match markings
   - Confirms proper excavation techniques
   - Documents as-built utility locations

3. **Street Restoration**
   - Contractor backfills trench per specifications
   - Restores street surface (asphalt, concrete, etc.)
   - Compacts backfill to specification

4. **Final Inspection**
   - DOT inspector inspects restored street
   - Verifies restoration meets standards
   - Releases street for reopening
   - Issues final permit closure

**Historic Districts**: If work in historic district, LPC notification may be required. DOT will advise.

**Bedrock or Obstacles**: If excavation hits bedrock or unexpected obstacles (old utilities, structures), work may be delayed. Budget contingency for additional exploration.

- Street restoration must be completed within timeframe specified on permit
- Contractor must notify DOT before work begins
- All utilities must be verified marked before any excavation
- Backfill and restoration must meet DOT specifications
- Final inspection required before work considered complete
- Permit holder (not contractor) is liable for damages to utilities

## 7. DOT Crane Permits

### When It's Needed

A DOT Crane Permit is required when:

- **Hoisting Equipment Over Public Right-of-Way**
  - Crane operating with load over any public street, sidewalk, or park
  - Temporary crane operations for construction material delivery
  - Permanent hoisting equipment installation

- **Overheight/Overwidth Loads**
  - Any load exceeding standard street clearance
  - Material delivery requiring street work for access

- **Building/Facade Work**
  - Hoisting materials or equipment during construction
  - Facade access for renovation/maintenance
  - Rooftop equipment installation

### Distinction from DOB Crane Notifications

**Important Distinction**:

| Aspect | DOT Crane Permit | DOB Crane Notification |
|--------|------------------|------------------------|
| **Authority** | Department of Transportation | Department of Buildings |
| **Scope** | Impact on public right-of-way | Building-specific hoisting requirements |
| **Focus** | Street access, traffic control | Construction safety, inspection |
| **Timing** | Required for public street work | Required for all building construction |
| **Coordination** | DOT reviews with NYPD, DOB | DOB coordinates with DOT |

**Both Required**: Most building construction with hoisting requires BOTH DOB notification AND DOT permit (if over public street).

#### Phase 1: Planning & Coordination (1-2 weeks)

1. **Determine Hoisting Scope**
   - Identify what needs to be hoisted
   - Determine crane type and size

2. **Assess Street Impact**
   - Determine if crane operation impacts public right-of-way
   - Identify street lines/utilities that could be impacted
   - Confirm crane access point location
   - Identify potential traffic impacts

3. **Select Crane Company**
   - Obtain crane company name and contact
   - Request preliminary crane plan
   - Confirm crane company has NYC licenses

#### Phase 2: Prepare Crane Plan (1-2 weeks)

1. **Coordinate with Crane Company**
   - Request detailed crane plan including:
     - Crane specifications (type, capacity, boom length)
     - Crane positioning (exact location on property)
     - Load specifications (weight, dimensions, balance)
     - Swing radius
     - Street clearance verification
     - Estimated duration of hoisting operation

2. **Develop Site Plan**
   - Show crane location and positioning
   - Show swing radius and clearances
   - Identify utility locations
   - Show loading/delivery area
   - Show traffic control plan (if street closure needed)

3. **Traffic & Safety Plan**
   - Identify traffic impacts
   - Develop traffic control plan (cones, flaggers, etc.)
   - Identify street closures needed (if any)
   - Coordinate with NYPD for traffic control [VERIFY]

#### Phase 3: Application Submission (1 week)

1. **Prepare Application Package**

   - Detailed crane plan (from crane company)

   - Traffic control plan
   - Insurance certificate

   - Crane company license/certification
   - Safety specifications and procedures

2. **Submit to DOT**
   - In-person: DOT Permits office [VERIFY location]
   - By mail: Certified mail to DOT Permits Division [VERIFY address]
   - Online: DOT PFMS (if available) [VERIFY]
   - Request dated receipt

#### Phase 4: DOT Review & Approval (5-10 business days)

1. **DOT Plan Review**
   - Review crane specifications
   - Verify street clearances
   - Confirm traffic control plan
   - Check for utility conflicts

2. **NYPD Coordination** [VERIFY if required]
   - DOT coordinates with NYPD for traffic control
   - NYPD may require traffic control details

3. **Approval Issued**
   - DOT issues Crane Permit
   - Specifies:
     - Permitted hoisting dates/times
     - Crane specifications (must match permit exactly)
     - Traffic control requirements
     - Required inspections
     - Work hour restrictions

#### Phase 5: Pre-Work Notification & Inspection (1-2 days before work)

1. **Notify DOT/NYPD**
   - Notify DOT 48 hours before hoisting work begins
   - Provide final schedule and crane company contact
   - Confirm all conditions met

2. **Pre-Work Inspection**
   - DOT may require pre-work inspection
   - Inspector verifies crane setup matches permit
   - Confirms traffic control in place
   - Authorizes work to proceed

#### Phase 6: Work Execution & Supervision

1. **Field Operations**
   - Crane company executes hoisting per plan
   - DOT or DOT-contracted inspector may observe

   - Work limited to permitted hours

2. **Ongoing Supervision**
   - If multi-day hoisting operation, DOT may visit periodically

3. **Work Completion**

### Common DOT Delays & How to Avoid Them

#### 1. Incomplete Applications
**Issue**: Missing required documents, incomplete forms, or insufficient information

**Cause**: Applicants submit without verifying all requirements

**Prevention**:
- Use DOT checklist for each permit type
- Have lawyer or expediter review before submission
- Double-check all required documents listed in instructions
- Call DOT before submitting if unclear on requirements

**If Delayed**: DOT will issue deficiency notice. Respond within 1-2 weeks to avoid application rejection.

#### 2. Unclear or Inadequate Plans
**Issue**: Site plans lack dimensions, scale, or detail; DOT cannot understand proposed work

**Cause**: Architectural drawings insufficient for DOT review

**Prevention**:
- Use qualified architect/engineer to prepare plans
- Include scale, north arrow, dimensions on all plans
- Provide clear legends and specifications
- Use 1/4" scale minimum (larger is better for clarity)
- Label all elements (utilities, proposed work, setbacks, etc.)

**If Delayed**: Revise plans with all details. Resubmit with explanation of modifications.

#### 3. Insurance Certificate Issues
**Issue**: Certificate expired, incorrect coverage limits, NYC not listed as additional insured, or wrong coverage type

**Cause**: Applicants submit outdated certificates or certificates that don't meet DOT requirements

**Prevention**:
- Request certificate with effective dates 3 months beyond anticipated permit end date
- Specify minimum $1,000,000 General Liability coverage [VERIFY current minimum]
- Ensure NYC is named as "additional insured"
- Use DOT template language in insurance request
- Verify certificate includes specific endorsements DOT requires

**If Delayed**: Obtain corrected certificate and resubmit. Can significantly delay approval.

#### 4. Utility Conflicts or Missing Locate Information
**Issue**: Proposed work conflicts with existing utilities, or utilities not properly located/documented

**Cause**: Applicant didn't order utility locates before submitting, or locates show conflicts

**Prevention**:
- Always order utility locates (call 811 or digsafely.com) BEFORE submitting street opening permits
- Document locate results with photographs and measurements
- Identify conflicts and resolve before submitting
- Request hand-dig verification for utilities directly under proposed work
- Coordinate with utility companies early

**If Delayed**: Must resolve utility conflicts with utility companies. Can add 2-4 weeks if conflicts discovered after submission.

#### 5. Missing Property Authorization or Ownership Documentation
**Issue**: DOT cannot verify applicant has right to file (property owner, lessee, or authorized agent)

**Cause**: Deed, lease, or Letter of Authorization missing or inadequate

**Prevention**:
- Include current deed or recorded lease
- If expediter/third party filing, include notarized Letter of Authorization from owner
- Ensure authorization letter clearly states expediter has permission to file permits
- Include contact information for property owner in application

**If Delayed**: Obtain missing documentation and resubmit. Can invalidate entire application.

#### 6. Escalation/Agency Coordination Issues
**Issue**: DOT requires LPC, DOB, or other agency approval; delays result from agency-to-agency coordination

**Cause**: Project impacts historic district, has structural implications, or requires multi-agency review

**Prevention**:
- For historic district projects, engage LPC early (in parallel with DOT)
- For structural/vault issues, ensure structural engineer documentation is complete
- Understand if other agencies need to approve before DOT final approval
- Submit to all required agencies simultaneously (don't wait for one agency's decision)

**If Delayed**: Coordinate directly with each agency. Escalate through supervisors if individual agency causing delay.

#### 7. Revocable Consent Processing Delays
**Issue**: The longest DOT process takes 6-12+ months due to multiple revision rounds, City Council review, and public hearing

**Cause**: Complex encroachments, design issues, or procedural requirements

**Prevention**:
- Engage DOT very early in design phase
- Provide complete, detailed plans upfront
- Anticipate 2-3 revision rounds minimum
- Plan for City Council coordination 6+ months out
- Consider competitive vs. standard process implications

**If Delayed**: No quick fix. Budget 6-12 months minimum. Escalate to DOT supervisor only if experiencing unusual delays beyond typical process.

#### 8. DOT Office Closures/Backlog
**Issue**: DOT offices closed for holidays, staffing issues, or system problems causing processing delays

**Cause**: External factors (weather, system maintenance, staffing)

**Prevention**:
- Submit during typical business hours (avoid Fridays, holidays)
- Avoid submission during summer (July-August), year-end (Dec-Jan), and major holidays
- Call ahead to confirm office is open
- Use online portal when available (may have slightly faster processing)

**If Delayed**: Follow up monthly with DOT. Escalate to supervisor if experiencing multi-month delays.

### Best Practices for DOT Permit Success

1. **Start Early**: Most DOT processes take 4-12 weeks. Begin applications immediately upon building permit approval or project scope finalization.

2. **Engage Professional Help**: Licensed expediters understand DOT requirements and can significantly accelerate approval.

3. **Be Complete**: Submit complete applications with all required documents. Incomplete applications face rejection/rework.

9. **Plan for Contingency**: Complex projects may require additional coordination, revisions, or unforeseen requirements. Budget extra time.

10. **Communicate**: Maintain regular communication with DOT, utility companies, building contractor, and other stakeholders to anticipate and resolve issues early.

# ECB/OATH Violation Resolution Guide

## Overview

The Environmental Control Board (ECB), now known as the Office of Administrative Trials and Hearings (OATH), adjudicates civil violations issued by the Department of Buildings. These violations carry monetary penalties and can block permits, Certificates of Occupancy, and property transactions until resolved.

## Violation Classes and Severity

### Class 1: Immediately Hazardous
- **Severity**: Most serious violations
- **Penalty**: Highest civil penalties
- **Correction**: Must be corrected immediately
- **Examples**:
  - Work without required permit
  - Structural issues in occupied buildings
  - Failure to maintain life safety systems
  - Hazardous conditions endangering public

### Class 2: Major Violations
- **Severity**: Significant non-compliance
- **Penalty**: Moderate civil penalties
- **Correction**: Must be corrected within specified timeframe
- **Examples**:
  - Failure to comply with approved construction plans
  - Missing or defective safety equipment
  - Failure to post required site certifications

### Class 3: Lesser Violations
- **Severity**: Minor violations
- **Penalty**: Lower civil penalties
- **Correction**: Should be corrected promptly
- **Examples**:
  - Failure to post permit in visible location
  - Missing or illegible site signage
  - Minor paperwork/documentation issues

## Understanding Default Status

**Default** is the most common violation status GLE encounters. A violation enters DEFAULT when:
- Respondent fails to appear at scheduled OATH hearing
- No timely request to reschedule is filed
- No response is submitted by deadline

**Impact of Default**:
- Penalties increase significantly (typically 25-50% multiplier)
- Violation record becomes harder to challenge
- Building record shows unresolved violation
- Can block future DOB approvals and transactions

## Resolution Process for DEFAULT Violations

### Step 1: Request to Vacate Default
This must be filed with OATH and demonstrate one of:
- **Good cause for missing hearing** (illness, emergency, miscommunication, etc.)
- **Violation has been cured** (corrective work completed with supporting documentation)

**Timeline**: File as soon as possible; OATH has discretion on acceptance.

### Step 2: File Certificate of Correction
If violation condition has been remedied, file a Certificate of Correction package including:
- **Description of corrective work** performed
- **Before/after photographs** showing condition correction
- **Licensed contractor affidavit** (if professional work required)
- **Engineer certification** (if structural/safety work)
- Supporting documentation (invoices, permits for remedial work, etc.)

### Step 3: Penalty Resolution

**Option A: Negotiate Reduction at Hearing**
- Request to vacate default + appear at hearing
- Present Certificate of Correction evidence
- OATH may reduce penalty below default amount
- Timeline: Faster, within 2-4 weeks typically

**Option B: Pay Default Amount**
- Pay full default penalty to close violation
- File Certificate of Correction simultaneously
- Violation marked as resolved/corrected
- Timeline: Immediate closure upon payment

## When to Contest vs. When to Pay and Correct

[VERIFY: What's GLE's philosophy? Key decision factors?]

**Factors favoring contest/hearing**:
- Violation issued in error or not supported by facts
- Inspector did not verify corrected condition
- Work was actually permitted or authorized
- Significant penalty amount (Class 1) justifies expense

**Factors favoring pay-and-correct**:
- Violation has clear factual basis
- Corrective work is simple and inexpensive
- Closure is urgent (blocking transaction, permit, CO)
- Penalty amount is reasonable

## Impact on DOB Business

**Open violations create blockers**:
- New permits: Applications may be delayed or denied
- Certificates of Occupancy: Cannot be issued with open violations
- Renewals: ALAs, PA renewals affected
- Closings/refinancing: Title issues; lenders may require resolution
- Public record: Violations visible on property BIS report

**Priority**: Resolve old, unresolved violations before filing new applications for same building.

## Pro Tips from GLE Experience

[VERIFY: Any specific tricks or common issues?]

- **Track hearing dates carefully**: Default is easy to enter, hard to exit
- **Photograph everything**: Before/after photos are your strongest evidence
- **Use contractor affidavits**: Licensed contractor statements carry weight at OATH
- **Don't ignore violations**: Penalties compound; default multipliers exceed original violation cost
- **File Certificate of Correction promptly**: Don't let violations age; recent corrections are more credible
- **Request violation records before hearing**: BIS reports help prove violation details
- **Follow up on e-filings**: Confirm receipt; some filings get lost in system

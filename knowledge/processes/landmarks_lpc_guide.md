# Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) Approval Guide

## Overview
The Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) is responsible for regulating buildings and districts of historic or cultural significance in NYC. Any visible alteration to a landmarked building, whether exterior or interior, typically requires LPC approval. This guide outlines the LPC approval process, application types, timelines, and common issues.

## 1. What is the Landmarks Preservation Commission

### Role and Authority
- Created under the New York City Landmarks Law (Section 3020 of NYC Charter)
- Independent agency protecting NYC's historic and cultural resources
- Regulate alterations, demolitions, and new construction affecting landmarks
- Enforce compliance with approval decisions

### Types of Protected Properties
- **Individual Landmarks:** Buildings, structures, or complexes designated individually (2,500+)
- **Historic Districts:** Areas containing clusters of historic buildings (approximately 90 districts)
- **Interior Landmarks:** Interiors or interior spaces of buildings (theaters, banks, lobbies, etc.)
- **Scenic Landmarks:** Parks and scenic areas of historic value

## 2. How to Determine if a Building is Landmarked

### LPC Database Search
- Access online database: **Landmarks.ny.gov** or use LPC Property Information Lookup
- Search by address, block/lot, or landmark name
- Database shows designation date and landmark category
- Provides designation report and any restrictions

### ACRIS (Automated City Register Information System)
- Property records system maintained by NYC Department of Finance
- Contains deed information and may reference landmark designation
- Searchable by address, block/lot, or owner name
- Free online access: **ACRIS.finance.ny.gov**

### On-Site Visual Indicators
- Bronze landmark plaque on building facade (not all landmarks have plaques)
- Historic District boundary markers or signs in neighborhood
- Check property deed for historical restrictions or conservation easements

### Title/Deed Search
- Historical deeds may contain landmark designation notice
- Property survey or title report often references landmark status
- Legal description may include "within Historic District" language

- Response typically within 1-2 business days

## 3. Types of LPC Applications

LPC maintains three primary approval pathways, each with different review processes and timelines.

### Staff-Level Approvals: CNE (Certificate of No Effect) and PMW (Permit Modification Work)

#### Certificate of No Effect (CNE)
- **Purpose:** Certifies that proposed work has no historic or architectural significance
- **Scope:** Minor alterations with no visible impact
- **Review Level:** Staff level (LPC staff, no commission hearing required)
- **Timeline:** 1-3 weeks
- **Examples:**
  - Interior non-structural work not visible from street
  - Replacement of mechanical systems (HVAC, plumbing) entirely concealed
  - Interior painting and finishes (in non-landmarked interiors)
  - Electrical system upgrades hidden within walls

#### Permit Modification Work (PMW)
- **Purpose:** Streamlined approval for routine maintenance and minor work
- **Scope:** Work types pre-approved by LPC as appropriate
- **Review Level:** Staff level with simplified documentation
- **Timeline:** 5-10 business days
- **Examples:**
  - Roof repairs using in-kind materials (slate roof repaired with slate)
  - Window reglazing or reputty (without frame changes)
  - Exterior masonry repointing using mortar matching original specification
  - Cornice or ornamental element repair/restoration
  - Awning repair (not replacement)

**Advantages:** Faster processing, lower documentation requirements, predictable outcomes

### Certificate of Appropriateness (CofA)

#### Standard Certificate of Appropriateness
- **Purpose:** Full approval process for work affecting landmark's historic character
- **Scope:** Alterations requiring aesthetic or preservation assessment
- **Review Level:** Commission-level review with public hearing
- **Timeline:** 6-12 weeks or longer
- **Examples:**
  - Window replacement (frame, sash, or material changes)
  - New door installation or storefront modification
  - Rooftop additions or mechanical equipment visible from street
  - Facade alterations, new materials, or color changes
  - Rear yard improvements visible from adjacent properties
  - Interior work on designated interior landmarks

#### Expedited Certificate of Appropriateness
- **Purpose:** Faster review for work meeting specific criteria
- **Eligibility:** Owner-occupied residential, affordable housing projects, sustainability improvements
- **Timeline:** 4-6 weeks [VERIFY expedited track availability]
- **Requirements:** Additional documentation demonstrating expedited eligibility

### Advisory Report

- **Use:** Preliminary design consultation before formal application

## 4. Staff-Level Approvals: Process and Eligibility

### CNE Eligibility Criteria
Work must meet ALL of the following:
1. Entirely interior work OR completely concealed from public view
2. Non-structural in nature
3. Does not alter external appearance
4. Does not affect protected architectural features
5. Complies with all applicable codes
6. Does not involve removal or alteration of any historic feature

### PMW Eligibility Criteria
Work must be on the LPC-approved PMW list and:
1. Materials, color, and design match original/existing condition (in-kind replacement)
2. No change to size, configuration, or architectural character
3. No structural modifications
4. Professional specifications available for review
5. Work supervised by qualified tradesperson or professional

### Application Process for Staff-Level

**Step 1: Determine Eligibility**
- Contact LPC staff to confirm CNE or PMW eligibility
- Request specific guidance on documentation requirements

**Step 2: Prepare Documentation**
- **CNE:** Detailed narrative, interior scope photos, mechanical equipment specs
- **PMW:** Specification sheets, material samples, product certifications, comparable photos
- Professional architect/engineer certification may be required [VERIFY]

**Step 3: Submit Application**
- File through LPC online system (if available) or in-person at LPC offices
- Address: 1 Centre Street, New York, NY 10007, 9th Floor
- Include application form, all required documents, and applicable fee
- Staff performs initial completeness review

**Step 4: Staff Review**
- LPC staff reviews for code compliance and historic appropriateness
- May request additional information or clarifications (Additional Information - AI)
- Staff may schedule site visit to verify scope

**Step 5: Issuance**
- CNE or PMW issued upon successful review (typically 1-3 weeks)
- Applicant receives official document and can proceed with work
- Work scope must not deviate from approved application

## 5. Certificate of Appropriateness: Full Commission Review

### CofA Eligibility Triggers
A full CofA (Commission review) is required when:
- Work affects visible exterior surfaces or features
- Project requires new construction or major alterations
- Proposed work changes historic character of interior landmark
- CNE/PMW eligibility cannot be confirmed
- Owner requests full commission review (even if staff-level eligible)

### Application Documentation Requirements

#### Drawings and Plans
- **Existing Conditions Plan:** Current state with dimensions and materials noted
  - Building facade elevation showing affected areas
  - Floor plan if interior landmark work
  - Sections showing construction details

- **Proposed Work Plan:** Design drawings showing changes
  - Architect-prepared elevations (1/4" scale typical)
  - Details of new elements: windows, doors, materials, colors
  - Sections showing integration with existing building
  - Full dimension and specification callouts

- **Detail Sheets:** Large-scale construction details
  - Window/door frame profiles and connections
  - Material transitions and connections
  - Color specifications and finish details
  - Compatibility with historic character

#### Photographs
- **Existing conditions photos:** Multiple angles, close-ups of affected areas
- **Contextual photos:** Showing streetscape and neighboring buildings
- **Comparable examples:** Reference photos of similar approved work (if applicable)
- **Labeled and dated:** Clear identification of location/subject

#### Material Samples
- **Physical samples of materials:** Paint colors, hardware, roofing materials
- **Manufacturer product information:** Specifications, certifications, finish options
- **Comparable precedents:** Historic equivalent materials if restoration

#### Written Narrative
- **Project description:** Scope, purpose, and historic justification
- **Design rationale:** Explanation of how proposal respects historic character
- **Historical analysis:** How proposal relates to building's history and significance
- **Public benefit:** Any community or cultural benefits (if applicable)
- **Alternative designs considered:** Why preferred design is most appropriate

#### Professional Certifications
- **Architect certification:** Licensed architect sign-off on drawings and appropriateness
- **Structural engineer:** If work affects building structure or stability
- **Historic preservation specialist:** For complex or sensitive projects [VERIFY if required]

### Application Submission Process

**Step 1: Pre-Application Consultation**
- Schedule meeting with LPC staff (optional but recommended)
- Present preliminary design and discuss concerns
- Obtain early feedback to avoid costly revisions

**Step 2: Prepare Complete Application**
- Compile all required drawings, photographs, samples, and narrative
- Organize materials in logical order (existing → proposed)
- Ensure drawings are legible, to-scale, and fully dimensioned
- Proof all documents for accuracy

**Step 3: Submit to LPC**
- File through LPC application system (LPC Permits Online [VERIFY name/availability])
- Hard copies may be required in addition to digital submission
- Include signed application form and applicable fee [VERIFY current fee]

**Step 4: Completeness Review**

- Once complete, application enters review queue

**Step 5: Staff Report and Recommendation**
- LPC staff prepares staff report (typically 2-4 weeks after completeness)
- Staff report includes project description, analysis, and recommendation

**Step 6: Commission Hearing and Vote**

## 6. Coordination with DOB Filing

### Concurrent Filing Approach (Typical)
- LPC application and DOB filing can proceed simultaneously
- Both agencies review respective responsibilities in parallel
- Reduces overall project timeline by 4-8 weeks compared to sequential filing

### LPC Approval First (Recommended in Some Cases)
- Some expeditors recommend obtaining CofA before DOB submission
- Ensures design is appropriate to historic context before structural/technical review
- Avoids rework if LPC requires significant modifications
- May delay overall timeline by 4-8 weeks

### Sequential Filing (Uncommon)
- Some projects require DOB approval first (rarely)
- Not recommended as delays project unnecessarily

### Coordination Requirements
- **LPC approval letter or CofA must accompany DOB filing** for landmark projects
- DOB may request confirmation that project complies with LPC approval
- Conditions imposed by LPC must be reflected in DOB construction documents
- Both approvals must align on scope and specifications

### Integration in DOB Plans
- Reference LPC approval number on DOB plan sheets
- Note any LPC-imposed conditions or requirements in title block
- Include material specifications required by LPC in construction documents
- Architect sign-off confirms compliance with both LPC and DOB approvals

## 7. Master Plans for Institutions

### Master Plan Purpose and Scope
- Long-term planning tool for institutional properties with multiple buildings
- Allows phased approach to campus/complex improvements
- Covers framework for future alterations, additions, and restorations
- Approved at high level, specific projects then filed as "minor works" under plan

### Master Plan Eligibility
- Large institutional properties (universities, hospitals, religious institutions, cultural organizations)
- Multiple buildings or complex properties on single or contiguous parcels
- Long-term maintenance and development anticipated

## 8. Violations and Enforcement

### LPC Enforcement Authority
- LPC Inspector investigates complaints about unlicensed work
- Illegal work includes: alterations without CofA, demolition without approval, unapproved painting
- Building owner ultimately responsible for compliance

### Violation Process
1. **Investigation:** LPC staff visits site, photographs violation, documents scope
2. **Violation Notice:** Owner issued violation stating required corrective action
3. **Compliance Period:** Owner given [VERIFY] days to cure violation
4. **Penalties:** Fines up to [VERIFY] per day of non-compliance (capped at [VERIFY])
5. **Escalation:** Continued non-compliance may result in:
   - Criminal charges (Class B/C misdemeanor)
   - DOB violations
   - Liens on property
   - Remedial work ordered and costs charged to owner

### Remediation Options
- Restoration to original/pre-violation condition
- Approved restoration/rehabilitation design
- Negotiated settlement with LPC (sometimes acceptable)

### Common Violations
- Unauthorized window replacement
- Unpermitted facade cleaning or alterations
- Unapproved painting colors (exterior)
- Rooftop additions without CofA
- Interior landmark alterations

## 11. Common Issues and Solutions

### Window Replacements
- **Common Issue:** Owner replaces windows with different material, color, or configuration
- **LPC Position:** Historic windows require matching replacement (frame, profile, materials, muntins)
  - Original 6-over-6 sash → Replacement must be 6-over-6 (no 1-over-1 or fixed panes)
  - Wood frames → Replacement typically must be wood (vinyl/aluminum require justification)
  - Glass type, color, and reflectivity must match or be historically appropriate
- **Solutions:**
  - Source compatible wood replacement windows (expensive but fully compliant)
  - Request LPC variance for alternative materials (sometimes granted if appearance identical)
  - Explore interior storm window retrofit to improve energy efficiency while keeping originals
  - Apply for expedited CofA if pursuing green energy/sustainability improvements

### Storefront Changes
- **Common Issue:** Ground-floor retail space alterations (storefronts, signage, entries)
- **LPC Position:** Ground floors are historically and visually prominent, require preservation of character
  - Maintain historic storefront rhythm and proportions
  - Preserve historic glass, mullion patterns, and materials
  - Signage must be historically appropriate in size, material, color
  - Entry doors must be compatible with historic configuration
- **Solutions:**
  - Work with LPC-familiar designer to create historically-sensitive storefront
  - Reuse or restore original storefront elements if possible
  - Modern materials acceptable if appearance matches historic precedent
  - Larger signs/digital signs typically require extensive justification

### Rooftop Additions
- **Common Issue:** HVAC equipment, solar panels, or structures added to visible roofline
- **LPC Position:** Rooftop alterations must not be visible from street level (or minimally visible)
  - Equipment typically must be screened or located behind parapet
  - Height limited to avoid profile change visible from landmarks district
  - Solar panels require design to minimize visual impact (low-profile racking, dark colors)
- **Solutions:**
  - Locate equipment behind parapet wall for full concealment
  - Request LPC variance if concealment infeasible (requires strong justification)
  - Explore interior mechanical spaces for equipment relocation
  - For solar: Pursue expedited CofA citing sustainability benefits (environmental policy priority)

### Rear Yard Work
- **Common Issue:** Alterations to rear courtyards, gardens, or rear facades
- **LPC Position:** Depends on visibility and historic character
  - If visible from historic district streets or adjacent properties: LPC review required
  - If completely screened from public view: May qualify for CNE
  - Historic courtyard patterns/character should be preserved
- **Solutions:**
  - Document visibility; if fully private, pursue CNE
  - If visible, design should respect historic patterns and materials
  - Provide photos showing visual relationship to historic context

### Interior Landmark Alterations
- **Common Issue:** Interior spaces of designated landmarks require approval for alterations
- **LPC Position:** Depends on whether work affects protected interior features
  - Historic plasterwork, moldings, floors, staircases, original finishes protected
  - Modern non-original interiors may have more flexibility
  - Subdividing historic large spaces typically not approved
- **Solutions:**
  - Preserve historic architectural features wherever possible
  - Work with preservation specialist to document original significance
  - Negotiate phased compliance if full restoration immediately infeasible
  - Explore creative design solutions that respect historic character

### Asbestos/Environmental Remediation
- **Common Issue:** Abatement of asbestos in windows or building materials conflicts with preservation
- **LPC Position:** Environmental safety takes priority, but historic character should be preserved
- **Solutions:**
  - Use encapsulation where possible instead of removal
  - Plan replacement of materials using historically appropriate alternatives
  - Obtain both environmental and LPC approvals simultaneously
  - Budget for higher-cost compliant materials/methods

## 12. Best Practices and Tips

### Pre-Application Strategy
- **Early consultation:** Schedule pre-application meeting with LPC staff before design is finalized
- **Preservation specialist:** Consider hiring architectural historian or preservation consultant for complex projects
- **Photography and research:** Document existing conditions thoroughly and research building history
- **Community engagement:** In historic districts, understand neighborhood concerns and address early

### Design Optimization
- **Historic appropriateness:** Design should be compatible with building's period and style
- **Material authenticity:** Specify historically appropriate materials (wood windows, slate roofing, etc.) from the start
- **Professional design:** Use architect with experience in LPC-approved projects
- **Reference precedents:** Include photos of similar approved work in application

### Documentation and Submission
- **Completeness:** Submit complete application first time to avoid delays from additional information requests
- **Professional drawings:** Hire architect to prepare dimensioned construction documents, not sketches
- **Quality photos:** Use professional photography of existing conditions and comparable examples
- **Narrative clarity:** Write compelling historic justification for proposed work

### Coordination Strategy
- **Concurrent filing:** Submit to DOB and LPC simultaneously to reduce overall timeline
- **Cross-referencing:** Ensure DOB plans reflect LPC conditions and approvals
- **Contractor communication:** Brief contractor on LPC requirements before work begins

### Ongoing Compliance
- **Work supervision:** Retain architectural inspector to verify work matches LPC-approved design
- **Material compliance:** Specify exact product names/colors in construction documents
- **Documentation:** Photograph completed work and maintain records of compliance

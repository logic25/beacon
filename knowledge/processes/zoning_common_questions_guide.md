## 1. FAR for R6 with C1-2 Commercial Overlay

**Question:** What's the maximum FAR for an R6 lot with C1-2 overlay?

**Answer:**
The answer depends on which development program applies to the R6 base zoning:

- **R6 Height Factor Program:** Base FAR 2.43
- **R6 Quality Housing Program:** FAR up to 3.6 (with Inclusionary Housing provisions)
- **C1-2 Commercial Overlay:** Adds commercial FAR of 2.0 (only for permitted commercial uses on lowest floors)

**How they combine:**
- If Height Factor program applies: R6 residential FAR is 2.43; commercial overlay adds separate 2.0 FAR for ground floor retail/office spaces
- If Quality Housing applies: R6 residential FAR is 3.6; C1-2 overlay adds 2.0 FAR for commercial
- **Total development potential:** Varies by lot configuration and use mix

**Key point:** The overlay does NOT automatically increase your base residential FAR. The 2.0 is additional FAR only for commercial uses, typically on lower floors.

## 2. R6B Contextual District Zoning Rules

**Question:** What are the bulk limits in R6B zoning?

**Answer:**
R6B is a **contextual residential district** with stricter controls than standard R6. Use these bulk regulations:

| Bulk Parameter | R6B Limit |
|---|---|
| **Maximum FAR** | 2.0 |
| **Maximum Base Height** | 40 feet |
| **Maximum Building Height** | 50 feet |
| **Maximum Lot Coverage** | 65% |
| **Street Wall Requirements** | Building must occupy 70–90% of street frontage; 60% must reach max height |
| **Side Yard Setback** | Required at base; at/above 40 ft, setbacks may apply |

**Key distinction:** R6B height controls are measured differently than standard R6. These heights are strict and often trigger design modifications for larger projects.

**Application:** R6B is common in outer-borough neighborhoods and historic commercial corridors. Development rights are more constrained than R6.

## 3. Is a Cellar Counted Toward Floor Area?

**Question:** Do cellars count as floor area?

**Answer:**
**No, generally not.** A true cellar is excluded from floor area calculations under NYC Zoning Resolution Section 12-10.

**Key definitions:**

| Term | Definition | Counted Toward FAR? |
|---|---|---|
| **Cellar** | More than 50% below curb level | NO (exception: if used for dwelling or certain commercial purposes) |
| **Basement** | Less than 50% below curb level | YES |

**Critical exceptions — cellars ARE counted if used for:**
- Dwelling purposes
- Certain commercial uses (retail, office if accessible)
- Manufacturing (F occupancy)

**Practical example:** A cellar used solely for mechanical/electrical equipment, storage, or parking—not counted. A cellar with a rental unit or commercial kitchen—counted.

**Why this matters:** Understanding curb level is critical. DOB surveys establish official curb elevation; if your calculation is off, the entire floor area calculation fails.

## 4. Non-Conforming vs. Non-Compliance: Critical Distinction

**Question:** What's the difference between non-conforming uses and non-complying buildings?

**Answer:**
These are fundamentally different legal concepts with different rules:

### Non-Conforming Use
- **Definition:** A USE that was legal when established but no longer permitted under current zoning
- **Example:** Single-family house in an R-8 commercial overlay zone (use predates current zoning)
- **Protection:** Grandfathered in; can continue indefinitely if non-interrupted
- **Constraints:** Cannot expand floor area; alterations limited; must discontinue if closed 2+ years
- **Conversion:** Cannot convert to a different non-conforming use

### Non-Complying Building
- **Definition:** A BUILDING that doesn't meet current bulk regulations (height, setback, FAR, lot coverage) but was legal when built
- **Example:** 6-story building in R6B with FAR 2.5 (exceeds current R6B limit of 2.0)
- **Protection:** Can continue as built; minor repairs allowed without full compliance
- **Alterations:** Trigger full code compliance if alterations exceed certain thresholds
- **Expansion:** Major alterations/additions require bringing entire building to current code

### Why GLE cares:
- **Non-conforming use:** Client operating a warehouse in residential zoning. Can continue operation, but cannot expand or change use. Impacts ALT-1 strategy.
- **Non-complying building:** Building exceeds FAR limits. Can operate, but any major renovation triggers sprinkler/accessibility/full code work.

## 5. Permitted Obstructions in Required Yards and Open Space

**Question:** What mechanical equipment/obstructions are permitted in required open space (especially R7 districts)?

**Background:** Beacon frequently hallucinates ZR section numbers on this topic. This is a high-error area.

**Answer:**
Permitted obstructions in required yards and open space are governed by:

- **ZR Section 12-10 (Definitions):** Defines "open space," "yard," and other terms
- **ZR Article 4 (Bulk Regulations):** Contains district-specific yard and open space requirements
- **ZR Section 4-[DISTRICT]:** Each residential district has specific obstruction rules

**Common permitted obstructions** (varies by district):
- Mechanical equipment (HVAC condensers, heat pump units)
- Flagpoles, antennas (within limits)
- Accessible ramps, stairs
- Loading dock equipment
- Certain architectural features (cornices, sills)

**What's NOT permitted (usually):**
- Building floor area
- Enclosed structures
- Parking spaces
- Trash enclosures (in some districts)

## 8. City of Yes for Housing Zoning Amendments

**Question:** What changed with City of Yes? Does it affect our projects?

**Background:** City of Yes amendments took effect in late 2024. Multiple provisions affect construction projects.

**Key City of Yes changes:**
- **ADU (Accessory Dwelling Units):** Permitted in R3–R10 districts as-of-right (no zoning variance needed). Allows secondary unit in existing or new buildings.
- **Parking Requirements:** Significantly reduced or eliminated in transit-accessible areas
- **Commercial-to-Residential Conversions:** Easier conversion of office/retail to residential in commercial districts
- **Reduced FAR/Bulk Constraints:** [VERIFY: Which districts saw reductions?]

**Impact on GLE Work:**
- Clients asking about adding ADU to brownstone: Now legal as-of-right in R6 (if other code requirements met)
- Office-to-residential conversions: Reduced zoning barriers; still need DOB ALT-1, fire compliance, accessibility
- Parking: Fewer required parking spaces = smaller buildings needed for same use

**Question:** What PRC applies to my use? How many parking spaces do I need?

| **PRC C** | Lower-density residential, some neighborhood retail |

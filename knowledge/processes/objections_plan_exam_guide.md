# Plan Examination, Objections, and Examiner Communication Guide

## Overview

After a permit application is filed with the Department of Buildings, a plan examiner is assigned to review the submitted plans for compliance with applicable codes and regulations. This examination phase is where most project issues emerge. Understanding the plan examination process, responding effectively to objections, and maintaining strong communication with examiners is critical to expediting permits. This guide covers the examination workflow, common objection types, response strategies, and best practices for examiner engagement.

---

## The Plan Examination Process — Step by Step

### Phase 1: Application Intake and Assignment (0–5 Business Days)
1. **Application filed**: ALT-1, ALT-2, PAA, NB, or other application submitted via DOB Build or BIS
2. **System validation**: DOB system checks for completeness (all required forms, fees collected, documents present)
3. **Examiner assignment**: DOB assigns application to an available plan examiner based on workload and specialty
4. **Status in system**: Application status changes from "Submitted" or "Accepted" to "Assigned to Examiner"
5. **Timeline**: Typically 2–5 business days from filing to assignment

**Pro Tip**: Do not call the Plan Desk until at least 5 business days after filing. Premature inquiries are met with "it's in the queue" and waste time.

### Phase 2: Plan Examination (5–30 Business Days)
1. **Examiner reviews**: Examiner conducts detailed review of all submitted plans against:
   - NYC Building Code (2014, as adopted, or current edition)
   - NYC Zoning Resolution (use, occupancy, FAR, parking, etc.)
   - NYC Energy Conservation Code (TR1, EN sheets, insulation, HVAC efficiency)
   - Local Law 97 (carbon emission reporting for large buildings)
   - Accessibility standards (ADA, FHA)
   - Fire code and fire suppression requirements
   - Hazardous materials, MEP, structural engineering standards
2. **Deficiencies identified**: Examiner notes any code violations, missing documents, or plan ambiguities
3. **Status**: Application remains "Assigned to Examiner" during this phase
4. **Timeline**: Typically 1–3 weeks of actual examiner review time (dependent on complexity and examiner workload)

### Phase 3: Objections Issued (Day 5–30)
1. **Objections generated**: Examiner documents all code issues in objection format
2. **Communication method**:
   - In **DOB Build**: Objections appear in portal under "Objections" tab with examiner comments
   - In **BIS**: Examiner sends email or mail with objection summary
3. **Status change**: Application status changes to "Objections Issued" or "Plan Examination Pending Response"
4. **Response deadline**: Typically 30 days to respond (check application portal for exact deadline)
5. **No objections**: If no deficiencies found, examiner may approve directly (rare on first review)

### Phase 4: Applicant Response (Days 1–30 from Objection Date)
1. **Response preparation**: Architect/engineer revises plans and prepares written responses addressing each objection
2. **Document submission**:
   - In **DOB Build**: Upload revised plans and response document through portal
   - In **BIS**: Email revised plans and response to examiner or submit via courier
3. **Response content**: Must include revised plan pages (clearly marked as revised) and narrative explaining how each objection is addressed
4. **Professional certification**: May require professional certification of objections (see below)
5. **Timeline**: Must be submitted before deadline or response is deemed abandoned and permit is rejected

### Phase 5: Re-Examination (3–15 Business Days)
1. **Examiner review**: Examiner reviews revised plans and responses
2. **Determination**: Examiner either:
   - **Approves application** (all objections resolved)
   - **Issues additional objections** (revised plans still have deficiencies, or new issues created)
   - **Requests supplemental information** (needs clarification without full objection)
3. **Status**: "Objections Resolved" or "Additional Objections Issued"
4. **Timeline**: Typically 1–2 weeks for re-examination

### Phase 6: Approval and Issuance (1–2 Business Days)
1. **Application approved**: All objections resolved, examiner signs off
2. **Permit issuance**: DOB issues permit and applicant can begin work
3. **Notice of Approval**: Applicant receives approval notice; permit number generated
4. **CO eligibility**: Permit may require inspections or sign-off before Certificate of Occupancy issued

## Types of Objections

Objections fall into several categories. Understanding each helps in developing effective responses.

### 1. Zoning Objections
Violations of NYC Zoning Resolution requirements:

**Common issues**:
- **Floor Area Ratio (FAR)**: Proposed floor area exceeds zoning FAR limit
- **Height and setbacks**: Building height or setback from property line violates zoning
- **Use not permitted**: Proposed use not allowed in zoning district
- **Parking requirements**: Insufficient parking spaces for proposed use
- **Lot coverage**: Building footprint exceeds maximum lot coverage percentage
- **Frontage requirements**: Required street frontage not met
- **Yard requirements**: Side yards, rear yards, or open space requirements not met

**Response strategy**: Zoning objections often require design changes (setback reconfiguration, FAR reduction, use modification). Coordination with architect essential. If zoning violation cannot be resolved through design, may require variance application to Board of Standards and Appeals (BSA) — **not handled through DOB plan exam**.

### 2. Building Code Objections
Violations of NYC Building Code (fire safety, structural, accessibility, egress, materials):

**Common issues**:
- **Egress**: Insufficient exit width, travel distance, or number of exits
- **Fire separation**: Rated partitions not correctly constructed or labeled
- **Fire-rated doors and windows**: Incorrect specification or rating
- **Accessibility (ADA)**: Ramps, doors, restrooms, elevators not accessible; circulation paths blocked
- **Structural**: Inadequate beam/column sizing, lateral bracing, or foundation design
- **Means of egress**: Doors open in wrong direction, hallways too narrow, landings missing
- **Occupant load**: Calculated occupant load exceeds code limits or exit capacity
- **Hazardous materials**: Chemical storage, asbestos abatement, fuel storage not code-compliant
- **Guardrails and safety railings**: Balconies, mezzanines, stairs missing required guards

**Response strategy**: Building Code objections usually require plan revisions (enlarging egress, adding exits, resizing structural members). Licensed engineer or architect must revise and certify compliance.

### 3. Energy Code Objections
Violations of NYC Energy Conservation Code (thermal performance, HVAC efficiency, insulation):

**Common issues**:
- **TR1 (Exterior Wall Assembly)**: Roof, wall, foundation insulation R-value below code minimum; thermal bridging
- **EN sheets (Energy sheets)**: Missing or incomplete mechanical system design; HVAC efficiency not documented
- **Fenestration**: Windows/glazing U-value or Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC) below code requirement
- **Heating and cooling**: Inefficient or undersized mechanical system
- **Lighting efficiency**: Insufficient LED conversion or daylighting integration
- **Water heating**: Hot water generation and distribution efficiency not code-compliant

**Response strategy**: Energy Code objections require MEP engineer involvement. Common solutions: upgrade insulation, select higher-efficiency HVAC units, upgrade windows, provide solar shading. TR1 and EN sheet completion is typical response.

### 4. Administrative Objections
Missing documents, incomplete forms, fee issues, or procedural deficiencies:

**Common issues**:
- **Missing documents**: Site plan, zoning analysis, mechanical drawings, structural calculations
- **Incorrect forms**: Forms not properly signed, notarized, or dated
- **Professional licenses**: Professional seal or license number missing or invalid
- **Fee issues**: Insufficient fees collected; need to pay additional fees
- **Inconsistent information**: Application narrative contradicts plans

**Response strategy**: Administrative objections are fastest to resolve. Identify missing document or form error and resubmit. Often can be resolved via email communication with examiner without formal response process.

### 5. QA (Quality Assurance) Failures
Quality Assurance review issues in DOB Build system (file format, readability, completeness):

**Common issues**:
- **File format**: Plan submitted as Word document instead of PDF; not accepted
- **File size**: Single plan page exceeds file size limit (e.g., >10 MB)
- **Missing pages**: Sheet list shows plans 1–10, but only 1–9 submitted
- **Image quality**: Plans scanned at low resolution, illegible when examined
- **File corruption**: PDF corrupted or cannot be opened by DOB system
- **Plan legibility**: Text too small, images pixelated, markup not readable

**Response strategy**: QA failures are **not examiner objections** but system rejections. Correct the flagged issue (resize image, convert to PDF, increase resolution) and resubmit. Build system will automatically notify examiner once QA passes.

## Objection Response Strategy

### Before Responding

1. **Review all objections thoroughly**: Read each objection 2–3 times. Note specific code sections cited.
2. **Assign responsibility**: Architect addresses building/design objections; MEP engineer addresses mechanical/energy; lawyer/surveyor for zoning.
3. **Assess feasibility**: Can objection be resolved through plan revision, or does it require design change? Is design change feasible?
4. **Set timeline**: Plan to respond in first 2 weeks of 30-day response period. Leaving response until day 28 risks missing deadline due to portal submission delays.
5. **Coordinate with client**: Ensure client understands scope change or cost implications of resolving objections.

### Preparing the Response

#### Revised Plans
- **Mark clearly as revised**: Every page revised to address objections should have "REVISED" stamp or notation with revision date
- **Track changes**: Include revision block showing what changed and why (e.g., "Added secondary exit per examiner objection to Means of Egress")
- **Complete plans only**: Submit all sheets (site plan, floor plans, elevations, sections, details, specifications) not just revised sheets
- **Professional seal**: Plans must bear architect/engineer license seal and signature (renewed certification if design changed)
- **Consistency**: All sheets must coordinate; if one sheet revised, check all related sheets for consistency

#### Written Response Document
- **Address each objection individually**: Number responses to match objection numbers
- **Format**: Clear, organized, professional; preferably PDF
- **Content per objection**:
  - Quote the objection or cite the specific code section
  - Explain the response/resolution
  - Reference revised plan page(s) where solution shown
  - If requesting waiver/exception: explain why code compliance is infeasible and request variance or waiver
- **Certification**: Response may need to be signed by professional (engineer, architect) if technical objections involved
- **Length**: Typically 5–10 pages for standard response; keep concise and focused

#### Supporting Documents
- If objection requires calculations, submittals, or special documentation:
  - Structural calculations (if structural objection)
  - HVAC load calculations and equipment cut sheets (if mechanical objection)
  - Accessibility matrix (if ADA objection)
  - Fire protection system design documents
  - Material certifications, test reports
  - Code interpretation or precedent examples (if unusual objection)

### Filing the Response

#### In DOB Build
1. Log into application
2. Navigate to "Objections" tab
3. Click "Respond to Objections" or "Submit Response"
4. Upload revised plan PDF files
5. Upload written response document
6. Submit (ensure confirmation message received)
7. **Timeline**: Submit by 11:59 PM of deadline date to avoid timezone issues

#### In BIS
1. Email response and revised plans to examiner (email address in objection letter)
2. Reference application/job number in subject line
3. Include written response as PDF and plan pages as PDF
4. Request read receipt to confirm receipt
5. If response is large, may need to submit via courier or in-person

## Examiner Communication and Relationship Management

### Direct Communication Methods

#### In DOB Build
- **Messaging tab**: Application portal has "Messages" or "Examiner Comments" section
- **Appropriate use**: Clarifying questions, requesting status update, or providing supplemental information
- **Response time**: Examiners typically respond within 2–3 business days
- **Document**: All communication logged in portal and part of official record

#### Email to Examiner
- **Examiner contact**: Email address usually on objection letter or in application portal
- **Subject line**: Always include job number and brief description
- **Format**: Professional, concise; avoid lengthy emails
- **Response time**: 3–5 business days (slower than portal messaging in Build)
- **Do not expect immediate response**: Examiners handle 30–50+ applications simultaneously

#### Phone Contact
- **Plan Desk general line**: [VERIFY]: Current DOB Plan Desk phone number?
- **Do not use for application-specific questions**: Plan Desk cannot access individual applications
- **Use for**: General policy questions, system status, or escalation if examiner unresponsive for 2+ weeks
- **Success rate**: Low — most calls routed to voicemail with callback request

#### In-Person Appointment
- **When available**: Some examiners allow in-person meetings at DOB offices
- **Purpose**: Complex objections, design clarification, or informal code discussion before formal response
- **Process**: [VERIFY]: Can expediers request in-person meeting with Manhattan plan examiner? What office location and how to schedule?
- **Benefits**: Face-to-face discussion sometimes resolves objection faster than written response
- **Drawback**: Very time-consuming and not always allowed

### Best Practices for Examiner Communication

1. **Be professional and respectful**: Examiners are not paid more to handle objections; treat them as partners in problem-solving
2. **Keep communication concise**: One-sentence questions get faster responses than three-paragraph emails
3. **Do not question examiner judgment in writing**: If objection seems incorrect, address in formal response document or request informal meeting, not in adversarial email
4. **Ask clarifying questions before responding**: If objection is ambiguous, email asking for clarification before spending time on incorrect response
5. **Provide context**: If objection relates to previous design iteration or examiner comment, reference it briefly
6. **Proactive communication**: If you foresee delay in response, email examiner with update ("Response in progress, will be submitted by Friday")
7. **Escalate appropriately**: If examiner is unresponsive for 14+ days, escalate to Plan Desk; do not send repeated emails

### Common Issues with Examiner Communication

#### Examiner Not Responding to Emails
**Problem**: Examiner stops responding to emails; application "stuck" with no communication.

**Causes**:
- Examiner on vacation or medical leave (no coverage assigned)
- Examiner workload spike causing email backlog
- Email went to spam or examiner missed it
- Examiner reassigned mid-examination (see below)

**Solutions**:
1. Call DOB Plan Desk: "I've been trying to reach [Examiner Name] on [Job Number] for 2 weeks with no response"
2. Request examiner reassignment if examiner has been unresponsive for 14+ days
3. Do not resend the same email repeatedly; escalate instead

#### Examiner Reassignment
**Situation**: Application has been assigned to 2nd or 3rd examiner (team example: 927 Broadway reassigned twice).

**When reassignment occurs**:
- Original examiner on leave or departed
- Application reassigned for workload balancing
- Rare: First examiner recused due to conflict

**Impact**: New examiner may start review from scratch or continue from objections stage (unclear). Typically adds 1–2 weeks to timeline.

### Informal Code Discussion (Pre-Application Meetings)

Some applicants prefer to discuss potential code issues with DOB before formal filing:

#### Pre-Application Meeting
- **Purpose**: Get preliminary feedback on proposed design and code compliance strategy
- **Participants**: Applicant, architect/engineer, sometimes examiner
- **Typical issues discussed**: Novel code interpretation, unusual request, desire to confirm strategy before design finalized
- **Timeline**: 2–4 weeks to schedule; meeting takes 1–2 hours
- **Cost**: None; DOB provides free pre-application consultation
- **Benefit**: May avoid major objections if examiner provides guidance upfront

## AI-1: Administrative Inquiry (Code Interpretation Request)

### What is an AI-1?

An **AI-1 (Administrative Inquiry)** is a formal request to DOB for administrative review and official code interpretation on a specific code question or technical issue.

### When to Use AI-1

- **Examiner interpretation seems incorrect**: You believe examiner misinterpreted code
- **Novel code question**: Code section is ambiguous or has not been clarified by DOB
- **Precedent request**: You want DOB to confirm that similar work was previously approved
- **Objection dispute**: You disagree with examiner's objection and want higher-level review

### When NOT to Use AI-1

- **Time-critical approvals**: AI-1 takes 4–8 weeks; not suitable for urgent permits
- **Simple objections**: If response plan addresses issue clearly, respond normally rather than request AI-1
- **Subjective disagreements**: "I think your objection is wrong" is not suitable; requires technical code issue

### Process and Timeline

1. **Request submission**: Written request submitted to DOB Plan Desk (address [VERIFY]: where exactly?)
2. **Application number and specific question**: Clearly state which code section and what interpretation sought
3. **Supporting information**: Provide background, proposed solution, and why examiner's interpretation is questioned
4. **Review**: Forwarded to higher-level reviewer (Chief Examiner, Policy Office, or external code expert)
5. **Response timeline**: Typically 4–8 weeks for formal response
6. **Ruling**: DOB issues written interpretation that examiner must follow

## Pro Tips for Expediting Objection Resolution

### Strategic Timing
1. **File before holiday**: If filing before Thanksgiving/Christmas, understand examiners may be backed up; plan response timeline accordingly
2. **Resubmit early in week**: Submitting response early Monday allows examiner to review before Friday; Friday submission might sit until Monday
3. **Monthly cycles**: Some examiners have monthly check-in cycles; if you miss one cycle, wait for next

### Response Quality
1. **Over-respond, not under-respond**: If objection seems minor, still provide complete response. Vague responses often trigger additional objections.
2. **Preempt follow-up objections**: When responding to one objection, check if your response creates issues elsewhere in plans (e.g., adding secondary exit may require egress path revision)
3. **Professional certification value**: If pro-cert available and feasible, consider it; examiners often approve pro-cert responses faster (no further objections)

### Relationship Building
1. **Learn examiner name and style**: After first objection, research examiner's previous projects if possible; understand their typical objection patterns
2. **Respectful tone in writing**: Even if frustrated, maintain professional tone; examiners notice and reciprocate
3. **Informality when appropriate**: After multiple interactions, informal email asking for clarification is okay; don't stay formal if relationship allows

### Know When to Escalate
1. **Unreasonable objection**: If objection contradicts Building Code or prior DOB rulings, escalate via AI-1 or Plan Desk call (do not waste time on bad response)
2. **Repeated requests for same correction**: If revised plans submitted and examiner issues identical objection again, escalate to examiner supervisor
3. **Examiner inconsistency**: If examiner's objections contradict their own prior comments, ask for clarification in writing and escalate if not resolved

## Objection Resolution Checklist

Use this checklist when responding to objections:

- [ ] All objections read and understood (code sections identified)
- [ ] Professional responsible for each type of objection assigned
- [ ] Revised plans prepared and clearly marked as revised
- [ ] All plan sheets updated (not just one page)
- [ ] Professional seals and signatures present
- [ ] Written response addresses each objection individually
- [ ] Response references revised plan pages
- [ ] Supporting documentation complete (calculations, cut sheets, precedents)
- [ ] Response submitted before deadline (preferably 2+ days early)
- [ ] Submission confirmed (portal confirmation or email read receipt)
- [ ] Examiner thanked for review (professional courtesy)
- [ ] Escalation planned if re-examination takes >2 weeks without communication

## Escalation and When to Contact Management

Escalate to GLE management if:

1. **Unreasonable objection**: Objection clearly violates Building Code or prior DOB approval
2. **Unresponsive examiner**: No communication for 14+ days after response submitted
3. **Repeated objections**: Same issue objected to twice after response submitted
4. **Examiner conflict or bias**: Examiner appears to have personal objection to project or applicant
5. **Novel objection**: Objection relates to code provision not encountered before and GLE guidance unclear
6. **Cost/timeline impact**: Objection resolution requires significant design change or timeline extension; client impact assessment needed

# BPP (Builders Pavement Plan) Approval and Permit Guide

## Overview

A Builders Pavement Plan (BPP) is a detailed plan showing how sidewalks, curbs, and other public right-of-way areas will be protected, maintained, or modified during construction. The BPP approval is a prerequisite for most construction projects in NYC that impact the public space.

## When BPP is Required

BPP approval is required for:

- **New building construction** - all new buildings must have BPP approval before permits are issued
- **Major alterations** affecting sidewalk width, curb cut, or street frontage
- **Excavation work** that impacts the sidewalk or curb area
- **Demolition** of existing structures
- **Scaffolding and hoisting** operations affecting public space
- **Construction fencing** or protective sidewalk sheds
- **Basement construction** or subsurface work extending under the sidewalk
- Any project where a DOT Street Work Permit is required

### When BPP May Not Be Required

- Interior-only renovations with no sidewalk/curb impact
- Routine maintenance within property lines [VERIFY]
- Small alterations under Department of Buildings (DOB) minor alteration thresholds without public space impact

## BPP vs. DOT vs. DOB: Understanding the Coordination

### Three Separate Approvals/Permits

**BPP Approval (DOT)**
- Approval of the plan showing how public space will be managed
- Issued by NYC Department of Transportation
- Non-binding design approval; doesn't authorize street work
- Required before DOB permits can be issued

**BPP Permit (DOT)**
- Actual authorization to perform work affecting public space
- Issued by DOT after BPP approval and DOB construction permit
- Required to begin work on sidewalk, curb, or street areas
- May come after or simultaneously with DOB permits [VERIFY exact sequence]

**DOB Permits (General/Building/Alteration)**
- Issued by Department of Buildings for the project itself
- Cannot be issued until BPP is approved
- Separate from BPP approval/permit
- Covers the actual construction activity

### Key Coordination Point
You must obtain BPP approval from DOT **before** submitting for DOB permits. Many expeditors handle both applications simultaneously in preparation, but DOT BPP approval must come first in the approval sequence.

## BPP Filing Process: Step-by-Step

### Phase 1: Preparation

1. **Engage a Licensed Professional**
   - BPP must be prepared by a licensed architect, engineer, or landscape architect
   - Professional stamp and signature required on all submissions
   - Consider expediter coordination with design professional

2. **Site Assessment**
   - Visit the site and document existing conditions
   - Photograph sidewalk, curb, utilities, street furniture
   - Identify utilities (Con Edison, Verizon, steam lines, subway)
   - Check for subway vaults or utility pits affecting design options
   - Note nearby hydrants, bus stops, loading zones

3. **Preliminary DOT Scoping Meeting** [VERIFY if required for all projects]
   - May be required for complex projects
   - DOT Manhattan or Brooklyn office (depending on location)
   - Present preliminary plans and site conditions
   - Identify potential issues early

### Phase 2: Document Preparation

4. **Prepare BPP Drawings**
   - Existing conditions plan showing sidewalk width, curb condition, utilities, street elements
   - Proposed BPP plan with:
     - Maintained sidewalk width for pedestrian passage (typically minimum 8 feet clear width required)
     - Location of protective scaffolding/sidewalk shed if needed
     - Utility protection details
     - Drainage and water management
     - Traffic control and pedestrian routing
     - Loading and unloading area (if applicable)
   - Multiple sheets may be required for large sites or complex conditions

5. **Prepare Required Documentation**
   - **Property Survey** - current survey showing property lines, building footprint, dimensions (typically required; dated within 1 year or updated)
   - **Site Plan** - from DOB application with BPP overlaid
   - **Insurance Documentation** - General Liability insurance (typically $1M minimum; [VERIFY current minimum])
   - **Proof of Insurance Certificate** - for contractor performing work
   - **Utility Company Clearances** - if work affects utilities (sometimes required before approval)
   - **Architect/Engineer's Certification** - professional seal on all plans

6. **Prepare Application Package**
   - Completed BPP application form [VERIFY current DOT form number]
   - Cover letter summarizing project scope and BPP features
   - Professional's certification letter
   - Insurance documents
   - All drawings and specifications

### Phase 3: Submission

7. **Submit to DOT**
   - **Department of Transportation - Bureau of Public Carriage and Right of Way**
   - Location: 33 Beaver Street, New York, NY 10004 (Manhattan) or relevant borough office [VERIFY current addresses]
   - Submission method: In person or by mail (electronic submission capability - [VERIFY current status])
   - Include cover letter listing all documents
   - Retain copies for your records and future reference

8. **Track Application**
   - Request confirmation of receipt with application number
   - Note submission date (important for timeline tracking)
   - Document all communications

### Phase 4: DOT Review and Approval

9. **Initial Review**
   - DOT reviews for completeness (5-10 business days typical)
   - May request additional information or revised drawings
   - Deficiency notices may be issued for missing documents or unclear details

10. **Plan Review**
    - DOT examines plans for:
      - Adequate pedestrian passage
      - Proper utility protection
      - Safe traffic control measures
      - Sidewalk shed design if applicable
      - Compliance with DOT standards and guidelines
    - May take 10-20 business days depending on complexity

11. **Approval**
    - BPP Approval letter issued (non-binding design approval)
    - Approval valid for [VERIFY duration - typically 1 year]
    - Document number and conditions noted in approval

### Phase 5: Obtaining BPP Permit (After DOB Permit Issuance)

12. **With DOB General Permit, Return to DOT**
    - Once DOB issues General Permit, return to DOT with:
      - BPP Approval letter
      - DOB General Permit copy
      - Updated insurance certificate
      - Any modified plans agreed to during DOB review
    - Obtain BPP Permit (authorizes actual street work)

13. **Final Verification Before Work**
    - Verify all site conditions match approved plans
    - Confirm contractor insurance is active and current
    - Schedule DOT inspection if required for complex scenarios
    - Photograph conditions before work begins

## BPP Approval vs. BPP Permit: Critical Distinction

### BPP Approval
- **What it is:** Non-binding design approval of the plan
- **Issued by:** NYC Department of Transportation
- **When issued:** Early in project timeline, typically 4-8 weeks from submission
- **What it authorizes:** Nothing - it's design review only
- **Next step:** Proceed to DOB permit applications
- **Duration:** Valid approximately 1 year [VERIFY exact validity period]

### BPP Permit
- **What it is:** Binding authorization to perform work affecting public space
- **Issued by:** NYC Department of Transportation
- **When issued:** After DOB General Permit is issued; typically 1-2 weeks
- **What it authorizes:** Actual work in sidewalk, curb, and street areas
- **Prerequisites:** Must have both BPP Approval AND DOB General Permit
- **Duration:** Valid until job completion; tied to DOB permit period

### Common Confusion
Many applicants think BPP Approval = permission to start work. This is incorrect. BPP Approval is design review only. You cannot begin street work until you have:
1. BPP Approval from DOT (design approved)
2. General Permit from DOB (building work authorized)
3. BPP Permit from DOT (street work authorized)

## Common Issues and Delays

### Issue: Insufficient Sidewalk Width in BPP Plan
**Problem:** Plan shows less than required pedestrian passage (typically 8 feet minimum clear width)
**Solution:** Modify BPP to maintain adequate pedestrian access or propose temporary pedestrian route around site

### Issue: Utility Conflicts
**Problem:** Proposed work conflicts with underground utilities (gas, electric, telecommunications)
**Solution:**
- Request utility verification from Con Ed, Verizon, etc.
- Modify plan to avoid conflicts or build in utility protection
- Obtain utility clearance letter before DOT approval

### Issue: Incomplete or Missing Insurance Documentation
**Problem:** Proof of insurance missing, inadequate coverage amounts, or City of New York not named as additional insured
**Solution:**
- Obtain updated insurance certificate from contractor
- Ensure $1M (or current minimum) General Liability coverage
- Verify City of New York is listed as additional insured [VERIFY exact requirement]
- Resubmit complete package

### Issue: Unclear or Inadequate Drawings
**Problem:** Plans lack sufficient detail on sidewalk configuration, utility locations, or traffic control
**Solution:**
- Work with professional to add detail
- Provide section views showing sidewalk shed construction, if applicable
- Include traffic control plan if street lanes affected
- Resubmit with covering letter explaining changes

### Issue: Subway Vault or Infrastructure Conflict
**Problem:** Site is above or adjacent to NYC subway infrastructure
**Solution:**
- Engage NYC Transit (via MTA) early for coordination
- May require special conditions in BPP approval
- Professional must certify compliance with Transit requirements
- Timeline may extend significantly

### Issue: Multiple Rounds of Deficiencies
**Problem:** DOT issues deficiency notices; corrections submitted but new issues found
**Solution:**
- At deficiency stage, request meeting with DOT reviewer to understand all concerns
- Provide comprehensive revised package addressing all issues at once
- Include cover letter explaining each change in response to deficiency

### Issue: Expired Approval
**Problem:** BPP Approval expires before DOB permit is issued
**Solution:**
- Obtain approval extension from DOT before expiration
- May require updated site conditions survey if approval is very old
- Some projects may need resubmission

## Relationship to Related Permits and Approvals

### DOT Street Work Permit
- May be required for work affecting street area (excavation, utility work)
- Separate from BPP but related
- Often filed simultaneously with BPP
- Must have BPP approval to proceed with Street Work Permit

### Sidewalk (Curb Cut) Permit
- Required if project modifies curb or creates new curb cut
- Filed with DOT separately or as part of BPP application [VERIFY]
- Different from BPP but often processed together
- Requires professional certification

### Sidewalk Shed (Protective Structure) Permit
- Required if protective scaffolding over sidewalk is needed
- Design specifications must meet DOT standards
- May be approved as part of BPP or separately [VERIFY]
- Adds complexity and timeline
- Contractor must have qualified shed installer

### Crane Permit (Hoisting Operations Permit)
- Required for any crane work affecting public space
- Separate from BPP but subject to same DOT coordination
- May have different timeline and approval requirements
- Professional engineer must certify load and rigging

### Construction Fencing
- Planning Board approval typically not required for standard fencing
- Shown on BPP for pedestrian safety and site control
- Specifications for height, materials, and screening per DOT standards
- Part of BPP review and approval

### Remedial Action Plan (RAP) / Environmental Permits
- If subsurface work triggers environmental concerns, RAP may be required separately
- Filed with NYC Department of Environmental Protection (DEP)
- Outside BPP scope but project cannot proceed without clearance
- Timeline impacts overall project schedule

## Key Takeaways for Expediting

1. **BPP Approval is a prerequisite** - Cannot obtain DOB permits without BPP approval
2. **Two-step DOT process** - BPP Approval (design review) then BPP Permit (authorization)
3. **Prepare thoroughly** - Incomplete packages cause multiple rounds of delays
4. **Professional requirements** - Architect/engineer seal required; cannot expedite without licensed professional
5. **Early utility coordination** - Identify and resolve utility conflicts early
6. **Insurance critical** - Ensure correct coverage and City is named insured
7. **Timeline realistic** - 8-16 weeks is typical from document prep to construction start; more for complex projects
8. **Concurrent processing** - BPP and DOB applications can be prepared in parallel but BPP must approve first
9. **Track deadlines** - Approvals can expire; monitor validity periods and request extensions if needed
10. **Document coordination** - BPP integrates with Street Work Permits, Sidewalk Permits, Shed Permits, and Crane Permits

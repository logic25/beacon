# Certificate of Occupancy (CO) and Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO) Guide

## Overview

A **Certificate of Occupancy (CO)** is the legal document issued by the New York City Department of Buildings that certifies a building's use classification, occupancy classification, and construction type. It is the final authorization required before a building can be legally occupied. No building can be lawfully occupied without a valid CO or temporary CO.

The **Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO)** allows occupancy for a limited period (90 days, renewable) when minor outstanding items remain, providing flexibility during construction closeout.

---

## CO vs. TCO: Key Differences

| Aspect | Certificate of Occupancy (CO) | Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO) |
|--------|------|------|
| **Duration** | Permanent; remains in effect | 90 days from issuance; must be renewed or converted to CO |
| **Prerequisites** | All work complete, all inspections passed, all certificates obtained | Outstanding items allowed if non-critical to occupancy safety |
| **Use** | Final occupancy authorization | Allows phased occupancy while completing minor work |
| **Renewal** | Not required | Must be renewed or final CO obtained before expiration |

## Letter of Completion (LOC)

The **Letter of Completion (LOC)** is a closing document for ALT-2 and PAA jobs that do not result in a change to the building's CO. It certifies that all required work has been completed in accordance with the approved plans and that no outstanding items remain.

**When LOC is used instead of CO:**
- ALT-2 (Alteration Type 2): Work that does not change the building's use, occupancy, or CO-level information
- PAA (Place of Assembly) jobs: Minor use-specific compliance items
- Work that affects only operational or compliance details without modifying the building's registered CO

## Types of Certificates of Occupancy

### New Building CO
Issued for newly constructed buildings upon completion of all construction and inspections. Establishes the building's initial occupancy classification and permitted uses.

### ALT-CO (Alteration Certificate of Occupancy)
Amends an existing CO when alterations change the building's use classification, occupancy type, or structural/systems information. The new ALT-CO supersedes the previous CO.

### Schedule A (Job-Specific Items)
Items specific to a particular job or alteration project. These appear on the CO and must be satisfied before CO issuance.

### Schedule B (Building-Wide Items)
Items affecting the entire building's compliance. These must also be cleared before CO issuance.

## When CO is Required vs. When LOC is Sufficient

### CO Required
- **New Building Construction:** Any new building must obtain a new CO
- **Change of Occupancy (ALT-1):** Any alteration that changes how the building is used or who can occupy it
- **Use Change:** Converting office to residential, retail to office, etc.
- **Occupancy Classification Change:** Altering density, egress, or occupancy limits
- **Structural or Code-Level Changes:** Modifications that affect building classification or permitted uses

### LOC Sufficient
- **ALT-2 Work:** Interior renovations, mechanical updates, or systems work that do not change building use or CO information
- **PAA Work:** Place of Assembly approvals that involve use-specific operational items only
- **Compliance Updates:** Work that addresses code violations without changing the building's registered use or occupancy

## TCO Process

### Step 1: Inspection Readiness
All required inspections must be passed or only minor, non-safety-critical items may remain outstanding. Critical items (fire safety, egress, structural integrity) must be resolved before TCO issuance.

### Step 2: TCO Request
Submit a formal request for TCO through DOB (via BUILD or in-person at 280 Broadway). Include:
- Building address and BIS/Build job number
- List of outstanding items (if any)
- Justification for temporary issuance (why final CO cannot yet be issued)

### Step 3: DOB Review and Issuance
DOB examines outstanding items and determines if TCO can be issued. TCO is issued with:
- List of approved conditions
- Explicit list of outstanding work items
- 90-day expiration date
- Any special restrictions on building use

### Step 4: Occupancy Under TCO
Building may be occupied subject to the approved conditions and with work on outstanding items ongoing.

### Step 5: Renewal or Final CO
Before TCO expires:
- **Complete outstanding work** and request final CO, OR
- **Request TCO renewal** for another 90 days

## Final CO Process

### Step 1: Work Completion
All construction work must be 100% complete per approved plans and specifications. Any deviations require formal change orders or amended plans.

### Step 2: Inspections Passed
All required inspections must be signed off in the system, including:
- Progress inspections (if applicable)
- TR (Technical Report) inspections
- Special inspections (structural steel, concrete, sprinkler systems, etc.)
- Final inspection

### Step 3: Certificates Obtained
All required third-party certifications must be uploaded to Build/BIS:
- Elevator certification and sign-off
- Boiler certification
- Fire alarm acceptance
- Sprinkler system acceptance
- Mechanical, electrical, plumbing certificates (if required)

### Step 4: BIS Items Satisfied
All items flagged in the BIS (Build Item System) must be resolved:
- All inspection sign-offs recorded
- All compliance documents uploaded
- All violations cleared or resolved

### Step 5: Final Inspection Request
Submit request for final inspection through DOB. Ensure building is inspection-ready (accessible, all work visible, no safety hazards).

### Step 6: Final Inspection Conducted
DOB inspector verifies that all work matches approved plans and no outstanding items exist.

### Step 7: CO Issued
Once inspection passes and all items satisfied, DOB issues the CO, which records:
- Building address and block/lot
- Occupancy classification (A, B, E, F, M, R, S, U)
- Construction type (I, II, III, IV, V)
- Building use and permitted occupancies
- Any job-specific or building-wide conditions

## Common Items That Hold Up CO Issuance

Based on typical expediting experience, the following items frequently delay final CO:

### DOB Inspections
- **TR1 (Energy Code Compliance - Initial):** Energy code documentation incomplete or rejected in QA
- **TR5:** [VERIFY: Team referenced TR5s as a holding item—what does TR5 inspection cover?]
- **TR8 (Energy Code Compliance - Final):** Final energy compliance certification not yet filed or accepted
- **EN2 (Final Energy Compliance Sheet):** MEP engineer or RCP has not signed final energy compliance documentation

### Fire Safety
- **FDNY Fire Alarm Acceptance:** Fire alarm system must be accepted by FDNY; DOB will not issue CO without this
- **Sprinkler Sign-Off:** Sprinkler system acceptance certificate missing or deficient
- **Fire Resistance Ratings:** Any deficiencies in fire-rated walls, doors, or assemblies

### Elevator and Vertical Transportation
- **Elevator Sign-Off:** Elevator certification and approval certificate required; common cause of final delays

### PTA Items (Tax Abatement)
- **PTA4: Solar Tax Abatement Application (Final):** If solar abatement was filed, final acceptance required
- **PTA4: Tax Abatement Eligibility Approval:** Abatement eligibility confirmation from Department of Finance

### Environmental Compliance
- **FEMA Elevation Certificate (086-0-33):** For buildings in FEMA flood zones; may require floodproofing certification
- **Floodproofing Certificate (086-0-34):** If floodproofing is part of the project scope

### Violations and Code Issues
- **Open Violations on Property:** Any existing violations on the building record (not related to current job) may need resolution before CO
- **Outstanding Administrative Items:** Missing owner correspondence, fees unpaid, or unresolved conflicts between plans and actual construction

## BIS (Build Item System) and Build Items

**BIS Items** are specific compliance requirements that must be satisfied before the system permits CO issuance. These include:

- **Inspection Sign-Offs:** Actual inspection results must be uploaded and signed
- **Compliance Documents:** Certificates, approvals, and technical reports must be attached
- **Owner and Professional Signatures:** All required approvals on file
- **Amended Plans (if applicable):** Any field changes must be documented

Build items appear as a checklist in the system. All items must show "Complete" before final CO can be issued.

## Pro Tips for Smooth CO Closures

[VERIFY: What are the most common CO blockers your team encounters on typical projects? What tricks or best practices has GLE developed to speed up CO issuance?]

### Best Practices
- **Start early:** Do not wait until final inspection to gather certificates. Begin collecting them during construction.
- **Coordinate inspections:** Schedule inspections in logical order to avoid double-booking and inspector conflicts.
- **FDNY coordination:** Initiate FDNY fire alarm and sprinkler acceptance early; FDNY moves slower than DOB and often is the critical path.
- **Energy compliance:** Ensure EN2 sheets are prepared by MEP engineer and RCP-signed well before final inspection.
- **Build hygiene:** Keep all uploads and documents organized in Build to facilitate QA review and sign-off.

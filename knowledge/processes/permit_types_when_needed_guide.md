# When Do You Need a Permit? — Filing Type Decision Guide

## Overview

One of the most critical first questions in any construction project is: "Does this work require a Department of Buildings permit?" Not all construction activities require permits. NYC Building Code, as adopted in 1 RCNY §101-14 (Rules of the City of New York, Chapter 1, Title 1, Section 101-14), defines work that is exempt from permitting. However, once a permit is determined to be necessary, selecting the correct filing type is equally important. This guide helps expediers and clients understand thresholds for permitting and which filing type applies to the proposed work.

**Note**: When in doubt about whether work requires a permit, recommend that the client consult with a licensed architect or engineer, or request a pre-application meeting with DOB Plan Desk.

## Work That Does NOT Require a Permit

The following work is classified as "ordinary repair" or "maintenance" under 1 RCNY §101-14 and does **not** require a DOB permit:

### Interior Work (Non-Structural)
- **Painting**: All interior and exterior painting, including surface preparation (sanding, priming)
- **Plastering and wall finishing**: Patching, finishing, skim coating (non-structural plaster work)
- **Wallpapering**: Installation of wallpaper, vinyl, or similar wall coverings
- **Flooring installation**: Carpet, vinyl, tile, or wood flooring in existing spaces (no structural modification of substrate)

### Fixtures and Utilities (Same Location, Same Count)
- **Plumbing fixture replacement**: Replacing existing sinks, toilets, faucets, or showers in the same location with the same number of fixtures [VERIFY: Does this include replacing a bathtub with a shower in same location?]
- **Kitchen cabinets**: Replacing existing kitchen cabinetry (not structural reconfiguration)
- **Lighting fixtures**: Replacing existing light fixtures
- **Electrical outlets and switches**: Replacing existing outlets, switches, or outlets with switches (same location, same electrical load)

### Exterior Work (Non-Structural)
- **Window replacement**: Replacing windows in existing openings with windows of same size and material [VERIFY: Still exempt? Any recent DOB guidance?]
- **Door replacement**: Replacing doors in existing openings (entrance, interior, or sliding doors)
- **Driveway surfacing**: Resurfacing an existing driveway with asphalt, concrete, or similar material without changing grade or drainage [VERIFY: Confirm — team question was "To replace cement on existing driveway in BK, 600sf, I need plans, right?" Is this exempt or does it require permit?]
- **Roof patching**: Repair and patching of roof (non-structural, no structural decking replacement) [VERIFY: Where is the line between patching and structural roofing work that requires permit?]

### Miscellaneous Ordinary Repairs
- **HVAC maintenance**: Servicing, cleaning, or minor repair of existing HVAC systems (not replacement of system)
- **Water heater servicing**: Maintenance or minor repair of existing water heater (not replacement with different type) [VERIFY: Does replacing gas water heater with tankless electric water heater require permit?]
- **Caulking and weatherization**: Sealing gaps, weatherproofing, application of sealants
- **Fencing repair**: Repair of existing fence (replacement of boards, posts in kind) [VERIFY: Does replacement of entire fence section require permit? What about new fence installation?]

### What "Ordinary Repair" Means in Practice
Ordinary repair means the work restores existing building systems or components to their original condition, using materials of the same type and specification, without changing the building's character, use, or occupancy. If the work involves a change in function, location, type of system, or structural modification, a permit is required.

## Work That REQUIRES a Permit

### Structural Work
- Any modification to structural frame, columns, beams, or bearing walls
- Removal of any wall (load-bearing or non-load-bearing, in some cases)
- Installation of new structural members (posts, beams, shear walls)
- Foundation work or underpinning
- Floor system modifications (adding joists, changing span, adding/removing floor openings)
- Roof system modifications (replacing deck, changing structure, adding loads)
- Lateral bracing systems (wind bracing, seismic work)

### Change of Use or Occupancy
- Converting residential to commercial (or vice versa)
- Converting office to restaurant or other food service
- Converting office to medical/health-related facility
- Converting space to assembly occupancy (meeting rooms, performance spaces)
- Any change in occupancy classification under Building Code
- **Filing type**: ALT-1

### Egress and Life Safety
- New or modified exit doors, stairways, corridors, or elevators
- Changes to occupant load or exit capacity
- Modifications to fire separation walls or rated partitions
- Installation of fire suppression systems (sprinklers, standpipe, CO₂)
- Changes to rated doors, windows, or glazing
- **Filing type**: ALT-1

### Building Systems — New Installation or Major Changes
- **Plumbing**: New fixtures, new locations, new service lines, new drainage branches
- **Electrical**: New service entrance, new panels, new branch circuits (beyond minor outlet/switch replacement)
- **HVAC**: New system installation, major system changes, or ductwork additions
- **Gas**: New gas line, gas meter relocation, new appliance connections beyond simple appliance swap
- **Fire protection**: Sprinkler systems, standpipe, alarm systems, CO detection

### Openings and Penetrations
- New windows or doors (beyond replacement in existing openings)
- New wall openings
- New floor openings (skylights, light wells, mechanical shafts)
- Stair removal or relocation

### Exterior and Structural
- **Roofing work**: Full or partial roof replacement where structural deck is modified or removed
- **Facade work**: Exterior wall repair, replacement, or modification (beyond painting/caulking)
- **Signage**: Permanent signage above 40 sq ft or at building edge (check local zoning)
- **Solar panels**: Installation of roof-mounted or ground-mounted photovoltaic systems [VERIFY: What filing type and current approval timeline?]
- **Antennas, satellite dishes**: Structural roof penetrations

### Accessibility and Code Compliance Upgrades
- ADA ramps and accessibility modifications
- Accessible restroom installations
- Grab bar and safety rail installations
- Accessible elevator or lift installations

## Filing Type Decision Tree

Once a permit is determined to be necessary, use this decision tree to select the correct filing type:

### Step 1: Is this a new building (ground-up construction)?
- **YES** → **New Building (NB)** filing
- **NO** → Continue to Step 2

### Step 2: Does the work involve a change in occupancy or use group?
- **YES** → **ALT-1** (Alteration with Change of Occupancy)
- **NO** → Continue to Step 3

### Step 3: Does the work involve structural modifications or new egress?
- **YES** → **ALT-1** (Alteration with Structural Work or New Egress)
- **NO** → Continue to Step 4

### Step 4: Is the work one single trade or building system?
- **YES** → **Single-trade filing** (see below for specific types)
- **NO** (multiple work types) → **ALT-2** (General Alteration)

### Step 5: If Single-Trade Work, What Trade?
- **Plumbing only** → **PAA** (Plumbing Alteration Application) if fixtures; **EW** if water supply reconfiguration
- **Electrical only** → **EW** (Electrical Work) via DOB NOW [VERIFY: Or now in Build?]
- **HVAC only** → **Mechanical** (MEP alteration) via Build [VERIFY: Confirmation]
- **Fire protection/sprinklers only** → **Fire Protection** filing [VERIFY: Proper filing type and location]
- **Elevator/lift only** → **Elevator** filing via DOB NOW [VERIFY: Confirmation]
- **Facade/structural exterior only** → **ALT-1** (may require structural engineer certification)

### Step 6: Is This Assembly Work (75+ Occupants)?
- **Work on space with 75+ persons capacity** → **PAA** (Place of Assembly Alteration)
- Filing type: PAA

## After-Hours Work Permits and Saturday Authorization

### When Required
Work performed outside standard building hours (Monday–Friday, 7:00 AM–6:00 PM) requires special authorization.

### Standard Hours vs After-Hours
- **Standard hours**: Monday–Friday, 7:00 AM–6:00 PM (no special permit needed)
- **After-hours work**: Work outside standard hours on weekdays, or any work on Saturday/Sunday
- **Saturday work**: Requires **After Hours Variance (AHV)** approval from DOB

## Professional Certification vs Full DOB Review

### Professional Certification (Pro-Cert)
- Licensed professional architect or engineer self-certifies that plans comply with applicable codes
- Faster approval (typically 3–5 business days)
- Subject to random DOB audit (5–10% audit rate [VERIFY: Current audit statistics?])
- **Best for**: Straightforward work, experienced professional, low audit risk tolerance
- **Risk**: Audit can result in correction work or fines if errors found

### Full DOB Review (Plan Examination)
- DOB examiner reviews all plans and issues objections
- Slower approval (typically 2–4 weeks, depending on objections)
- No audit risk
- **Best for**: Complex work, novel code questions, highest confidence required
- **Cost**: Same base fee; no additional cost for examination

### When to Recommend Each
- **Pro-cert recommended**: Simple ALT-2, straightforward plumbing or electrical, experienced professional
- **DOB review recommended**: First-time applicant, complex structural work, novel code issue, assembly occupancy

## Legalization and "No-Work" Permits

### What is Legalization?
Legalization filing formalizes existing conditions without proposing new work. Example: Building has been operating as a two-family home but was never legally converted; file ALT-1 (no-work) to legalize the occupancy.

### Process
1. Document existing conditions with "as-built" plans
2. File ALT-1 or PAA indicating "No new work proposed"
3. DOB reviews existing conditions for code compliance
4. If deficiencies found, either request waiver or propose remedial work
5. Approval issued; certificate of occupancy can be amended if needed

### Common Uses
- Legalizing illegal conversions (residential subdivision, secondary dwelling)
- Formalizing long-term use changes that occurred without permits
- Correcting records after buildings have been operating in existing state for years

## Pro Tips for Filing Type Selection

- **When in doubt, file ALT-1**: More conservative, prevents rejection due to wrong filing type
- **Consult the client's architect/engineer first**: They should guide filing type selection
- **If you need to pivot**: Filing wrong type and then withdrawing/refiling costs time and can alert examiner to scope confusion
- **Assembly (75+ persons) always PAA**: Do not try to hide assembly work within ALT-2
- **Multiple trades often = ALT-2**: Unless there is also a use change or structural work
- **Trust the decision tree**: If you follow the steps above, filing type will be correct

# DOB Acceptance: Use of Tempered Glass for Sloped Glazing (Skylight)

**Project:** Verizon Sloped Glazing | **Engineer:** Richard C. Apfel, P.E. (License #58539) | **Contractor:** Whitestone Construction Corp., Don Arthur | **Date:** November 29, 2006 | **DOB Reviewer:** Max S. Lee, P.E., Acting Deputy Borough Commissioner | **Outcome:** ACCEPTED — OK to use tempered glass as sloped glazing with wire or laminated on interior light.

---

## PE Analysis

Following the New York State code and accepted industry standards, sloped glazing (skylight) requires a dual-pane approach. The exterior light should be tempered and the interior light should be either wire or laminated glass.

Tempered glass has a multiplying factor of 4 per Table 10-8 of the NYC Building Code — it is 4 times the strength of standard glass. This provides structural strength on the exterior. Wire or laminated glass (multiplying factor of 0.5 and 0.6 respectively) is used on the interior to capture any debris should the tempered glass fail. The tempered glass is 8 times stronger than the wired or laminated glass.

## DOB Reviewer Notes

OK to use tempered glass as sloped glass. Alternative: wire glass in lieu of tempered. Interior light must be wire or laminated. First story applications — use wire or laminated on inner light.

---

## Practical Impact

**Sloped glazing requires two layers:** exterior = tempered (for strength), interior = wire or laminated (for debris capture). Tempered glass alone is not acceptable for sloped/overhead glazing because when it fails, it shatters into small pieces rather than holding together.

**Table 10-8 of the NYC Building Code** is the key reference for glass strength multiplying factors in sloped glazing applications. This table governs the engineering analysis for any skylight or sloped glass installation.

**A PE letter resolves glazing objections.** When DOB objects to a glass type for skylights or sloped glazing, a PE letter analyzing the specific glazing package against code requirements and Table 10-8 factors can resolve the objection without a formal reconsideration.

**This applies to any project with overhead or sloped glass** — not just skylights. Atriums, clerestory windows, sloped curtain walls, and greenhouse-style additions all trigger the same dual-pane requirement.

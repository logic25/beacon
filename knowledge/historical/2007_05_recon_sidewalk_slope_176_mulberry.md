# Reconsideration: Sidewalk Slope Transition — 176 Mulberry Street, Manhattan

**Location:** 176 Mulberry Street, Manhattan | **Job Number:** 103692185 | **BPP Application:** #103732007 | **Applicant:** Thomas J. Velleca, P.E. (NY PE #077414) | **Filing Type:** BPP (Builder's Pavement Plan) — Alteration | **Recon Date:** June 20, 2006 | **Reviewer:** Kalyan K. Bhattacharyya (May 31, 2007) | **Outcome:** DENIED initially, then partial acceptance with required revisions for sidewalk transition compliance.

---

## The Objection

DOB objected that the sidewalk slope between the south end of the overhead door and the end of new sidewalk within 1 foot of the building is approximately 11%, exceeding permissible limits.

## The Argument

The construction at 176 Mulberry Street was an alteration project — the ground floor slab elevations were existing, not created by the new work. The existing floor elevation at the overhead door generated the top of this slope. The slope does not extend to the curb.

The approved BPP shows sidewalk elevations at the north and south property line that match the elevations shown on the Final Survey. The south neighboring sidewalk starts from their sidewalk hatch and extends into the subject property line, which is why the new sidewalk does not extend to the property line but stops short.

## DOB Response

First review (Kalyan K. Bhattacharyya, May 31, 2007): DENIED. "The pavement needs to have to be fixed to have permissible slope." Subsequent review notes: "OK to accept. To revision for transition." The DOB required changes to create a compliant transition even though the building's floor elevation was pre-existing.

---

## Practical Impact

**The "existing conditions" argument has limits for sidewalk slope.** Even on alteration projects where the floor slab elevation is pre-existing, the DOB will still require sidewalk transitions to meet slope standards where feasible. An 11% slope within 1 foot of the building was considered excessive.

**This recon was partially unsuccessful** — the initial argument was denied, and the DOB required revisions. This makes it a useful teaching example: document pre-existing conditions thoroughly (reference approved BPP, Final Survey), but be prepared to propose a transition solution rather than simply arguing that existing conditions should be accepted as-is.

**Adjacent property encroachments complicate sidewalk work.** The neighboring sidewalk hatch extending into the property line limited what could be done. Document these conditions early in the project.

**When scoping alteration projects with BPP requirements,** flag potential sidewalk slope issues during site inspection. If the existing floor elevation creates a steep transition to the sidewalk, budget for potential recon work or plan the transition design upfront to avoid objections.

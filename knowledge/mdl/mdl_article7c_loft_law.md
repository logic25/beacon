# MDL Article 7-C - Loft Law (§§280-287)

## Overview

MDL Article 7-C, known as the "Loft Law," provides a legalization pathway for buildings that were already occupied residentially without the benefit of permits or DOB approval. Unlike Article 7-B (which covers buildings with city-approved conversions), Article 7-C applies to buildings that existed in a state of non-compliance but housed residential occupants prior to certain deadlines. This article establishes a framework for recognizing residential occupancy rights and requiring building owners to bring structures into compliance with residential code requirements.

---

## §280: Legislative Findings and Purpose

**Purpose of the Loft Law**

Section 280 establishes the legislative intent behind Article 7-C:

- Recognizes the existence of buildings that have been converted to residential use without formal approval or permits
- Acknowledges that many such buildings house residential occupants who deserve legal protection and safe housing conditions
- Establishes that these buildings represent a significant housing resource in NYC
- Provides a framework for legalization that protects tenants' residential rights while requiring code compliance
- Aims to resolve the legal uncertainty surrounding buildings used residentially without proper conversion authorization

The Loft Law represents a pragmatic legislative approach to address the gap between actual residential occupancy and legal permitting status.

## §281: Definitions

### Key Definitions

**Interim Multiple Dwelling (IMD)**

An "interim multiple dwelling" is a building or structure that:
- Contains three or more residential units (dwelling units)
- Was occupied residentially on June 21, 1982, OR at any point between June 21, 1982, and the applicable deadline
- Was NOT previously approved by DOB as a multiple dwelling or residential conversion
- Does not have a Certificate of Occupancy for residential use
- May have been in commercial, manufacturing, or other non-residential use when originally constructed
- Contains units occupied by "qualified occupants" (defined below)

**Covered Buildings**

Buildings eligible for IMD designation under Article 7-C must meet specific criteria regarding:
- Date of occupancy (on or before June 21, 1982, or within extension periods)
- Number of residential units (three or more)
- Lack of prior residential approval
- Occupancy by qualified individuals

**Qualified Occupants**

A "qualified occupant" is a person who:
- Occupied a residential unit in the building on June 21, 1982, OR
- Occupied a residential unit prior to the applicable deadline specified by the Loft Board
- Can demonstrate lawful possession or residence in the unit
- May include original occupants, successors, and individuals with tenant rights

Qualified occupant status is crucial for establishing eligibility for protective rights under Article 7-C.

**Primary Residence/Principal Residence**

Units in IMDs must be occupied as the primary residence of the occupant, distinguishing them from investment properties or secondary residences.

## §282: Eligibility and Coverage

### Determination of IMD Status

**Eligibility Criteria**

A building qualifies as an Interim Multiple Dwelling under Article 7-C if it meets ALL of the following:

1. **Residential Occupancy**: Contained three or more residential units occupied as dwelling units
2. **Timing Requirement**: Was occupied residentially on or before June 21, 1982
3. **No Prior Approval**: Was not previously granted a Certificate of Occupancy for multiple dwelling or residential use
4. **Qualified Occupants**: Contains at least one unit occupied by a qualified occupant

### Timeline Requirements and Deadlines

**Original June 21, 1982 Deadline**

- June 21, 1982 serves as the pivotal date for establishing qualified occupant status
- Occupants in residence on this date are presumptively protected under the Loft Law
- Buildings must have been in residential use on or before this date

**Extension Deadlines**

The Loft Law has been extended multiple times to expand coverage:

- **1989 Extension**: Extended protection to buildings occupied between June 21, 1982, and October 1, 1989
- **2003 Extension**: Further extended deadlines for establishing qualified occupant status
- **2010 Extension**: Continued extensions through 2010
- **2019 Extension**: Most recent extension through December 31, 2019

Each extension broadened the pool of potential IMDs by allowing qualified occupant status established within the new timeframes.

**Loft Board Determination**

- The NYC Loft Board has authority to determine whether a building qualifies as an IMD
- Buildings apply to the Loft Board for recognition of IMD status
- Proof of residential occupancy (rent receipts, utility bills, witness testimony) is required
- The Loft Board maintains registries of recognized IMD buildings

## §283: Rights of Residential Occupants in IMDs

### Tenant Protections

**Legal Recognition of Occupancy**

Once a building is designated as an IMD:
- Qualified occupants obtain legal recognition as residential tenants
- Prior occupancy without a permit does not invalidate tenancy rights
- Occupants gain protection from arbitrary eviction
- Lease agreements become subject to rent stabilization

**Lease Requirements**

- Owners must provide written leases to all occupants of IMDs
- Leases must comply with rent stabilization requirements
- Lease terms and conditions are subject to Loft Board oversight
- Occupants cannot be required to waive Article 7-C protections

**Right to Remain**

- Qualified occupants have the right to remain in their units
- Owners cannot evict based on the building's previous lack of compliance
- Occupants cannot be penalized for residing in an IMD without prior approval
- Succession rights may be established in accordance with rent stabilization law

### Owner Obligations to Tenants

**Code Compliance Timeline**

- Owners must bring the building into full residential code compliance
- Specific timeline requirements are outlined in §284
- During the compliance period, occupants have the right to remain
- Temporary or partial remedies do not satisfy the owner's obligation

**Habitability Standards**

- The building must meet minimum habitability standards throughout the compliance period
- Heat, hot water, electricity, and plumbing must function adequately
- Essential services cannot be deliberately cut off
- Violations of habitability standards may reduce rent obligations

## §284: Code Compliance and Owner Obligations

### Requirement for Code Compliance

**Mandatory Bringing into Compliance**

Section 284 imposes an affirmative duty on building owners to:
- Bring the entire building into compliance with applicable provisions of the Housing Maintenance Code
- Comply with all applicable sections of the MDL and Building Code
- Complete all necessary alterations, modifications, and improvements
- Address structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing deficiencies
- Ensure compliance with fire safety requirements

**Scope of Compliance**

Code compliance must extend to:
- All common areas (hallways, stairwells, roofs, yards)
- All residential units
- All building systems (electrical, plumbing, HVAC)
- Fire safety systems and equipment
- Structural integrity of the building

### Timeline for Compliance

**Two-Year Primary Period**

- Owners have two years from the date the Loft Board recognizes the building as an IMD to achieve full code compliance
- This represents the initial compliance deadline
- Construction and remedial work must be completed within this period

**Extensions and Modifications**

- The Loft Board may grant extensions beyond the two-year period for good cause
- Extensions typically require demonstration of financial hardship or extraordinary circumstances
- Owners must file for extensions before the initial deadline expires
- Extensions are not automatic and require Loft Board approval

**Phased Compliance**

- In some cases, the Loft Board may authorize phased compliance for particularly complex or costly repairs
- Phased approaches must still result in full compliance within a specified extended period
- Each phase must address critical habitability issues as a priority

### Enforcement and Violations

**Inspection and Certification**

- DOB and the Loft Board monitor compliance progress
- Owners may be required to submit plans and certifications of compliance
- Inspections verify that work has been completed to code standards
- Non-compliance may result in violations and penalties

**Consequences of Non-Compliance**

- Owners who fail to comply may face substantial fines and penalties
- The Loft Board may impose restrictions on owner activities in the building
- Tenants may pursue remedies for code violations
- Non-compliance can result in denial of future applications or certifications

## §284-a: Narrative Statement of Compliance

### Required Documentation

**Narrative Compliance Plan**

Section 284-a requires building owners to file a "narrative statement of compliance" (also called a "compliance plan" or "narrative plan") that includes:

1. **Description of Building**: Detailed information about the building's current condition, systems, and structure
2. **Code Analysis**: Identification of all code violations and deficiencies
3. **Remedial Work Plan**: Specific proposed work to bring the building into compliance
4. **Timeline**: Detailed schedule for completion of all work
5. **Budget and Resources**: Estimated costs and identification of funding sources
6. **Phasing (if applicable)**: If compliance is to occur in phases, detailed description of each phase and its sequence

### Submission and Approval

**Filing Requirements**

- The narrative statement must be filed with the Loft Board
- It must be certified by a licensed architect or engineer
- It must comply with Loft Board standards and forms
- Incomplete submissions may be rejected for filing

**Loft Board Review**

- The Loft Board reviews narrative statements for adequacy and feasibility
- The Board may require modifications or additional information
- Approval of a narrative statement does not guarantee that the work will be adequate; it must still meet code requirements
- The Board may revise or condition its approval

### Binding Effect

- Once approved, the narrative statement becomes a binding obligation on the owner
- Work must proceed substantially as described in the approved plan
- Changes to the plan require Loft Board approval
- Failure to follow the approved plan constitutes a violation

## §285: Rent Regulation

### Applicability of Rent Stabilization

**Mandatory Rent Stabilization Coverage**

All residential units in recognized IMDs are subject to rent stabilization under Article 26 of the NY General Business Law:

- Units are protected as "covered housing accommodations"
- Rent increases are limited to percentages determined by the Rent Guidelines Board
- Leases must comply with all rent stabilization requirements
- Tenants are protected from preferential rent schemes that exceed RGB allowances

**Rent Determinations**

- Initial rents in IMDs may be subject to special rules
- The Loft Board may establish stabilized rent amounts for units
- Owners cannot charge preferential rents that exceed RGB-approved increases
- Succession rents for new occupants must comply with rent stabilization provisions

### Lease Protections

**Lease Terms**

- All leases in IMDs must be in writing
- Leases must comply with rent stabilization requirements
- Lease terms cannot waive Article 7-C protections
- Renewal leases must comply with RGB guidelines

**Prohibited Provisions**

- Leases cannot condition housing on waiver of code compliance rights
- Leases cannot impose unreasonable restrictions on occupant rights
- Leases cannot contain terms that effectively evade rent stabilization
- Lease provisions that violate Article 7-C are void and unenforceable

## §286: Loft Board Jurisdiction and Procedures

### Creation and Authority

**NYC Loft Board**

- Established as an administrative body with specific jurisdiction over IMD matters
- Maintains office and staff to process applications and conduct hearings
- Issues determinations, permits, and variances under Article 7-C
- Registers recognized IMD buildings and maintains databases

### Powers and Duties

**Designation Authority**

The Loft Board has power to:
- Determine whether buildings qualify as IMDs
- Recognize and register qualifying buildings
- Maintain list of recognized IMDs and qualified occupants
- Establish filing procedures and requirements

**Compliance Oversight**

- Receives and approves narrative statements of compliance
- Issues certificates upon achievement of code compliance
- Monitors compliance progress
- May grant extensions or modifications to compliance schedules

**Tenant Protection Authority**

- Hears disputes concerning qualified occupant status
- Resolves tenant-owner disagreements regarding lease terms
- Determines rent amounts when parties cannot agree
- Ensures compliance with rent stabilization requirements

**Regulatory Authority**

- Issues and enforces rules and regulations implementing Article 7-C
- May establish standards for building inspections and certification
- May require submission of documents and information from owners and occupants
- Issues decisions that are subject to judicial review

### Procedures

**Application Process**

- Building owners apply to the Loft Board for IMD recognition
- Applications must include evidence of residential occupancy and qualified occupants
- The Board reviews applications and may conduct hearings
- Approval results in official recognition and registration

**Hearings and Determinations**

- The Loft Board conducts hearings on disputed issues
- Both owners and tenants have opportunity to present evidence
- Determinations are made in writing with findings of fact and conclusions of law
- Determinations may be appealed to a court of competent jurisdiction

**Certificate of Compliance**

- Upon completion of code compliance work, owners may request a Certificate of Compliance
- The Loft Board or DOB verifies that work meets code requirements
- Issuance of the certificate confirms that the building meets residential standards
- The certificate may be required for future financing or sales

## §287: Relation to Other Laws

### Applicability of Other Statutes and Regulations

**MDL and Building Code**

- All applicable provisions of the MDL apply to IMDs except where specifically modified by Article 7-C
- All applicable provisions of the NYC Building Code apply to IMDs
- Housing Maintenance Code requirements are mandatory for IMDs
- Fire Code provisions apply to IMD buildings

**Rent Stabilization Law**

- Article 26 of the General Business Law (rent stabilization) applies fully to all IMD units
- RGB guidelines control rent increases
- Succession and renewal provisions apply as in other stabilized housing
- Preferential rent schemes are subject to RGB approval

**Tenant Rights Laws**

- Tenant rights under NY Real Property Law apply to IMD occupants
- Eviction procedures must comply with RPAPL requirements
- Habitability standards under common law apply
- Protection against illegal lockouts and other self-help remedies apply

**HDC and Other Programs**

- IMD buildings may be eligible for various housing finance or rehabilitation programs
- NYC Housing Development Corporation (HDC) may provide financing
- Tax abatement programs may apply if approved
- Other programs depend on specific eligibility criteria

**Distinction from Article 7-B**

- Article 7-B covers buildings converted WITH city approval and permits
- Article 7-C covers buildings already occupied residentially WITHOUT permits
- Both articles provide similar protections but follow different legal pathways
- Buildings cannot be simultaneously under both articles
- The choice of article depends on the building's history and the path selected for legalization

---

## Key Distinctions: Article 7-C vs. Article 7-B

| Aspect | Article 7-C (Loft Law) | Article 7-B (Conversion Law) |
|--------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Original Use** | Non-residential (commercial, manufacturing) | Any prior use |
| **Legal Status** | Building already occupied residentially without approval | Building converted with DOB approval and permits |
| **Occupancy Date** | On or before June 21, 1982 (or extension dates) | After proper application and approval |
| **Legalization Path** | Recognition of existing occupancy; retrofit to code | Planned conversion with approved plans |
| **Tenant Protections** | Immediate recognition; rent stabilization applies | Lease negotiation under rent stabilization |
| **Compliance Timeline** | Two years from IMD recognition | Per approved plans at time of conversion |
| **Owner Obligations** | Retrofit entire building to residential code | Meet conversion standards from approval |

## Summary

MDL Article 7-C provides essential legal protection for occupants in buildings that developed residential use without formal approval. The "Loft Law" recognizes the reality of NYC's loft conversions and establishes a legalization pathway that:

1. Protects qualified occupants who occupied units before specified deadlines
2. Requires owners to bring buildings into full code compliance
3. Ensures all units are subject to rent stabilization protections
4. Vests jurisdiction in the NYC Loft Board for administration and enforcement
5. Provides remedies and enforcement mechanisms for violations

For building owners, Article 7-C creates significant obligations to remediate code deficiencies. For tenants, it provides statutory protection and lease security in buildings that might otherwise lack legal standing. The two-year compliance timeline creates an important deadline for owners, with potential extensions available from the Loft Board.

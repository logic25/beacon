# MDL Article 3 - Light, Ventilation, Sanitation, and Fire Protection

## Overview

MDL Article 3 establishes comprehensive requirements for light, ventilation, sanitation, and fire protection in multiple dwelling buildings. These provisions apply to most multiple dwelling conversions, particularly those under Article 7-B. Many sections of Article 3 are specifically referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions, as noted throughout this document. These requirements ensure that residential buildings maintain minimum health and safety standards for all occupants.

---

## §28: Two or More Buildings on Same Lot

### Multiple Buildings on Single Lot

**Applicability**

Section 28 addresses situations where two or more building structures exist on the same lot or parcel of land:

- If two or more buildings occupy the same tax lot or lot line
- Buildings may share access ways, yards, or other common areas
- Requirements apply to both buildings and their shared resources

**Yard and Court Requirements**

When multiple buildings share the same lot:
- Each building must have access to open yard or court space
- Shared yards must be adequately sized for ventilation and light
- Space cannot be counted toward yard requirements for more than one building
- Access to yards must be unobstructed and adequate for emergency egress

**Application to Article 7-B**

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Conversion buildings sharing lots with other structures must comply with multi-building yard requirements
- Shared facilities must be maintained adequately
- Dedicated access and use rights must be established

**Practical Implications**

- Lot splits or recombinations may affect compliance
- Easements and rights-of-way may be necessary for adequate yard access
- Common areas used by multiple buildings must meet minimum standards
- Disputes over shared facilities must be resolved to ensure compliance

## §29: Painting of Courts and Shafts

### Paint Requirements for Interior Spaces

**Purpose**

Section 29 requires painting of courts (interior courtyards) and shafts to:
- Maximize light reflection in naturally lit interior spaces
- Maintain sanitary conditions
- Prevent deterioration of building materials
- Reduce pest harborage and accumulated grime

**Painting Standards**

**Courts (Interior Courtyards)**

- All surfaces of courts must be painted with light-colored paint
- Paint should be white or light neutral color to reflect light and heat
- Surfaces include floor, walls, and enclosing building surfaces
- Paint must be kept clean and in good repair

**Shafts (Vertical Spaces)**

- Mechanical shafts, vent shafts, and stairwells must be painted
- Light coloring improves safety in these spaces by enhancing visibility
- Paint reduces fire hazard by covering flammable materials
- Paint must be non-flammable and meet fire code standards

**Materials and Finish**

- Paint must be washable and durable
- Quality of paint must be appropriate for the location (interior vs. exterior exposure)
- Application must be thorough with adequate coverage
- Paint must not obscure ventilation openings or mechanical systems

### Maintenance Obligations

**Owner Responsibility**

- Building owners must maintain painted surfaces in good condition
- Repainting is required when paint deteriorates or becomes discolored
- Peeling or flaking paint must be removed and repainted
- Maintenance schedule should include periodic inspection and touch-up

**Application to Article 7-B**

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Conversion buildings must paint all courts and shafts prior to C of O issuance
- Existing deteriorated paint in courts must be remediated
- New conversions should include painting as part of alteration work
- Ongoing maintenance obligations continue after C of O issuance

## §31: Size of Rooms

### Minimum Room Dimensions

**General Application**

Section 31 establishes minimum room sizes for multiple dwelling units to ensure livability and adequate space:

- Applies to bedrooms, living rooms, and other habitable spaces
- Minimum floor area requirements vary by room type
- Room dimensions affect the number of occupants permitted
- Combined room areas may count toward minimum requirements in certain configurations

**Room Area Requirements**

**Living Rooms / Primary Habitable Rooms**

- Minimum floor area of 150 square feet for living rooms or principal habitable rooms
- Any room smaller than 150 square feet cannot be counted as the principal room
- Must be at least 10 feet in one horizontal dimension
- Area is measured wall-to-wall, excluding closets and storage

**Bedrooms**

- Minimum floor area of 100 square feet for a single-occupancy bedroom
- Minimum floor area of 150 square feet for a double-occupancy bedroom
- At least one dimension must be at least 8 feet
- Room area is measured between wall surfaces, excluding closets

**Dining Rooms / Cooking Areas**

- If separate dining room, minimum 80 square feet
- Kitchen or kitchenette areas follow separate requirements
- Open-concept layouts may combine living and dining if total meets requirements

**Ventilation in Rooms**

- Habitable rooms must have windows or mechanical ventilation meeting §36 and §37 requirements
- Room size does not eliminate ventilation obligations
- Artificial light and ventilation can supplement but not replace windows

### Special Provisions for Article 7-B

**Limited Application to Conversions**

Per §278 of MDL, **only Subdivision Six of §31 applies to Article 7-B conversions**, not the full section:

- Subdivision Six addresses specific room configuration issues
- Full compliance with all §31 dimensions may not be required
- Conversions may be granted variance for existing room configurations if they substantially comply
- This limitation reflects the practical constraints of converting existing structures

**Variance and Compliance Paths**

- Building owners may seek variance from DOB for rooms that don't meet full §31 standards
- Evidence of existing construction and conversion constraints is relevant
- Partial compliance may be acceptable if it addresses health and safety concerns
- Mechanical ventilation can compensate for room size limitations in some cases

**Practical Considerations**

- Existing room divisions in converted buildings often cannot be changed
- Market-rate or stabilized units may have different size requirements
- Layout planning must account for these limitations
- Documentation of pre-conversion room dimensions may support variance applications

## §37: Artificial Hall Lighting

### Lighting in Common Areas

**Purpose and Scope**

Section 37 requires artificial lighting in hallways and common areas to ensure:
- Safe passage through common corridors
- Prevention of accidents and injuries
- Adequate visibility for emergency egress
- Functional lighting for daily use

**Application to Hallways**

**Corridor and Hallway Lighting**

- All interior hallways and corridors must have permanent artificial lighting
- Lighting must be sufficient to read a newspaper at normal distance
- Minimum illumination of 10 foot-candles is typical for corridors
- Light fixtures must be adequately spaced to prevent dark areas

**Stairwell Lighting**

- Stairways must have artificial lighting on each flight
- Lighting must illuminate all steps clearly
- Emergency lighting may be required in addition to regular lighting
- Light switches should be located at landings for safe operation

**Entryways and Lobbies**

- Building entrances must be adequately lit
- Common entrances and entry hallways require artificial lighting
- Night-time visibility must be adequate for tenant security
- Lighting for outdoor entry areas (stoops, porches) if applicable

**Basement and Common Areas**

- Basements, cellars, and mechanical rooms require lighting if regularly accessed
- Common laundry areas, storage areas, and utility spaces need adequate illumination
- Lighting in these areas supports safety and fire safety egress requirements

### Technical Requirements

**Quality of Lighting**

- Lighting fixtures must be permanent installations, not temporary
- Wiring must be properly installed to electrical code standards
- Fixtures should be energy-efficient (LED or equivalent) where feasible
- Lighting must not create glare or safety hazards

**Maintenance**

- Fixtures must be kept in working order
- Bulbs must be replaced promptly when burned out
- Cleaning of fixtures maintains light output
- Owners are responsible for ongoing maintenance

**Switch Locations**

- Light switches must be conveniently located
- Multiple switches may be required for long corridors
- Switches should be accessible and clearly marked
- Emergency conditions should not prevent access to light switches

### Application to Article 7-B

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Existing hallways in converted buildings must have artificial lighting installed if not already present
- Lighting must meet minimum illumination standards
- This is a mandatory requirement prior to C of O issuance
- Upgrades to existing lighting may be necessary to meet current standards
- Emergency lighting codes may impose additional requirements

## §53: Fire-Escapes

### External Emergency Egress

**Purpose and Requirements**

Section 53 addresses fire-escapes and external emergency egress systems:

- Fire-escapes provide emergency evacuation routes from upper floors
- Required in buildings where internal stairs are insufficient for egress
- Must be independently accessible and safely constructed
- Separate detailed requirements apply (cross-reference separate MDL §53 file)

### For Article 7-B Conversions

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Buildings without adequate internal stairway egress require fire-escapes
- Existing fire-escapes must be inspected and certified for safety
- Fire-escape design must comply with current standards
- Installation costs are typically owner responsibility
- Safety inspection certificates are required

**Cross-Reference**: See separate comprehensive file on MDL §53 (Fire-Escapes) for detailed requirements, construction standards, maintenance obligations, and inspection protocols.

## §55: Wainscoting

### Wall Coverings and Finish Materials

**Purpose**

Section 55 addresses wainscoting (protective wall coverings) to:
- Protect walls from damage in high-traffic or high-wear areas
- Facilitate cleaning and sanitation
- Prevent moisture infiltration and mold
- Improve durability of wall finishes

**Wainscoting Requirements**

**Material Standards**

- Wainscoting must be non-porous, washable material
- Acceptable materials include ceramic tile, glazed brick, painted plaster, or comparable finishes
- Materials must be durable and suitable for the location
- Wainscoting must be properly sealed and waterproofed

**Application Areas**

- Required in kitchens around cooking areas
- Required in bathrooms above fixtures
- Required in laundry areas subject to moisture
- May be required in other areas prone to splashing or heavy use

**Height Requirements**

- Wainscoting typically extends 4-5 feet up from floor in kitchens and bathrooms
- Height may vary depending on room use and splash potential
- Upper wall surface must be finished appropriately
- Transitions must be properly finished

### Special Provision for Article 7-B

**Limited Application**

Per §278 of MDL, **only Subdivision Two of §55 applies to Article 7-B conversions**, not the full section:

- Subdivision Two addresses specific wainscoting configurations in conversions
- Full wainscoting may not be required throughout building
- Practical conversion considerations are accommodated
- Focus may be on functional areas (kitchens, bathrooms) rather than all common areas

**Compliance Flexibility**

- DOB may grant variance for existing finishes if they substantially meet intent
- High-wear areas must have protective finishes regardless
- Existing paint or finishes may be acceptable if properly maintained
- Upgrades during conversion should address deteriorated or inadequate finishes

## §56: Frame Buildings and Extensions

### Structural Requirements for Wood-Frame Buildings

**Purpose and Scope**

Section 56 addresses requirements for wood-frame buildings and frame extensions to:
- Ensure structural safety and stability
- Reduce fire risk in wood construction
- Establish standards for additions and alterations
- Protect occupant safety

**Fire Protection in Frame Buildings**

**Non-Combustible Coverage**

- Wood frame buildings must have non-combustible exterior coverings
- Interior structural elements may be wood but must be properly protected
- Walls must have fire-rated assemblies where required
- Exposure to adjacent properties affects requirements

**Roof Construction**

- Roof structures must be appropriately fire-rated
- Combustible roofing materials may be restricted
- Roof openings and penetrations must be properly protected
- Access to roof must not compromise fire safety

**Structural Requirements**

**Foundation and Support**

- Frame buildings must have adequate foundations
- Floor systems must be properly supported
- Connections and joinery must be secure
- Sagging or deteriorated structural elements must be repaired

**Building Extensions**

- Additions or extensions must match structural standards of main building
- Extensions must be properly connected to existing structure
- Materials and methods must be consistent with main building
- Building code standards apply to new construction portions

### Application to Article 7-B

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Existing frame buildings must be evaluated for structural adequacy
- Fire-rating requirements may necessitate upgrades
- Extensions or alterations must comply with current standards
- Inspection and certification of structural elements may be required
- Deteriorated frame elements must be replaced or reinforced

## §57: Bells; Mail Receptacles

### Building Access and Communication Systems

**Purpose**

Section 57 addresses bell systems and mail receptacles:
- Enable communication between visitors and residents
- Facilitate secure mail delivery
- Support security and emergency communication

**Bell Systems**

**Entrance Bell Requirements**

- Building entrances must have bell system or intercom
- System must allow visitors to communicate with residents
- All units must have accessible bell or intercom connection
- System must function reliably and be properly maintained

**Bell Placement and Accessibility**

- Bell buttons must be located at main entrance(s)
- Buttons must be clearly labeled with unit numbers or tenant names
- Placement must accommodate persons with disabilities
- Buttons must be illuminated or visible at night

**Intercom Systems**

- Electronic intercom systems may substitute for mechanical bells
- Intercom must provide two-way communication
- System must be maintained in working order
- Backup power may be required for emergency communication

**Mail Receptacles**

**Location Requirements**

- Mail receptacles (mailboxes) must be provided for all units
- Location should be readily accessible to residents
- Placement should facilitate mail carrier delivery
- Outdoor locations must be protected from weather

**Design Standards**

- Mailboxes must be clearly marked with unit numbers
- Construction must protect mail from weather damage
- Boxes must be secure against unauthorized access
- Multiple boxes may be grouped in a central location (mail wall)

**Maintenance**

- Owners must maintain mail receptacles in good condition
- Damaged or broken mailboxes must be repaired or replaced
- Labels must remain legible
- System must be kept clean and functional

### Application to Article 7-B

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Existing bell systems must be evaluated and upgraded if non-functional
- New intercom systems may be required for security
- Mail delivery systems must be established prior to occupancy
- Central mail walls may be preferable to individual exterior boxes
- Systems must be accessible to all residents and mail carriers

## §58: Incombustible Materials

### Fire-Resistant Construction Requirements

**Purpose and Scope**

Section 58 establishes requirements for incombustible and fire-resistant materials in multiple dwelling buildings:
- Reduce fire spread and potential for catastrophic fires
- Protect occupants by limiting available combustibles
- Establish minimum standards for materials and finishes
- Apply throughout the building, in common areas and units

**Incombustible Material Standards**

**Definition and Testing**

- "Incombustible" materials are those that do not burn or contribute to fire spread
- Testing per ASTM or equivalent standards determines classification
- Fire-rated assemblies must meet UL or FM standards
- Composite materials are evaluated for their flame spread rating

**Acceptable Materials**

- Steel and steel structures
- Masonry and concrete
- Glass and mineral wool
- Non-combustible paints and coatings
- Gypsum board and similar fire-rated products
- Insulation materials with low flame spread ratings

**Prohibited or Restricted Materials**

- Untreated wood in certain locations
- Foam insulation without proper fire rating
- Flammable paints or finishes in common areas
- Vinyl or plastic coverings without fire rating in certain locations

**Application by Building Area**

**Structural Elements**

- Primary structural members should be non-combustible where required by code
- Fire-walls must be of incombustible construction
- Roof framing and floor joists must meet fire-rating requirements
- Connections and fasteners must not compromise fire ratings

**Interior Finishes**

- Walls in common areas must have low flame-spread finishes
- Ceiling finishes in corridors and stairs must be fire-rated
- Flooring in common areas should have low flame-spread rating
- Paint and coatings must not be excessively flammable

**Unit Interiors**

- Finishes in individual units have somewhat more flexibility
- Kitchens have stricter requirements due to fire hazard
- Bedrooms and living areas should use fire-rated or non-combustible materials where practical
- Adhesives and installation materials must not be excessively flammable

### Application to Article 7-B

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Existing buildings must be evaluated for non-compliant materials
- Flammable interior finishes must be addressed during conversion
- Structural fire ratings must be assessed and certified
- Non-compliant insulation must be replaced with fire-rated alternatives
- Paint and finishes throughout must meet fire code standards
- Documentation of material compliance is required for C of O

## §59: Bakeries and Fat Boiling

### Commercial Uses Involving Heat and Fire Risk

**Purpose**

Section 59 addresses bakeries and commercial cooking operations within or attached to multiple dwelling buildings:
- Commercial use of heat and cooking equipment creates additional fire risk
- Fat and cooking vapors present fire hazards
- Ventilation and separation requirements protect residential occupants

**Bakery Requirements**

**Separation and Ventilation**

- Bakeries must be separated from residential areas by fire-rated walls
- Ventilation must exhaust cooking vapors and heat outside the building
- Hood systems must be properly installed and regularly cleaned
- Grease traps must be provided and regularly maintained

**Equipment and Installation**

- Commercial ovens and equipment must be properly installed
- Equipment must be listed and labeled for commercial use
- Clearances from combustibles must meet code requirements
- Electrical systems must be rated for commercial equipment

**Fat Boiling and Cooking Operations**

**Temperature Control**

- Heating of cooking fat must be controlled to prevent ignition
- Equipment must have temperature controls and safety devices
- Ventilation must capture cooking vapors and grease particles
- Hood filters must be regularly cleaned or replaced

**Fire Suppression**

- Cooking areas may require specialized fire suppression (wet chemical systems)
- Grease fires require specific extinguishing agents
- Building fire alarm and sprinkler systems must be compatible
- Staff must be trained in proper use of suppression systems

### Application to Article 7-B

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Bakeries or commercial cooking within conversion buildings require special approval
- Existing bakeries or cooking operations must meet separation and ventilation standards
- Conversion plans must address any existing commercial food operations
- Fire suppression and ventilation systems may require significant investment
- Separation walls between commercial and residential areas must be fire-rated
- Ongoing inspections and maintenance are required for compliance

## §60: Motor Vehicle Storage

### Parking and Vehicle Storage in Multiple Dwellings

**Purpose and Scope**

Section 60 addresses parking garages, vehicle storage areas, and automobile-related facilities within or beneath multiple dwelling buildings:
- Vehicles and fuel present fire and explosion hazards
- Exhaust and emissions pose health concerns
- Separation and ventilation protect residential occupants

**Parking Garage Requirements**

**Separation from Residential Areas**

- Parking garages must be separated from residential areas by fire-rated floors/walls
- Walls must extend to underside of roof or next floor above
- Openings (stairs, elevators) must have fire-rated doors and smoke seals
- Separation must prevent smoke and fumes from entering residential areas

**Ventilation Standards**

- Continuous mechanical ventilation is required for enclosed parking
- Exhaust must be directly vented to exterior (not into residential areas)
- Ventilation fans must run continuously during occupancy and periodically otherwise
- Carbon monoxide levels must be monitored and controlled

**Fire Safety**

- Fire extinguishers must be provided and clearly marked
- Sprinkler protection is required for parking areas in some cases
- Fire detection systems must be installed
- Clear floor markings and directional signage required

**Fuel Storage**

- Gasoline and propane storage is generally prohibited in buildings with residential occupancy
- Portable fuel containers must not be stored in residential buildings
- Propane systems must meet specific requirements if permitted
- Emergency procedures must be established

### Application to Article 7-B

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Existing parking areas must comply with separation and ventilation standards
- Upgrades to ventilation systems may be necessary during conversion
- Fire separation walls may require installation or repair
- If ground floor is converted to parking, residential areas above require increased protection
- Mechanical ventilation systems must be designed by licensed engineer
- Ongoing operation and maintenance of ventilation is mandatory

## §61: Business Uses

### Commercial and Non-Residential Uses in Multiple Dwellings

**Permitted and Prohibited Uses**

**General Prohibition**

- Multiple dwelling buildings are designed and zoned for residential use
- Commercial or industrial uses within residential buildings are restricted
- Certain uses are prohibited; others may be permitted with restrictions
- Mixed-use buildings require specific design and separation measures

**Permitted Business Uses**

- Professional offices (limited scope and floor area)
- Ground floor retail or commercial (subject to separation requirements)
- Management offices for building operations
- Services incidental to residential use (laundry facilities, etc.)

**Restricted Uses**

Section 61 limits the following uses based on separation and intensity:
- Retail establishments in non-basement locations
- Service businesses with limited impact
- Medical or professional offices
- Certain food service or restaurant operations

**Prohibited Uses**

Uses that create excessive hazard and are prohibited include:
- Manufacturing and industrial operations
- Hazardous material storage or handling
- High-intensity food service (see §59)
- Automotive service and repair
- Adult entertainment establishments

**Special Application to Article 7-B**

Per §278 of MDL, **paragraph (c) of Subdivision One and Subdivision Three of §61 are NOT applicable to Article 7-B conversions**:

- This exclusion provides flexibility for conversion buildings
- Existing commercial uses may be accommodated more readily
- Conversion buildings may have more flexibility for ground-floor use
- However, fire separation and safety requirements still apply

**Practical Implications**

- Ground floor commercial use in conversions may not require full §61 compliance
- Existing businesses may be permitted to continue with modifications
- Fire separation walls and ventilation are still mandatory
- Parking areas and hazardous uses remain prohibited

### Fire Separation Requirements

**Wall and Floor Construction**

- Any commercial space must be separated from residential areas by fire-rated construction
- Walls must extend from floor to structural ceiling/roof
- Doors between commercial and residential must be fire-rated and self-closing
- Air gaps and penetrations must be sealed

**Ventilation**

- Commercial spaces with cooking or chemical use require separate exhaust
- Ventilation must not introduce odors or fumes into residential areas
- HVAC systems may be separate or ducted separately
- Exhaust must be vented to exterior

## §62: Parapets, Guard Railings, and Wires

### Safety Features and Protective Barriers

**Purpose and Scope**

Section 62 addresses protective structures and safety features on building exteriors:
- Parapets prevent falls from roofs and upper floors
- Guard railings protect occupants on stairs and balconies
- Wire guards and protective barriers prevent injuries
- These requirements apply to all multiple dwelling buildings

**Parapet Requirements**

**Roof Parapets**

- All roofs must have parapets (raised walls) at edges
- Minimum height of 42 inches above roof surface (some jurisdictions require 48 inches)
- Parapets must be of solid construction with no gaps
- Parapets must be sufficiently strong to resist impact and wind loads

**Structural Requirements**

- Parapets must be properly anchored to building structure
- Materials must be durable and weather-resistant
- Connections must resist lateral loads (wind)
- Maintenance must prevent deterioration

**Guard Railing Requirements**

**Stairway Railings**

- All stairs must have railings on at least one side
- Both sides required if stairway is wider than 44 inches
- Minimum height of 34-38 inches measured from stair nosing
- Railings must be securely attached

**Balcony and Deck Railings**

- Balconies, decks, and platforms require protective railings
- Height and spacing must meet current building code standards
- Railings must prevent objects (especially children) from passing through
- Minimum 42-inch height is typical

**Railing Design Standards**

- Vertical balusters must not be spaced more than 4 inches apart
- Horizontal rails must not create climbing hazards
- Materials must be durable (wood, metal, or composite)
- Railings must withstand 200-pound horizontal force without failure

**Protective Mesh and Wire Guards**

**Window Guards**

- Window guards or protective grates may be required in certain locations
- Guards protect against falls and unauthorized exits
- Mesh opening size must restrict passage of children
- Guards must not obstruct emergency egress

**Exterior Stairs and Landings**

- External stairs and landings require protective wire or grating
- Prevents objects from falling on pedestrians below
- Mesh or grating must have appropriate opening size
- Must be maintained to prevent corrosion and deterioration

### Application to Article 7-B

Referenced in §278 as applicable to Article 7-B conversions:
- Existing roofs must be evaluated for parapet adequacy
- Parapets that are deteriorated or undersized must be repaired or replaced
- New stairs or balconies created during conversion must have compliant railings
- Existing railings must meet current spacing and height standards
- Upgrades may be required if existing conditions are non-compliant
- Documentation of compliance is required for C of O

## Summary and Relationship to Article 7-B Conversions

MDL Article 3 establishes comprehensive standards for multiple dwelling buildings covering:

**Light and Ventilation**: Windows, natural light, and mechanical ventilation systems ensure healthy indoor environments.

**Fire Safety**: Fire-escapes, fire-resistant materials, separation of hazardous uses, and protective barriers reduce fire risk and enable emergency egress.

**Sanitation and Finishes**: Wainscoting, painting, and material standards facilitate cleaning and prevent pest and mold issues.

**Structural and Safety Elements**: Room sizes, railings, parapets, and structural integrity protect occupant safety.

**Common Areas**: Bell systems, mail receptacles, artificial lighting, and hallway standards support tenant needs and building operations.

### Article 3 and Article 7-B Conversions

MDL §278 specifically identifies Article 3 sections that apply to Article 7-B conversions, with important limitations:

- **Full Sections Apply**: §28, §29, §37, §53, §56, §57, §58, §59, §60, §62 apply in their entirety to Article 7-B conversions
- **Limited Sections**: §31 (only Subdivision Six), §55 (only Subdivision Two), §61 (excluding certain paragraphs)
- **Practical Effect**: Article 7-B conversions must substantially comply with Article 3 standards, with flexibility provided for structural constraints inherent in converting existing buildings

Owners undertaking Article 7-B conversions must conduct thorough audits of existing conditions against Article 3 standards and plan remedial work accordingly. The limitations noted in §278 provide some relief from perfect compliance but do not eliminate the fundamental requirements for safe, healthy residential environments.
